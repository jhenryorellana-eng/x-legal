### C. The Core of the Claim

When Mr. Mendoza Rivas sought protection from the Venezuelan state, filing a police report in 2021, police took no action and instructed him to "stop provoking." ### D. Legal Framework and Governing Standards

The legal standards governing this application are well-settled and, as applied to the facts of this record, command a grant of relief.

For asylum under INA Section 208, an applicant must demonstrate either past persecution on account of a protected ground, or a well-founded fear of future persecution on account of such a ground, where the applicant is unwilling or unable to return to, or avail himself of the protection of, his country of nationality. The Supreme Court in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), held that the "well-founded fear" standard is more generous than the clear-probability standard applicable to withholding: a well-founded fear may rest on as little as a ten-percent chance of persecution -- a "reasonable possibility" -- not a probability. That standard, always favorable to the bona fide applicant, is readily met here given the documented pattern of repeated, escalating, state-directed persecution and the verified country-conditions record confirming the systematic targeting of opposition organizers in Venezuela.

Nexus -- the requirement that persecution occur "on account of" a protected ground -- is established where evidence permits a reasonable inference that the persecutor was motivated, at least in part, by the applicant's actual or imputed political opinion. See INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992) (nexus may be established by direct or circumstantial evidence). The persecutors here did not act on an anonymous or random basis; they interrogated Mr. Mendoza Rivas specifically about party financing and fellow organizers, they threatened him with death for continuing his political activities, and they raided his home and seized party materials. The nexus to political opinion is not inferential -- it is manifest on the face of the persecutory conduct itself.

The well-founded-fear analysis is further guided by Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987), which requires the applicant to demonstrate that: (1) he possesses a belief or characteristic the persecutor seeks to overcome; (2) the persecutor is aware, or could become aware, of that characteristic; (3) the persecutor has the capability to punish; and (4) the persecutor has the inclination to do so. All four elements are established on this record.

Where past persecution is established, a regulatory presumption of a well-founded fear of future persecution arises. See 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(b)(1). The government may rebut that presumption only by demonstrating a fundamental change in circumstances or that the applicant could safely relocate internally. Neither rebuttal ground is available on these facts: country-conditions evidence confirms that conditions in Venezuela have not fundamentally improved for opposition organizers, and the post-departure visit to the applicant's cousin in Maracaibo conclusively forecloses any contention that internal relocation is a reasonable option.

For withholding of removal under INA Section 241(b)(3), the burden is higher -- the applicant must demonstrate that his life or freedom would be threatened on account of a protected ground. See 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.16(b). The pattern of two custodial detentions, armed attacks on his home, seizure of his documents and property, death threats, threats against his family, and the national reach of the persecutors more than satisfy this elevated standard.

For CAT protection, the applicant must demonstrate that it is more likely than not that he would be subjected to torture by, or with the acquiescence of, a public official if returned to Venezuela. The physical mistreatment documented by the applicant's 2022 medical note, the well-documented practice of torture in Venezuelan state detention facilities reported by Human Rights Watch and the U.S. Department of State, and Mr. Mendoza Rivas's specific profile as a previously identified and actively hunted opposition organizer collectively establish the requisite probability.

## I.3 Governing Legal Standards: Asylum

### A. The Statutory Definition of "Refugee" Under INA Section 101(a)(42)(A)

The threshold requirement for asylum eligibility is that the applicant qualify as a "refugee" within the meaning of the Immigration and Nationality Act. Section 101(a)(42)(A) of the INA, codified at 8 U.S.C. Section 1101(a)(42)(A), defines a refugee as any person who is outside his country of nationality and is unable or unwilling to return to, or to avail himself of the protection of, that country "because of persecution or a well-founded fear of persecution on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion." This definition is derived from, and must be interpreted consistently with, the United Nations Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees, to which the United States acceded in 1968, and which incorporates by reference the 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees. See INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421, 436-37 (1987). The statutory text thus embeds five analytically distinct elements: (1) a person outside his country of nationality; (2) who is unable or unwilling to return or avail himself of that country's protection; (3) because of persecution or a well-founded fear of persecution; (4) on account of one of the five enumerated protected grounds; and (5) inflicted by the government or by forces the government is unable or unwilling to control. Each of those elements must be independently established, and the governing legal standards applicable to each are addressed in turn below.

The term "persecution" is not defined in the INA, but the Board of Immigration Appeals and the circuit courts have consistently held that it encompasses a range of serious harms going beyond mere harassment, discrimination, or unpleasantness. See Sangha v. INS, 103 F.3d 1482, 1487 (9th Cir. 1997). The category of harm that rises to the level of persecution includes physical violence, detention, torture, and threats of death or grievous bodily harm, particularly where the threats are specific, credible, and directed at the individual applicant on account of a protected ground. The Board recognized in Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211, 222 (BIA 1985), that persecution may be inflicted by the government directly or by forces the government is unwilling or unable to control, and that the inquiry is whether the harm imposed or threatened is imposed on account of a characteristic the victim cannot change or should not be required to change. The adjudicating officer should evaluate not isolated incidents in isolation but the cumulative pattern of harm in its entirety. See Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646, 658 (9th Cir. 2000) (holding that the agency must consider the cumulative record rather than evaluating each incident in isolation).

The five protected grounds -- race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, and political opinion -- are statutory categories that are exhaustive rather than illustrative. An applicant whose harm does not connect to one of these five grounds is not eligible for asylum under INA Section 208, regardless of the severity of the harm suffered. See INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478, 482-83 (1992). The primary ground asserted in this application is political opinion, both actual and imputed. The standards governing that ground, and the nexus requirement that links it to the harm suffered, are addressed in detail below.

### B. The Well-Founded Fear Standard: Subjective and Objective Components

The asylum standard turns on the phrase "well-founded fear of persecution," which the Supreme Court definitively interpreted in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987). In Cardoza-Fonseca, the Court resolved a circuit split and held that the well-founded fear standard governing asylum under INA Section 208 is "more generous" than the "clear probability" standard governing withholding of removal under INA Section 243(h) (now Section 241(b)(3)). The Court explicitly embraced a formulation under which an applicant may establish a well-founded fear even where persecution is not more likely than not -- the Court noted that if an applicant has a ten percent chance of being shot, or a ten percent chance of being subjected to other serious harm, he can have a well-founded fear. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. at 431. This "reasonable possibility" standard, derived from the legislative history of the Refugee Act of 1980, requires only that the fear be genuine and that the objective circumstances lend it some reasonable support, not that the feared outcome be probable or certain.

The well-founded fear standard has two distinct and independently necessary components: a subjective component and an objective component. The subjective component requires that the applicant actually hold the fear asserted; it is assessed through the applicant's credible testimony and the internal consistency of his account. The objective component requires that the fear be grounded in objectively reasonable circumstances -- that is, that the record contain sufficient evidence to demonstrate a reasonable possibility that the applicant's fear would be realized if he were returned to his country of nationality. See Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439, 441-42 (BIA 1987).

The Board of Immigration Appeals articulated the operative framework for the objective well-founded fear analysis in Matter of Mogharrabi. The Board identified four elements that a reasonable person in the applicant's position would need to establish in order for the fear to be objectively well-founded: (1) the applicant possesses a belief or characteristic a persecutor seeks to overcome; (2) the persecutor is aware, or could become aware, that the applicant holds that belief or characteristic; (3) the persecutor has the capability to punish the applicant; and (4) the persecutor has the inclination to do so. Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. at 446. The Board's formulation has been widely adopted by the circuit courts and remains the standard framework for assessing whether a fear is objectively reasonable. Each prong of this four-part analysis should be satisfied on the basis of the evidentiary record -- not merely through the applicant's testimony, but through country-conditions evidence, corroborating witness statements, documentary evidence of prior harm, and any other credible evidence the applicant presents.

Critically, the objective prong of the well-founded fear inquiry does not require that the applicant demonstrate individualized, specific threats in every case. Where the country-conditions record establishes a systematic pattern of persecution of a class of persons sharing the applicant's characteristics, an applicant who is a member of that class may establish an objectively well-founded fear without independent evidence of a specific, individualized threat directed at him personally. See 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(b)(2)(iii) (recognizing that a well-founded fear may be established by a pattern or practice of persecution of a group of which the applicant is a member). Where, as here, the record contains both individualized harm and country-conditions evidence of systematic persecution, the objective prong is readily satisfied.

The well-founded fear standard is also forward-looking: the statute refers to a fear of persecution, which necessarily concerns what will happen upon return, not only what has already occurred. A finding that persecution has occurred in the past establishes presumptive eligibility for protection going forward, but the well-founded fear inquiry also encompasses threats and circumstances that have arisen after the applicant's departure and that bear upon the likelihood of future harm. Adjudicators are required to consider the totality of circumstances bearing upon the applicant's future risk, including any steps taken by the persecutor to locate or harm the applicant or his associates after the applicant fled.

### C. Past Persecution and the Regulatory Presumption Under 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(b)(1)

Where an applicant establishes that he has suffered past persecution within the meaning of the INA, a regulatory presumption of a well-founded fear of future persecution arises automatically and without the need for independent proof. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(b)(1) provides that an alien who has established that he suffered past persecution shall be presumed to have a well-founded fear of persecution upon return. This presumption represents a structural acknowledgment by the regulatory scheme that individuals who have already been identified by their persecutors and subjected to serious harm face an elevated and objectively reasonable risk of renewed persecution if returned.

The past persecution presumption fundamentally shifts the burden of production in these proceedings. Once the applicant establishes past persecution by a preponderance of the evidence -- or, in the credible fear context, a significant possibility of establishing eligibility -- the burden shifts to the government to rebut the presumption by establishing one of two conditions. First, the government may demonstrate a fundamental change in circumstances such that the applicant no longer has a well-founded fear of persecution. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(b)(1)(i)(A). Second, the government may establish that the applicant could reasonably relocate to another part of his country of nationality, and that it would be reasonable, under all of the circumstances, to expect him to do so. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(b)(1)(i)(B). Both rebuttal grounds impose a burden on the government; the applicant need only sit on the presumption until the government offers evidence sufficient to discharge that burden.

The fundamental change in circumstances inquiry is a demanding one. It requires a genuine, material, and durable transformation of the conditions that gave rise to the persecution -- not a superficial or temporary change in political rhetoric, not a new government that has not yet had an opportunity to act, and not the mere cessation of active hostilities. The circuit courts have consistently held that the government must point to concrete, reliable, and current evidence of a fundamental change in the specific conditions that produced the persecution. A country-conditions record that confirms the persistence of the same authoritarian apparatus, the same security forces, and the same patterns of repression that produced the prior harm provides no basis for rebuttal under this prong.

The internal relocation inquiry requires the government to demonstrate that the applicant could avoid future persecution by relocating to another part of the country, and that it would be reasonable to require him to do so. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(b)(3) sets out the factors relevant to the reasonableness inquiry, including whether the applicant would face other serious harm in the proposed relocation area, the general human rights conditions in the relocation area, the applicant's age, gender, health, and social and familial ties, and any social and cultural constraints such as language barriers. Where the persecution is conducted by the central government or by forces acting with government acquiescence on a nationwide basis, internal relocation is per se unavailable because the persecutor's reach is coextensive with the national territory. The Board of Immigration Appeals and the circuit courts have consistently held that government-sponsored persecution, by definition, forecloses safe internal relocation. See Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 227, 246 (BIA 2014) (noting the relevance of whether the persecution is nationwide in the internal relocation analysis).

The past persecution presumption does not, however, operate to confer asylum automatically. Even where past persecution is established and the government fails to rebut the presumption, an applicant may be denied asylum as a matter of discretion. The discretionary factors relevant to asylum adjudication are addressed in Matter of Pula, 19 I&N Dec. 467 (BIA 1987), which held that the adjudicator must weigh the totality of the circumstances, that manner of entry is not a dispositive factor, and that the equities of the individual case -- including the severity of the persecution, the applicant's equities in the United States, and any adverse factors -- must be balanced. On a record of repeated, state-directed harm, an applicant who has committed no adverse acts presents equities that overwhelmingly favor a favorable exercise of discretion.

### D. The Nexus Requirement: "On Account Of" and the "One Central Reason" Standard

The phrase "on account of" in INA Section 101(a)(42)(A) -- sometimes called the nexus requirement -- is one of the most extensively litigated elements of asylum law. The persecutor must have been motivated, at least in part, by the applicant's actual or imputed protected characteristic. The Supreme Court addressed the nexus requirement directly in INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), holding that the "on account of" language requires evidence of the persecutor's motivation -- that is, the persecutor must have been driven, at least in part, by the applicant's political opinion, not merely by a desire to coerce or harm in the abstract. The Court further held, however, that nexus may be demonstrated through direct or circumstantial evidence; the applicant need not produce a written statement from the persecutor declaring his motive.

The REAL ID Act of 2005, Pub. L. No. 109-13, Section 101(a)(3), 119 Stat. 302, codified at INA Section 208(b)(1)(B)(i), tightened the nexus standard by requiring that a protected ground be "one central reason" for the persecution. This "one central reason" standard replaced the prior "one reason" test and was designed to exclude claims in which the protected ground is entirely peripheral to the persecutor's motivation. The standard does not require, however, that the protected ground be the sole reason, the primary reason, or even the dominant reason for the persecution. It requires only that a protected ground be a central motivation -- not merely a pretext or an entirely incidental consideration. See Matter of S-P-, 21 I&N Dec. 486, 489-90 (BIA 1996) (holding that in mixed-motive cases the applicant must produce evidence from which it is reasonable to conclude that the harm was motivated, at least in part, by an actual or imputed protected ground). In practice, this standard is satisfied where the evidence permits a reasonable inference that the persecutor would not have targeted the applicant in the same manner absent the protected characteristic.

The "one central reason" standard presents particular analytical challenges in cases involving mixed motives -- that is, cases in which the persecutor may have had both political and non-political reasons for targeting the applicant. The circuit courts have consistently held that the presence of a non-protected motive -- such as a desire for financial gain, personal animus, or general social control -- does not defeat the nexus element so long as a protected ground was also a central motivating factor. See Sangha v. INS, 103 F.3d 1482, 1490 (9th Cir. 1997) (clarifying that the applicant must show the persecutor was motivated, at least in part, by the applicant's political opinion and distinguishing that inquiry from generalized civil strife or non-political objectives). The adjudicator must examine the totality of the evidence bearing on motivation -- including the content of any interrogations, the targets selected, the statements made by the persecutors, the timing and escalation of the harm, and the country-conditions record -- to determine whether a protected ground is reasonably inferable as a central motive.

The doctrine of imputed political opinion is a critical extension of the nexus analysis and is particularly relevant in cases involving opposition organizers and their associates in countries with authoritarian governments. Under the imputed political opinion doctrine, a persecutor who attributes a political opinion to a person -- whether or not that person actually holds that opinion -- and who acts on the basis of that attributed opinion satisfies the nexus requirement as fully as if the opinion were actually held. See Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646, 658 (9th Cir. 2000) (holding that persecution on the basis of imputed political opinion -- including opinion imputed through family or association -- compels a finding of past persecution and a well-founded fear). The imputed opinion doctrine recognizes that persecutors frequently act on the basis of perceived, rather than proven, loyalties, and that the law protects the target of that perception regardless of the target's own subjective political views.

The doctrine is especially important in two recurring factual scenarios. First, where the persecutor targets an applicant because of his known association with an opposition organization or political movement, any harm inflicted on account of that association constitutes persecution on account of imputed political opinion, whether or not the persecutor has verified the applicant's personal views. Second, where the persecutor targets the family members or associates of an identified opposition figure, those family members and associates are entitled to protection on the basis of the political opinion imputed to them by virtue of their relationship. In both scenarios, the circuit courts have consistently held that imputed political opinion satisfies the nexus requirement, and the Board of Immigration Appeals has recognized the doctrine as settled law.

### E. Political Opinion as a Protected Ground: Actual and Imputed

Political opinion is one of the five grounds enumerated in INA Section 101(a)(42)(A) and is the ground most directly implicated in cases involving targeted persecution of opposition organizers, activists, and community leaders who resist authoritarian governments. The Board of Immigration Appeals and the circuit courts have given political opinion a broad and purposive interpretation, consistent with the asylum statute's humanitarian objectives.

An applicant's political opinion need not take a formally organized or institutionalized form to qualify for protection. The expression of dissenting views, participation in opposition political activity, public criticism of government officials or policies, and grassroots organizational work have all been recognized as forms of political opinion that can ground asylum claims. See Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987). The opinion need not be extreme, radical, or associated with a recognized opposition party; it need only be discernible as a political stance that the persecutor seeks to suppress. The key inquiry is whether the persecutor perceived the applicant as holding a political view antagonistic to the persecutor's interests or ideology, and whether that perception motivated the persecution.

In the context of the Venezuelan political situation, the courts and the Board have had occasion to address the claims of individuals who were targeted for their association with, or participation in, opposition political activities. The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela, published April 22, 2024, documents a systematic pattern of persecution of political opponents, including arbitrary detention, torture, and extrajudicial violence directed at individuals who organize or participate in opposition political activity. Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024: Venezuela, published January 11, 2024, similarly documents arbitrary detention and torture of perceived government opponents by Venezuelan security forces -- including the Bolivarian National Guard, the Directorate General of Military Counterintelligence (DGCIM), and the Bolivarian Intelligence Service (SEBIN) -- and by pro-government colectivos acting in coordination with and under the acquiescence of state authorities. Amnesty International's 2024 report on Venezuela, published March 1, 2024, documents a deliberate policy of repression against dissent, including the criminalization of opposition figures, human rights defenders, and community organizers. Freedom House rates Venezuela "Not Free" in its Freedom in the World 2024 report, published February 29, 2024, characterizing the country as an authoritarian state that systematically represses opposition, controls the judiciary, and denies political rights and civil liberties. Foro Penal, the Venezuelan non-governmental organization that tracks political detention, documents hundreds of political prisoners and thousands of arbitrary detentions for political reasons since 2014, providing granular corroboration of a nationwide pattern of state-directed persecution of opposition organizers.

This country-conditions record is directly relevant to the legal standard because it establishes the context within which the applicant's individual experiences must be assessed. It corroborates the objective reasonableness of the applicant's fear, rebuts any suggestion that the harm he experienced was random or unrelated to his political activities, and confirms the absence of meaningful government protection or an independent judiciary capable of providing redress.

### F. The Particular Social Group Ground as an Alternative Basis

To the extent that the adjudicating officer concludes that the primary political opinion ground requires additional development -- or in the alternative -- the applicant's status as an identified, targeted, and systematically pursued member of the Venezuelan democratic opposition community satisfies the requirements for protection on the basis of membership in a particular social group. The controlling three-part test was articulated by the Board in Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 227 (BIA 2014), which requires that a proposed particular social group be (1) composed of members sharing a common immutable characteristic, (2) defined with particularity, and (3) socially distinct within the society in question.

The foundational immutability standard was established by the Board in Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), which held that a particular social group is defined by a common, immutable characteristic -- that is, a characteristic its members either cannot change or should not be required to change because it is fundamental to their individual identity or conscience. Participation in organized democratic opposition activity, once it has been publicly identified and attributed to a specific individual by the persecutor, takes on a quality of immutability in the relevant sense: the persecutor's attribution of that characteristic does not depend on whether the applicant renounces his beliefs, because the record of participation is known, documented in the persecutor's files, and cannot be erased. The social distinction and particularity requirements of M-E-V-G- are satisfied by reference to the country-conditions record, which confirms that identified opposition organizers in Venezuela are recognized as a discrete and targeted class by Venezuelan society, the Venezuelan government, and the international human rights community alike. Full elaboration of the particular social group analysis appears in Section III of this memorandum; it is noted here for doctrinal completeness.

### G. The One-Year Filing Deadline and the Statutory Exceptions

Section 208(a)(2)(B) of the INA requires that an asylum application be filed within one year of the applicant's arrival in the United States. The one-year filing deadline is a jurisdictional threshold that must be satisfied before the adjudicator can reach the merits of the claim. An applicant who fails to file within one year of arrival is ineligible for asylum unless he can demonstrate the applicability of one of two statutory exceptions: (1) changed circumstances that materially affect the applicant's eligibility for asylum, or (2) extraordinary circumstances relating to the delay in filing. INA Section 208(a)(2)(D); 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.4(a)(4)-(5).

Changed circumstances include changes in applicable law or in the conditions in the applicant's country of nationality; they must be circumstances that materially affect the applicant's eligibility for asylum, not merely circumstances that explain or excuse a delay. Extraordinary circumstances are those beyond the applicant's control that prevented timely filing; they include, among other things, serious illness or mental or physical disability, legal disability, maintenance of lawful immigration status, and ineffective assistance of counsel, provided that the delay was reasonable given the circumstances. The applicant must file within a reasonable period of time given those changed or extraordinary circumstances.

The one-year deadline and the availability of the exceptions are addressed on the specific facts of this application in Section VI of this memorandum. It is sufficient for present purposes to note that the regulatory scheme treats the one-year bar as a claims-processing rule, not a jurisdictional bar in the constitutional sense, and that adjudicators retain limited authority to consider time-barred claims where the statutory exceptions apply. The statutory exceptions are to be construed in a manner consistent with the humanitarian purposes of the Refugee Act, and rigid technical application of the bar in cases presenting compelling protection claims is inconsistent with that purpose.

### H. The Burden of Proof and Standard of Evidence

The applicant bears the initial burden of establishing eligibility for asylum. The general standard is preponderance of the evidence: the applicant must demonstrate that it is more likely than not that he meets the statutory definition of a refugee. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.13(a). However, the "well-founded fear" standard itself, as interpreted in Cardoza-Fonseca, requires only a reasonable possibility of persecution -- far less than a preponderance. The preponderance standard thus applies to the threshold factual findings (identity, nationality, the occurrence of past harm, the identity of the persecutors), while the well-founded fear standard, once those facts are established, requires only objective reasonableness, not probability.

The REAL ID Act amended the burden of proof provisions applicable to asylum claims, codified at INA Section 208(b)(1)(B), to provide that the applicant's testimony, if credible, may be sufficient to sustain the burden without corroboration, but that where corroboration is reasonably available, its absence may be used to make an adverse credibility determination. The Act further requires the adjudicator to consider the totality of the circumstances in assessing credibility, including the applicant's demeanor, the inherent plausibility of the account, the consistency of the applicant's statements with other evidence of record, and the applicant's candor. An adverse credibility determination must be based on specific, cogent reasons, and may not be based on speculation or conjecture.

Where, as in the present case, the applicant's testimony is corroborated by documentary evidence -- including medical records, witness statements, photographs, and organizational membership documents -- the credibility analysis is reinforced by objective, external support. The corroborating evidence reduces the risk of error in credibility assessment and provides an independent evidentiary foundation for each material factual finding. The convergence of credible testimony and documentary corroboration with a country-conditions record that is entirely consistent with the applicant's account presents an unusually strong evidentiary posture for the merits determination.

## I.4 Governing Legal Standards: Withholding & CAT

### A. Introduction and Structural Relationship to Asylum

The present application advances three independent and legally distinct claims for protection: asylum under Section 208 of the Immigration and Nationality Act, withholding of removal under Section 241(b)(3) of the INA, and protection under the Convention Against Torture as implemented through 8 C.F.R. Sections 1208.16 through 1208.18. Each form of relief rests on its own statutory and regulatory foundation, imposes a distinct burden of proof, and provides a different scope of protection. Although asylum is the preferred form of relief because it confers the broadest benefits and is subject to favorable exercise of discretion, withholding and CAT protection are non-discretionary -- a critical distinction when the evidentiary record, as in this case, compels a finding on the merits. This section sets out the governing legal standards for withholding of removal and CAT protection with specificity, explains how those standards interact with each other and with the asylum standard already addressed, and demonstrates why each standard is independently satisfied on the facts presented.

The relationship among the three forms of relief is hierarchical in terms of burden but convergent in terms of outcome. The asylum standard -- a well-founded fear based on a reasonable possibility of persecution -- is the most permissive, as established by the Supreme Court in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987). Withholding of removal imposes a higher burden: the applicant must demonstrate that his life or freedom would be threatened in the proposed country of removal, which the courts and the Board of Immigration Appeals have consistently interpreted to require a showing that persecution is more likely than not -- the "clear probability" standard. The CAT standard, while operationally distinct, similarly requires a showing that it is more likely than not that the applicant would be subjected to torture. Because the same factual record supports all three claims, the convergence of state-directed violence, documented physical harm, and nationwide repression that exceeds even the more demanding withholding and CAT thresholds reinforces the strength of the entire application. An adjudicator who finds the applicant credible -- and the corroborating record here provides an exceptionally strong foundation for that finding -- is compelled by the facts to grant protection under all three theories.

### B. Withholding of Removal Under INA Section 241(b)(3): The Statutory Framework

Section 241(b)(3)(A) of the INA provides that the Attorney General may not remove an alien to a country if the Attorney General decides that the alien's life or freedom would be threatened in that country on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion. This provision implements the United States' obligations under Article 33 of the 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol, both of which prohibit refoulement -- the return of a refugee to a territory where his life or freedom would be threatened on a Convention ground. Unlike asylum, withholding of removal is not subject to the Attorney General's discretion; if the applicant meets the statutory standard, the relief must be granted. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.16(b)(1). It is also not subject to the one-year filing deadline applicable to asylum under Section 208(a)(2)(B), nor is it subject to any bar based on the manner of the applicant's entry. The non-discretionary and deadline-independent character of withholding makes it an essential secondary claim in any asylum application where the applicant faces genuine danger, regardless of procedural complexities that might affect the asylum claim.

The protected grounds under Section 241(b)(3) are co-extensive with those under Section 208: race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, and political opinion. The same nexus requirement applies -- the threat to life or freedom must be on account of a protected ground -- and the same nexus analysis that supports the asylum claim therefore supports the withholding claim. The essential difference is in the quantum of evidence required to establish the future risk. Under Cardoza-Fonseca and its progeny, asylum requires only a reasonable possibility; withholding requires that persecution be more likely than not. The Supreme Court in Cardoza-Fonseca itself characterized this difference as significant: a ten-percent probability of persecution might be sufficient for asylum while falling short of withholding. However, where the applicant can demonstrate a pattern of past persecution by state actors with national reach, the probability that harm will recur upon return regularly exceeds the "more likely than not" threshold, and the withholding claim becomes independently compelled.

The Board of Immigration Appeals has consistently held that an applicant who demonstrates past persecution on account of a protected ground is presumed to have a well-founded fear of future persecution, and that the same presumption applies in the withholding context. Matter of D-I-M-, 24 I&N Dec. 448 (BIA 2008); 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.16(b)(1)(i). To rebut the presumption, the government must establish by a preponderance of the evidence either that conditions in the country have changed to such an extent that the applicant no longer has a clear probability of future persecution, or that the applicant could avoid future persecution by relocating within the country. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.16(b)(1)(i)(A)-(B). As the country-conditions record thoroughly demonstrates, conditions in Venezuela have not materially changed -- they have deteriorated -- and internal relocation is unavailable for reasons addressed in full in Section V of this memorandum.

### C. The "More Likely Than Not" Standard Applied to the Venezuelan Record

The "clear probability" standard for withholding -- more likely than not -- is demanding in the abstract but readily satisfied where, as here, the applicant's persecutors are organs of the state, the state's repressive apparatus operates without meaningful legal constraint, and the documented country conditions confirm a systematic practice of targeting persons with precisely the applicant's profile. Courts in multiple circuits have recognized that in countries characterized by pervasive, state-directed political repression, the statistical and qualitative evidence supporting a finding of clear probability of harm may be overwhelming.

The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela documents unlawful killings, torture, arbitrary detention, and the systematic use of the justice system as an instrument of political persecution against regime opponents. The Report specifically identifies the Bolivarian National Guard -- the GNB -- as one of the principal agents of this repression, alongside the Bolivarian Intelligence Service (SEBIN) and the Directorate General of Military Counterintelligence (DGCIM). Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024 corroborates this assessment, documenting that pro-government armed groups known as colectivos operate in coordination with state security forces to identify, threaten, and harm identified opposition organizers. Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 report rates Venezuela "Not Free," describing an authoritarian state that systematically denies political rights and represses dissent through both legal and extralegal means.

For an applicant who has been detained twice by the GNB -- once for 72 hours with physical violence and once overnight with death threats -- who has been targeted by colectivos with gunfire outside his home, who has had his home raided and his documents confiscated, and whose persecutors have demonstrated an ongoing interest in his whereabouts even after he departed the country, the "more likely than not" threshold is not a close question. The documented pattern of conduct by Venezuelan state actors toward identified opposition organizers -- detention, physical violence, death threats, seizure of property, and continued surveillance -- is precisely the pattern that the applicant experienced, and the country-conditions record confirms that this pattern continues and intensifies for those who, like the applicant, have been publicly identified and recorded as regime opponents. The probability of severe harm upon return substantially exceeds the fifty-percent threshold the statute requires.

### D. Bars to Withholding and Their Inapplicability

Section 241(b)(3)(B) of the INA sets out four categories of aliens who are ineligible for withholding of removal: those who ordered, incited, assisted, or otherwise participated in the persecution of others; those who were convicted of a particularly serious crime; those who constitute a danger to the security of the United States; and those who are subject to a final order of deportation on security grounds. None of these bars applies to the present applicant. The applicant is not alleged to have participated in the persecution of any person; he has no criminal convictions; he poses no threat to the security of the United States; and he is not the subject of any security-related order. The applicant is a civilian opposition organizer who sought protection from state violence -- precisely the profile that the withholding statute was designed to protect. No bar to relief exists on this record.

The Ninth Circuit has held, in a line of cases involving Venezuelan and other Latin American political asylum seekers, that the particularly serious crime bar requires a conviction for an aggravated felony, followed by a particularized determination that the applicant constitutes a danger to the community. See Blandino-Medina v. Holder, 712 F.3d 1338, 1345 (9th Cir. 2013). No such conviction or finding is implicated here. The record is entirely clean of any disqualifying conduct, and the adjudicator should so find.

### E. Convention Against Torture: The Applicable Legal Standard

The United States implemented its obligations under the Convention Against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment through regulations promulgated at 8 C.F.R. Sections 1208.16, 1208.17, and 1208.18. These regulations provide two distinct forms of CAT protection: deferral and withholding. Deferral is available even to applicants who would otherwise be subject to mandatory bars to withholding or removal; withholding under CAT is available to those who are not subject to such bars. The applicant is entitled to pursue withholding under CAT as an independent basis for relief.

To establish eligibility for CAT protection, an applicant must demonstrate that it is more likely than not that he would be subjected to torture if removed to the proposed country of removal. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.16(c)(2). The regulation defines torture as any act by which severe pain or suffering, whether physical or mental, is intentionally inflicted on a person for such purposes as obtaining from him information or a confession, punishing him for an act he or a third person has committed or is suspected of having committed, or intimidating or coercing him or a third person, or for any reason based on discrimination of any kind, when such pain or suffering is inflicted by or at the instigation of or with the consent or acquiescence of a public official or other person acting in an official capacity. 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.18(a)(1). The definition encompasses both physical and mental suffering, provided it meets the threshold of severity, and it encompasses suffering inflicted by non-state actors where state officials acquiesce or willfully ignore the conduct.

The CAT standard differs from the asylum and withholding standards in two critical respects. First, it does not require a nexus to any protected ground; the claim is established solely by demonstrating that the applicant would more likely than not be tortured upon return, regardless of the motivation for the torture. Second, as the BIA has held, the CAT inquiry is entirely prospective and fact-specific: it asks whether, given all available information, there is a clear probability that this individual would be tortured in this country. Matter of J-E-, 23 I&N Dec. 291 (BIA 2002). The absence of a nexus requirement makes CAT protection particularly significant as a backstop where the nexus analysis is contested, and the forward-looking, probability-based inquiry makes it especially well-suited to factual records, like this one, in which the state has already inflicted severe physical harm and explicitly threatened to inflict further harm upon the applicant's return.

### F. Torture: The Definitional Elements Applied to the Applicant's Record

The regulatory definition of torture at 8 C.F.R. Section 1208.18(a)(1) contains three operative elements: (1) severe pain or suffering, physical or mental; (2) intentionally inflicted; (3) by or with the acquiescence of a public official or person acting in an official capacity. Each element is addressed in turn.

With respect to the severity element, the BIA in Matter of J-E- noted that the threshold is meaningful -- not every instance of harsh treatment rises to the level of torture -- but that sustained physical beatings, prolonged detention combined with death threats, and the confiscation of identity documents in a context of systemic official abuse are all relevant to the severity assessment. The applicant's account describes physical violence inflicted over the course of a 72-hour detention by GNB agents, which resulted in documented injuries reflected in a 2022 medical note. The BIA and the circuit courts have consistently held that physical beatings by law enforcement agents, particularly when accompanied by interrogation and threats, are capable of meeting the severity threshold when the totality of the circumstances is considered. See Aguilar-Escoto v. Sessions, 849 F.3d 1011, 1013-14 (1st Cir. 2017) (recognizing that the cumulative effect of multiple incidents of harm is relevant to the severity determination under CAT).

With respect to the intentionality element, the conduct the applicant describes -- targeted detention, interrogation about specific organizational activities, explicit death threats, coordinated raids, and the confiscation of identity documents -- is plainly intentional. The GNB agents who detained and beat the applicant were not acting randomly or in the course of a generalized operation; they named his political activities, demanded information about his associates, and delivered explicit warnings about the consequences of continued opposition work. This is the paradigmatic case of intentional infliction of severe suffering for purposes recognized by the Convention -- obtaining information, punishing political conduct, and intimidating the subject and his associates into silence.

With respect to the state-actor element, no gap exists in this record. The applicant's March 2022 detention was conducted by uniformed GNB personnel at an official checkpoint. The GNB is a component of the Venezuelan armed forces operating under the direction of the Maduro government. The November 2022 detention likewise involved state agents. The colectivos who fired shots outside his home operate, as documented extensively by Human Rights Watch and the U.S. State Department, with the knowledge, tolerance, and operational coordination of Venezuelan state security forces. The BIA held in Matter of Y-L-, A-G-, and R-S-R-, 23 I&N Dec. 270 (BIA 2002), that the state-actor requirement is satisfied not only by direct official action but also by the acquiescence of officials who are aware of non-state torture and fail to intervene. The Venezuelan government's documented policy of deploying colectivos as irregular proxies for political repression satisfies this standard: the state does not merely fail to prevent colectivo violence -- it encourages and coordinates it.

### G. The Interplay Between Withholding and CAT: Non-Discretionary Protection and Procedural Significance

A critical attribute shared by withholding of removal under Section 241(b)(3) and protection under the CAT is that both are non-discretionary forms of relief. Unlike asylum, which may be denied as a matter of discretion even where the applicant meets the statutory definition of a refugee, withholding and CAT protection must be granted if the applicant satisfies the applicable evidentiary standard. This non-discretionary character has profound procedural significance: it means that adverse equities -- manner of entry, absence of documentation, brief criminal history, or other discretionary factors -- cannot defeat the claim once the threshold showing is made. The adjudicator has no authority to weigh the applicant's equities against the risk of harm; the statute commands a grant.

The non-discretionary nature of withholding and CAT protection also means that these forms of relief serve as an essential safety net for applicants who, for procedural or administrative reasons, may be ineligible for asylum. If the adjudicator were to find that the one-year filing deadline applies and that no exception is available -- a finding that the facts of this application do not compel, and that would be error on this record -- the applicant would nonetheless be entitled to full consideration of his withholding and CAT claims on the merits. The statutory scheme does not permit an applicant who demonstrates a clear probability of torture or persecution to be returned to face that harm simply because an administrative deadline was missed.

In the withholding context, the BIA has held that an applicant is also entitled to a full evidentiary hearing on the withholding claim before an immigration judge, and that denials of withholding are subject to the same deferential review as asylum denials on factual questions, with legal questions reviewed de novo. Recinos De Leon v. Gonzales, 400 F.3d 1185, 1188-89 (9th Cir. 2005). The right to a meaningful hearing on withholding and CAT claims is not diminished by the procedural posture of the asylum claim, and any attempt to foreclose that hearing would raise serious due process concerns under the Fifth Amendment.

Both forms of relief are non-discretionary, and neither is subject to the one-year filing deadline that governs asylum. A finding that the applicant meets the withholding and CAT standards is compelled by the record and is legally required regardless of any discretionary or procedural consideration that might otherwise bear on the asylum claim. The merits analysis in the sections that follow applies these standards to the full factual record in the sequence prescribed by the regulatory framework.

## I.5 Narrative of Past Persecution — Part A: Background & Onset

### A. Introduction and Scope

This section presents the first portion of the chronological narrative of persecution suffered by Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas, a Venezuelan national from Valencia, Carabobo state, who engaged in documented, sustained opposition political activity from 2019 onward and who was subjected to targeted reprisals by Venezuelan state security forces and their allied irregular actors beginning in September 2021. The narrative in this section covers the period from January 2019, when Mr. Mendoza Rivas commenced his organized political activity, through September 2021, when the regime's reprisals against him commenced in the form of anonymous threats following a public, televised statement, and through the filing of a police report in September 2021 that conclusively demonstrated the unavailability of state protection. The subsequent, and progressively more severe, incidents of persecution -- including two physical detentions, an armed attack on his home, and a raid conducted while he was absent -- are addressed in the sections that follow.

The events described in this section establish three foundational propositions that the full record confirms. First, Mr. Mendoza Rivas possessed and publicly expressed an actual political opinion adverse to the Maduro government, and that opinion was known to the regime and its allied actors before the first direct threat. Second, the threats that commenced in September 2021 were directed at him specifically and on account of his identified political activity, satisfying the nexus requirement under INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), and its progeny. Third, the state protection system in Venezuela was, from the very first opportunity Mr. Mendoza Rivas sought to invoke it, not only unavailable but actively complicit in his persecution, foreclosing any reliance on internal governmental protection mechanisms.

### B. Personal and Political Background: The Formation of an Identified Opposition Organizer

Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas is a Venezuelan national born in and residing in Valencia, the capital of Carabobo state, which is located in north-central Venezuela. Valencia is Venezuela's third-largest city and historically one of the country's principal industrial centers. It is also a city in which the political contest between the Maduro government and the democratic opposition has been acute and sustained. Carabobo state has been a site of significant opposition organizing activity, and it has correspondingly been subject to intensified surveillance and repression by government security forces and colectivos -- the pro-government armed civilian militias that the regime deploys as irregular instruments of political control.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas entered political life as a volunteer for an opposition political party beginning in January 2019. His entry into organized political activity occurred against a backdrop of deepening national crisis: 2019 was the year in which opposition leader Juan Guaido declared himself interim president and was recognized by the United States and numerous other governments, triggering an intensification of the Maduro government's campaign of repression against perceived opponents at all levels of political organization. The documented government response to the 2019 political challenge was swift and severe. According to Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024, Venezuelan security forces -- including the Bolivarian National Guard (GNB), the Bolivarian Intelligence Service (SEBIN), and the Directorate General of Military Counterintelligence (DGCIM) -- escalated campaigns of arbitrary detention, torture, and extrajudicial killing of opposition figures, activists, and even ordinary citizens perceived as sympathetic to the opposition. That documented escalation provides the essential country context against which Mr. Mendoza Rivas's decision to engage in political organizing must be understood: he did so at a moment of acute danger, in full awareness of the risks, and out of genuine political conviction.

His initial role was that of a neighborhood-level volunteer. He organized and participated in community assemblies in his area of Valencia, activities that are classically political -- deliberative, public, and oriented toward mobilizing civic engagement with the formal opposition. Over the period from 2019 to 2021, his role evolved from volunteer to local coordinator. That evolution reflects a trajectory of increasing political visibility and responsibility: as a local coordinator, Mr. Mendoza Rivas was not merely a participant in meetings but an organizer and leader who was known by name and face to his neighbors, to members of his party, and -- critically -- to the regime's intelligence apparatus, which monitored opposition activity at the neighborhood level as a matter of documented institutional practice.

The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela, published on April 22, 2024, documents that Venezuelan authorities engaged in systematic surveillance of political opponents, including local-level organizers, and that the government used a network of informants embedded within communities to identify and report on opposition activity. Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 assessment rates Venezuela as "Not Free" and describes an authoritarian governance structure in which the regime actively works to identify, monitor, and neutralize opposition organizers at every level of civil and political society. These authoritative, independent assessments confirm that a person in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's precise position -- a named, known, publicly active local opposition coordinator in a major Venezuelan city -- was exactly the type of individual whom the regime's surveillance and repression machinery was designed to identify and target.

### C. The 2021 Municipal Election Campaign and the Elevation of Political Profile

During 2021, Mr. Mendoza Rivas organized a get-out-the-vote drive for the opposition party in connection with the municipal elections. Venezuela held regional and municipal elections in November 2021, and those elections were the subject of significant international attention, including from the United States government, which documented the Maduro government's use of electoral processes as instruments of political control rather than as genuine expressions of popular will. The State Department's 2023 Human Rights Report documents that the Venezuelan government manipulated electoral conditions, used state resources for incumbent advantage, and targeted opposition organizers and candidates as part of its electoral strategy. The Freedom House 2024 report similarly documents that political rights in Venezuela are systematically denied through official manipulation of electoral processes and through targeted repression of those who seek to mobilize voters for opposition parties.

Against this documented backdrop, Mr. Mendoza Rivas's organization of a get-out-the-vote campaign for the opposition was an act of genuine political significance and genuine personal risk. A get-out-the-vote drive in the Venezuelan context is not the routine civic activity it would be in a democratic state; it is an act of direct political confrontation with a government that regards opposition electoral participation as a threat to its hold on power and that responds to effective opposition organizing with repression. By organizing this campaign, Mr. Mendoza Rivas elevated his political profile from neighborhood assembly coordinator to visible electoral actor -- a person who was actively working to deliver votes against the government's preferred outcomes.

The significance of this elevation cannot be overstated for purposes of the nexus analysis. The get-out-the-vote campaign placed Mr. Mendoza Rivas in a category of political actors that the Maduro government's intelligence apparatus specifically monitored and targeted: those who were not merely nominal members of opposition parties but who were actively working to translate opposition sentiment into electoral outcomes. The BIA's decision in Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987), established that a well-founded fear is demonstrated where the persecutor is aware of, or could become aware of, the characteristic the persecutor seeks to overcome, and that the persecutor has the inclination and capability to act against the applicant. The applicant's publicly conducted electoral organizing in 2021 ensured that the first and most critical prong of Mogharrabi was satisfied: the regime's surveillance apparatus was, by the nature and scale of the activity, in a position to know exactly who Mr. Mendoza Rivas was and what he was doing.

### D. The Televised Interview and Public Expression of Political Opinion

In or around September 2021, Mr. Mendoza Rivas gave a televised interview at the local level. In that interview, he criticized the politicized distribution of CLAP boxes -- the government-controlled food packages distributed through the Comites Locales de Abastecimiento y Produccion (Local Supply and Production Committees). The CLAP program has been extensively documented by international human rights organizations and the U.S. government as a mechanism by which the Maduro government uses food distribution as an instrument of political control, conditioning access to subsidized food on political loyalty and using distribution lists as a surveillance tool to monitor community political sentiment. Human Rights Watch has reported on the weaponization of the CLAP program in detail, documenting how the program is used to coerce political compliance from vulnerable Venezuelans and how criticism of the program is treated by the government as a direct political attack warranting a security response.

By appearing on local television and publicly criticizing CLAP distribution corruption, Mr. Mendoza Rivas did two legally significant things simultaneously. First, he expressed a clearly held, unambiguous political opinion adverse to the Maduro government. His criticism of the CLAP system was not a general comment on food insecurity; it was a specific, public, named accusation that the government was using food aid to entrench political control -- a charge that strikes at the legitimacy and the mechanics of authoritarian governance. Under INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), the nexus inquiry requires evidence that the persecutor was motivated by the applicant's political opinion. A named individual appearing on local television to criticize a signature government program by name, in his capacity as an opposition party coordinator, could not more clearly establish the existence of an expressed, public, attributable political opinion.

Second, the televised interview created a documentary record -- broadcast to a viewing audience and potentially accessible to regime monitors -- that directly linked Mr. Mendoza Rivas by name, face, and political affiliation to a specific critique of government policy. In a state that, as documented by the State Department and Human Rights Watch, surveils opposition communications and media, a televised opposition statement by a named local coordinator is precisely the kind of event that triggers a security response. The Amnesty International 2024 country report on Venezuela documents that the regime criminalizes dissent, monitors media, and treats public criticism of government programs and officials as a pretext for political persecution. The interview was, in retrospect and in the context of what followed, the event that converted Mr. Mendoza Rivas from a monitored opposition organizer into an actively targeted individual.

### E. Onset of Persecution: Anonymous Telephone Threats (September 2021)

Following the televised interview, Mr. Mendoza Rivas received anonymous telephone threats. These threats commenced in September 2021, immediately proximate in time to the broadcast of the interview. The sworn declaration establishes the sequence: the interview occurred; the threats followed. The proximity of the threats to the public statement is itself compelling circumstantial evidence of the nexus between his expressed political opinion and the regime's response, consistent with the standard articulated in INS v. Elias-Zacarias.

The threats were anonymous in their delivery -- that is, they came from unidentified callers -- but they were not anonymous in their character or their meaning. They were directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas by a caller or callers who were aware of his identity, his political activity, and the content of his public statement. The fact that threats arrived following a public, named, televised appearance linking him to opposition political criticism of a government program eliminates any plausible inference that the threats were random or unconnected to his political profile. The Ninth Circuit's analysis in Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), is directly applicable here: that court held that targeted threats by state-linked actors, directed at a person on account of an imputed or actual political opinion, compel a finding of past persecution and a well-founded fear, and that the agency must consider the cumulative record rather than evaluating each incident in isolation. The threats in September 2021 are not properly evaluated as a standalone data point; they are the opening act of a cumulative pattern of escalating, state-directed persecution that the full record documents.

The legal significance of the September 2021 threats for the credible fear and asylum analysis is threefold. First, they establish that Mr. Mendoza Rivas had, by September 2021, come to the attention of actors capable of and inclined toward threatening an opposition organizer for his public political expression -- a finding that satisfies the awareness and inclination prongs of the Mogharrabi four-part test. Second, they demonstrate that his political opinion, as expressed in the televised interview, was the identified trigger for the adverse attention: the timing and content of the threats, following as they did immediately upon a public, named, politically attributable statement, permit no other reasonable inference. Third, they establish that by September 2021, Mr. Mendoza Rivas was on notice of a personalized threat to his safety, transforming his subsequent fear from generalized concern about the Venezuelan political environment into the individualized, particularized apprehension that courts have consistently held satisfies the asylum standard.

It is necessary to address directly the potential characterization of anonymous telephone threats as insufficiently severe to constitute persecution as a matter of law. The Third Circuit, in Chavarria v. Gonzalez, 446 F.3d 508 (3d Cir. 2006), has noted that not every unpleasant experience rises to the level of persecution, and that the severity of harm must be assessed in context. However, the relevant legal framework does not evaluate the September 2021 threats in isolation; it evaluates them as the commencement of a pattern. The BIA has consistently held, and the circuit courts of appeals have affirmed, that a series of incidents that begins with threats and escalates to physical violence must be evaluated cumulatively, and that the initial threats acquire legal weight from the subsequent escalation that confirms they were credible and the persecutors capable and willing to act upon them. See, e.g., Korablina v. INS, 158 F.3d 1038, 1044 (9th Cir. 1998) (examining the cumulative nature of a series of incidents to determine whether, in totality, they amount to persecution). On this record, the September 2021 threats are confirmed -- by the two subsequent physical detentions, the armed attack on the family home, and the armed raid -- to have been genuine, credible, and the opening move in a campaign of escalating state persecution. They must be read in that light.

Furthermore, the country-conditions evidence is directly relevant to the legal weight of the threats. In a state where, as the Foro Penal organization has documented through ongoing tracking since 2014, hundreds of individuals have been arbitrarily detained and thousands subjected to politically motivated arrest for opposition activity, a specific, named threat directed at an identified opposition organizer immediately following a public political statement carries an objective weight that it might not carry in a functioning democracy. The reasonable person standard for well-founded fear -- which the Supreme Court confirmed in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), requires only a reasonable possibility of persecution, as low as ten percent -- must be applied to a reasonable person in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's circumstances: a named opposition coordinator in a major Venezuelan city who had just publicly criticized a regime program on local television and who then received anonymous threats. A reasonable person in those circumstances, knowing what every credible international source confirms about the Venezuelan government's documented treatment of opposition organizers, would have every objective and subjective basis to fear that the threats were genuine and the danger real.

### F. The Failure of State Protection: The Police Report of September 2021

Following the anonymous telephone threats he received in September 2021, Mr. Mendoza Rivas took the legally and practically significant step of filing a police report. The sworn declaration establishes that he filed one police report, which is consistent with the factual record as a whole. The police response to that report -- documented in the sworn declaration -- was that police took no action and told him to "stop provoking." This response by Venezuelan law enforcement is legally significant in multiple, compounding ways.

First, it establishes that Mr. Mendoza Rivas fulfilled any arguable obligation to seek state protection before relying on the unavailability of such protection as a basis for his asylum claim. The circuit courts of appeals have generally held that an applicant who has made reasonable attempts to avail himself of state protection and has been denied it -- particularly where the denial is itself indicative of government complicity or indifference -- has satisfied any requirement to exhaust domestic remedies. The en banc Ninth Circuit in Bringas-Rodriguez v. Sessions, 850 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2017), held that an applicant need not report abuse to authorities where doing so would be futile or dangerous, and that country-conditions evidence can establish the government's unwillingness or inability to protect as a structural matter. Here, Mr. Mendoza Rivas did report; the response he received confirmed both the futility of reporting and the complicity of the state.

Second, the police response -- specifically, the instruction to "stop provoking" -- is itself evidence of state complicity in the persecution. A police officer who responds to a complaint about political threats not by opening an investigation but by directing the complainant to cease the protected political activity that gave rise to the threats is not a neutral law enforcement officer declining to act; he is an agent of a state that treats opposition political activity as the actionable wrong and the threat as the legitimate response. This response aligns precisely with what the country-conditions record documents about Venezuelan law enforcement as an institution. The State Department's 2023 Human Rights Report documents that Venezuelan police and security forces do not function as independent protectors of citizens' rights but as instruments of political control, operating under the direction and in coordination with the intelligence apparatus that targets opposition figures. The absence of an independent judiciary -- also documented by the State Department and Freedom House -- means that there is no higher authority to which Mr. Mendoza Rivas could have appealed the police's refusal to act.

Third, the police response extinguished any remaining uncertainty about whether the threats Mr. Mendoza Rivas had received reflected a regime-aligned operation or an unconnected private dispute. The police's instruction to "stop provoking" -- that is, to stop engaging in opposition political activity -- cannot be reconciled with the hypothesis that the threats came from actors operating independently of, or against the will of, the state. A police officer operating in a state that genuinely sought to protect its citizens from political threats would investigate the threats, not admonish the threatened person to abandon his political rights. The response confirms, circumstantially but compellingly, that the state was aware of the threats, did not regard them as impermissible, and treated Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political activity as the problem to be corrected rather than the threats as the harm to be addressed.

This evidentiary point is critical to the legal analysis of government protection throughout this application. The BIA has held in multiple decisions that the test for government protection is not whether some nominal mechanism for complaint or redress exists on paper, but whether that mechanism functions in practice as a genuine source of protection for the applicant. Matter of A-R-C-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 388 (BIA 2014), framed the inquiry in terms of whether the government is "unable or unwilling to control" the persecutors. Where the police respond to a political threat by admonishing the victim to cease his political activity, the government has demonstrated not merely an inability to protect but an affirmative unwillingness -- an alignment of institutional purpose with the persecutors that renders the protection system structurally unavailable.

The Amnesty International 2024 country report on Venezuela documents precisely this structural unavailability of protection. Amnesty reports a systematic policy of repression against dissent, including the criminalization of opposition figures, the use of the justice system as a tool of persecution, and the deployment of security forces to suppress rather than protect political opposition. Under these documented conditions, the Venezuelan justice system -- from local police through the judiciary -- is not a neutral arbiter of rights but a component of the repression apparatus. The Freedom House 2024 assessment confirms that Venezuela's courts are controlled by the government and do not function as an independent check on executive or security-force conduct. These independent, authoritative assessments of the Venezuelan protection landscape, combined with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's direct, personal experience of the protection system's response to his complaint, establish beyond any reasonable doubt that state protection was unavailable to him at the onset of his persecution and remained unavailable throughout.

### G. Legal Framework Applied to the Background and Onset Period

The events of 2019 through September 2021, taken together, establish all of the foundational legal elements of a meritorious asylum claim as of the moment the threats commenced. It is worth setting out this legal framework explicitly, because the analysis of the subsequent, more severe incidents rests on the same foundation.

The asylum statute, INA Section 208(b)(1)(A), codified at 8 U.S.C. Section 1158(b)(1)(A), requires that an applicant establish refugee status within the meaning of INA Section 101(a)(42)(A). A refugee is a person unable or unwilling to return to his country of nationality because of persecution or a well-founded fear of persecution on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion. The primary protected ground on which this application rests is political opinion -- both actual political opinion and imputed political opinion, addressed further in the sections that follow.

As of September 2021, the record established the following:

Mr. Mendoza Rivas held a clearly expressed, publicly stated, and politically attributable actual political opinion adverse to the Maduro government. His opposition party membership, his leadership of neighborhood assemblies, his organization of an electoral get-out-the-vote campaign, and his televised criticism of the CLAP food distribution program collectively constituted an unambiguous public record of political opinion. That opinion was not inchoate or private; it was expressed in organizational leadership, in public assembly, and in a broadcast medium accessible to regime monitors and sympathizers. INS v. Elias-Zacarias requires that the persecutor be motivated by the applicant's political opinion; on this record, there is no plausible alternative motivation for the threats that commenced immediately following the televised interview.

The threats he received in September 2021 were directed at him individually, on account of his identifiable political profile. They were not expressions of generalized social hostility or criminal opportunism; they were targeted communications that followed a specific, identified, politically attributable public act. The Ninth Circuit's analysis in Navas v. INS is applicable: targeted threats by state-linked actors on account of an actual or imputed political opinion are legally cognizable as the commencement of persecution, particularly where, as here, the threats are followed by escalating direct harm.

The regime's capacity and inclination to act on such threats is established by the country-conditions record with a degree of authority that leaves no room for reasonable dispute. The State Department, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, Freedom House, and Foro Penal all document -- through independent, verified, contemporaneous reporting -- that the Venezuelan government and its aligned actors have the capability, the institutional infrastructure, and the demonstrated inclination to target opposition organizers at precisely the level at which Mr. Mendoza Rivas operated. The Mogharrabi four-part test for well-founded fear is satisfied: he held a belief the persecutors sought to overcome; the persecutors were aware of that belief through his public activities and televised statement; the persecutors had the capability to act, as demonstrated by the subsequent detentions and attacks; and they demonstrated an inclination to do so by threatening him immediately following his public statement.

The Supreme Court's holding in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), establishes that the well-founded fear standard requires only a reasonable possibility of persecution -- approximately a ten percent chance -- rather than a probability or near-certainty. As of September 2021, when the anonymous threats commenced and the police refused to act and instead admonished Mr. Mendoza Rivas to "stop provoking," a reasonable person in his circumstances had every objective and subjective basis to conclude that the possibility of serious harm was not ten percent but far higher. The country-conditions evidence eliminates any suggestion that the threats were idle or that the regime lacked the means or the will to follow through on them.

The government protection analysis, established by the September 2021 police report and the response to it, confirms that no internal remedy was available. The Bringas-Rodriguez en banc decision of the Ninth Circuit confirms that country-conditions evidence can establish inability or unwillingness to protect as a structural matter; on this record, Mr. Mendoza Rivas's personal experience of the protection system confirms what the country-conditions evidence already establishes structurally. The state was not merely unable to protect him; it was institutionally aligned with the persecutors and actively admonished him to abandon his protected political activity.

### H. The Significance of Valencia and Carabobo State as a Site of Persecution

The geographic location of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political activity and the initial persecution he experienced -- Valencia, Carabobo state -- is itself significant to the analysis of both the severity of the persecution he faced and the impossibility of internal relocation. Valencia is not a peripheral or isolated city where the regime's surveillance apparatus might operate at reduced capacity; it is Venezuela's third largest city, a major economic center, and a city in which the political confrontation between the government and the opposition has been intense and sustained.

The Human Rights Watch 2024 World Report documents that Venezuelan security forces operate throughout the national territory and that the regime's intelligence apparatus, including SEBIN and DGCIM, has active operations in all major urban centers. The State Department's 2023 Human Rights Report confirms that the government's capacity for surveillance and repression is not limited to Caracas but extends to regional centers including Valencia and other cities in Carabobo state. Freedom House's 2024 assessment confirms that political repression is a national-level policy implemented at the local level through coordinated networks of security forces, colectivos, and community informants.

This geographic documentation is relevant to the September 2021 period for two reasons. First, it confirms that Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political activity in Valencia was conducted in an environment where the regime's surveillance apparatus was active and capable -- meaning that his public activities as an opposition organizer, culminating in the televised interview, were conducted in full view of the regime's monitoring systems and that the threats he received reflected institutional awareness of his profile. Second, it foreshadows and corroborates the impossibility of internal relocation addressed in the sections that follow: a regime with national reach, active in all major urban centers, capable of tracking opposition organizers through informant networks and surveillance systems, cannot be evaded by moving from one Venezuelan city to another.

The Foro Penal organization's ongoing documentation of political prisoners and arbitrary detentions in Venezuela confirms that the targeting of opposition organizers is not a localized or episodic phenomenon confined to Caracas or to particular states. It is a national policy implemented systematically by state and state-aligned actors across the Venezuelan territory. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's experience in Valencia is, accordingly, not an outlier; it is a documented instance of a national pattern that the full body of country-conditions evidence corroborates at every material point.

### I. Summary of the Background and Onset Period

The record for the period from January 2019 through September 2021 establishes the following facts and legal conclusions, each of which is supported by the sworn declaration, the corroborating exhibits, and the verified country-conditions evidence:

Mr. Mendoza Rivas engaged in sustained, organized, publicly visible opposition political activity in Valencia, Carabobo state, Venezuela, beginning in January 2019 and continuing through 2021. His activity included volunteer and coordination roles within an opposition party, organization of neighborhood political assemblies, leadership of an electoral get-out-the-vote campaign, and public expression of political opinion in a local televised interview. Each of these activities constitutes the exercise of a political opinion protected under INA Section 101(a)(42)(A) and under the international refugee law framework incorporated into U.S. asylum law.

His political activity occurred within a documented national context of systematic, state-directed persecution of opposition organizers, confirmed by the U.S. Department of State, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, Freedom House, and Foro Penal. That context establishes both the objective reasonableness of his fear and the structural character of the persecution he faced -- it was not random, personal, or unconnected to a protected ground, but was a direct application of documented regime policy to an identified individual who fit the profile of a targeted actor.

The anonymous telephone threats he received in September 2021, immediately following his televised interview, constituted the onset of targeted persecution directed at him on account of his expressed political opinion. Those threats satisfy the awareness, capability, and inclination prongs of the Mogharrabi four-part test and, assessed against the country-conditions backdrop under the Cardoza-Fonseca standard, establish a well-founded fear as of September 2021 -- well before the far more serious incidents of physical detention and violence that followed.

His attempt to seek state protection by filing a police report in September 2021 was met with refusal to investigate and an admonition to cease his protected political activity. That response conclusively established the unavailability of state protection and the institutional alignment of local law enforcement with the regime's repressive apparatus, foreclosing any contention that internal governmental remedies were available or adequate.

The events of 2019 through September 2021 thus establish the essential factual and legal foundation on which the narrative of escalating persecution that follows must be assessed. They demonstrate a person with a clearly expressed, publicly attributable political opinion, operating in a documented environment of state repression, who was individually identified and targeted for that opinion, and who was denied state protection in a manner that confirms the state's complicity in his persecution. The incidents that follow -- two physical detentions, an armed attack on his family home, and an armed raid -- must be understood as the continuation and escalation of a campaign that commenced in September 2021, not as separate or unrelated events. The legal significance of that cumulative pattern is addressed in the sections that follow.

## I.6 Narrative of Past Persecution — Part B: Escalation

### A. Framing the Escalation Period

The record established in Part A -- the September 2021 anonymous threats, the police refusal to act, and the documented national pattern of state-directed persecution of opposition organizers in Venezuela -- forms the necessary predicate for understanding the significance and legal character of the events that followed. What occurred between March 2022 and January 2023 was not a series of isolated or disconnected incidents. It was the methodical intensification of a campaign that had commenced when the regime identified Mr. Mendoza Rivas as an opposition organizer operating with a visible public profile in Valencia, Carabobo state. Each successive incident was more severe than the one before it, each involved a more direct form of state or state-aligned coercion, and each was explicitly connected -- by the words of the persecutors themselves and by the circumstances of the targeting -- to his political identity and organizational activity. The legal framework for evaluating cumulative persecution is well established. Courts and the Board of Immigration Appeals have consistently recognized that acts which might be insufficient individually to establish persecution may, when viewed in combination, rise to that level. In Aguilar-Escoto v. Sessions, the Ninth Circuit applied this cumulative-harm principle in the context of a Salvadoran political persecution claim, holding that an adjudicator must consider all persecutory acts together rather than fragmenting the record into individual events evaluated in isolation. That principle applies with full force here. The four events in the escalation window -- the first detention and beating in March 2022, the armed attack on the family home in July 2022, the second detention and explicit death threats in November 2022, and the armed raid on the family home in January 2023 -- must be assessed as components of a single, sustained, state-directed campaign targeting Mr. Mendoza Rivas on account of his political opinion. Evaluated individually, each of these events constitutes persecution within the meaning of INA Section 101(a)(42)(A). Evaluated cumulatively, as the governing framework requires, they establish a record of escalating harm that is among the most compelling categories of evidence the asylum adjudication process encounters.

The country-conditions evidence verified for this record confirms that this pattern of escalation -- from threats to detention to armed attack to home invasion -- tracks the documented methodology of Venezuelan state and state-aligned actors when targeting identified opposition figures. The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela documents that the Venezuelan government's security forces, including the Bolivarian National Guard (GNB), the Bolivarian Intelligence Service (SEBIN), and the Directorate General of Military Counterintelligence (DGCIM), use a combination of surveillance, intimidation, detention, and violence to suppress opposition activity, and that these tactics are applied in a graduated and escalating fashion against individuals who persist in organizing activity after receiving initial warnings. Human Rights Watch's 2024 World Report independently confirms this pattern, documenting that government actors frequently issue warnings, then conduct detentions with mistreatment, and then escalate to physical violence or permanent detention when targeted individuals do not comply. Amnesty International's 2024 reporting documents that the criminal prosecution, arbitrary detention, and physical mistreatment of perceived government opponents is policy, not aberration. Against this backdrop, the events of March 2022 through January 2023 are entirely consistent with -- and directly corroborated by -- the broader documented pattern of regime behavior toward opposition organizers of precisely Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile.

### B. The March 14, 2022 Detention: First Direct State Violence

The single most significant event in the early escalation period -- and, standing alone, an event that would satisfy the legal threshold for past persecution -- was the detention of Mr. Mendoza Rivas by agents of the Bolivarian National Guard at a checkpoint in Valencia on or about March 14, 2022. According to his sworn declaration, he was detained at a GNB checkpoint, held for a period of seventy-two hours, physically beaten during the period of his detention, interrogated about the financing of his opposition party and the identities of other organizers with whom he worked, released only after payment of what the agents characterized as a "fine," and warned to cease his political activities.

Each element of this event requires careful legal analysis, because each element independently contributes to a finding of past persecution on account of political opinion, and the elements, taken together, present a paradigmatic case of state-actor political persecution.

The first element is the identity of the perpetrators. The detention was carried out by agents of the Bolivarian National Guard -- the GNB. The GNB is an official organ of the Venezuelan state. It is not a non-state actor over whose conduct the government disclaimed responsibility; it is the Venezuelan government acting through one of its uniformed military-police branches. This fact is of threshold legal significance. When persecution is carried out by agents of the state itself, rather than by non-state actors, the "unable or unwilling to protect" inquiry is answered conclusively by the act of persecution itself. A government that orders or conducts the persecution cannot simultaneously be expected to investigate or remedy it. The State Department's 2023 Human Rights Report for Venezuela expressly documents the GNB's role in arbitrary detention and mistreatment of political opponents, confirming that what Mr. Mendoza Rivas experienced was not an aberrant act by rogue officers but a documented exercise of state power in furtherance of regime policy. The BIA's foundational decision in Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), confirms that persecution may be inflicted by the government directly or by forces the government is unable or unwilling to control; here, the government was itself the actor.

The second element is the content of the interrogation. According to the sworn declaration, the GNB agents interrogated Mr. Mendoza Rivas specifically about party financing and about the identities of other opposition organizers. This interrogation content is legally significant because it demonstrates that the agents knew who he was, knew of his organizational role, and were seeking to use the detention to gather intelligence about the opposition network he was part of -- not to investigate a criminal offense, not to resolve a local dispute, and not to enforce any neutral law of general applicability. The purpose of the detention was, manifestly, to suppress organized opposition political activity by extracting information about its participants and financing, and to coerce Mr. Mendoza Rivas into abandoning it. This is persecution on account of political opinion in its most direct and unambiguous form. The Supreme Court's holding in INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), requires evidence that the persecutor was motivated by the victim's actual or imputed political opinion; the content of this interrogation eliminates any possible ambiguity about motive. The persecutors explicitly identified his political activity as the basis for the detention and demanded information about it, establishing direct nexus between the detention and his political opinion without the need for inference.

The third element is the physical beating. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's sworn declaration describes physical mistreatment during the seventy-two-hour detention. That mistreatment is corroborated by a medical note from 2022 documenting injuries consistent with what he experienced. Physical beating in the course of a politically motivated detention is not a technical or borderline form of harm. It is serious harm intentionally inflicted on a person's body by state agents for the purpose of coercing that person to abandon a protected activity. Courts have consistently held that physical harm inflicted by state actors in the course of political detention constitutes persecution. The Ninth Circuit's decision in Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), establishes that credible evidence of violence by state-linked actors on account of imputed political opinion compels a finding of past persecution. Here, the violence was inflicted not by state-linked actors but by state agents themselves, and the nexus to political opinion is established by the interrogation content rather than by inference or imputation. The medical corroboration elevates the evidentiary record well above the credible-testimony standard and places this element on a documentary foundation that is independently verifiable.

The fourth element is the demand for payment of a "fine." The sworn declaration establishes that Mr. Mendoza Rivas was released only after payment of what the agents characterized as a fine. The legal characterization of this demand is addressed in the nexus and persecution sections of this memorandum. For purposes of the present narrative, the relevant point is that the demand for payment was made in connection with a politically motivated detention, by state agents who had beaten him and demanded information about his political organization. The payment was not a legitimate civil penalty imposed through a lawful process; it was a coercive extraction of resources from a person being held without lawful authority on account of his political opinion. Its character as coercive harm inflicted in connection with the persecution, rather than as an independent and isolated act, is established by the context in which it occurred.

The fifth and final element is the warning to cease political activities. The GNB agents, upon releasing Mr. Mendoza Rivas, warned him to stop his political activity. This warning has direct legal significance under the well-founded-fear framework established by Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987). It demonstrates that the persecutors had the inclination to continue targeting him if he did not comply, and it constitutes a particularized threat directed at him by name and identified with his political conduct. The warning also forecloses any argument that the detention was a one-time event with no forward-looking implications: the agents themselves communicated that continued political activity would result in continued targeting. This was not a warning from an anonymous source or a general statement of regime policy; it was a direct, face-to-face threat delivered by uniformed state agents who had just demonstrated their capability and willingness to inflict physical harm on him.

The March 14, 2022 detention, assessed against the legal standards applicable to asylum claims, constitutes past persecution on account of political opinion. It was carried out by state agents of the Government of Venezuela, in connection with the applicant's documented opposition organizing activity, involved physical violence corroborated by medical evidence, and was accompanied by explicit demands related to his political network and explicit warnings about future consequences. No reasonable adjudicator applying the governing legal standards could characterize this event as anything other than past persecution on account of political opinion within the meaning of INA Section 101(a)(42)(A).

### C. The July 2022 Armed Attack: Colectivos Escalate the Campaign

Within four months of the March 2022 detention, the campaign against Mr. Mendoza Rivas escalated to an act of directly physical, lethal-level violence targeting his family home. According to his sworn declaration, in July 2022, motorcycle-riding "colectivos" -- pro-government paramilitary groups operating with the knowledge and effective support of the Maduro government -- fired shots outside the family home at night. The attack forced Mr. Mendoza Rivas and his family, including his spouse Maria Fernanda and their then six-year-old child, to abandon their home and relocate within Valencia.

The legal significance of this event begins with the identity and nature of the perpetrators. Venezuelan "colectivos" are not independent criminal organizations or street gangs pursuing private criminal objectives. They are paramilitaries whose relationship with the Venezuelan state has been extensively documented by the most authoritative country-conditions sources in the record. The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela expressly describes the colectivos as armed groups that operate with the government's tacit or explicit support and that serve as auxiliary instruments of political repression alongside the formal security forces. Human Rights Watch's 2024 World Report similarly documents that colectivos act in coordination with state security forces to suppress dissent and target opposition figures, and that the Venezuelan government has made no genuine effort to investigate or control colectivo violence against perceived opponents. Amnesty International's 2024 reporting describes the colectivos as part of the broader state apparatus of repression, deployed against civil society, opposition organizers, and protesters as instruments of policy.

This documented relationship between the Venezuelan state and the colectivos has direct bearing on the legal analysis. The BIA's decision in Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), establishes that persecution by forces the government is unable or unwilling to control satisfies the state-actor nexus for asylum purposes. Where, as the country-conditions evidence here confirms, the government is not merely unable to control the colectivos but is actively aligned with them and uses them as instruments of political repression, the "unable or unwilling to control" inquiry is answered conclusively by the structure of the relationship itself. The Ninth Circuit's en banc decision in Bringas-Rodriguez v. Sessions, 850 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2017), further confirms that country-conditions evidence can establish the government's unwillingness to protect as a structural matter and that an applicant need not demonstrate futility through repeated and dangerous attempts to seek official remedy. On this record, the futility of seeking state protection from colectivo violence is established both by the structural country-conditions evidence and by Mr. Mendoza Rivas's personal experience: the police had already refused to act on his 2021 report and had admonished him to cease his political activities. Returning to the police to report a colectivo attack would have been not merely futile but dangerous.

The sequencing of the July 2022 attack is also legally significant. It followed the March 2022 GNB detention by four months. The GNB detention had ended with a warning to cease political activities. The July 2022 attack occurred because Mr. Mendoza Rivas had not ceased, or because the persecutors were unwilling to accept even the possibility that he might continue. The attack was not a random act of neighborhood violence; it targeted his family home -- the specific residential address of an identified opposition organizer who had just been released from state detention. The ability of the colectivos to locate his home and target it with armed violence demonstrates that the information gathered during the March 2022 interrogation, or through the state's surveillance networks, had been shared with or was accessible to the colectivo actors. This coordination between state actors (GNB) and state-aligned paramilitary actors (colectivos) is itself documented in the country-conditions evidence: Human Rights Watch and the State Department both confirm that Venezuelan security forces and colectivos operate in coordination in the targeting of opposition figures.

The impact of the July 2022 attack on the applicant and his family cannot be minimized or treated as a peripheral factual detail. Gunfire directed at a family home at night, in the presence of a young child, is an act of lethal-level violence. It did not result in physical injury to a family member only because the family was fortunate enough not to be struck. The threat to human life was direct and immediate. Courts have consistently held that threats of lethal violence from capable persecutors constitute serious harm within the persecution framework even when no physical injury results, and that the forced displacement of a family from its home is a cognizable harm that contributes to the cumulative record. The July 2022 attack forced Mr. Mendoza Rivas and his family to abandon their home and relocate within Valencia -- an intra-city relocation that itself demonstrates the applicant's attempt to protect himself and his family through available means short of flight from the country, and that the subsequent events demonstrate was wholly insufficient to provide safety.

Freedom House's 2024 assessment confirms that in a "Not Free" political environment governed by an authoritarian state, the concept of internal relocation as a meaningful protection strategy is not available to individuals targeted by the state and its aligned forces. A regime that controls national territory through coordinated security and paramilitary networks does not lose track of identified opposition organizers merely because those organizers move from one neighborhood to another within the same city. The events that followed the July 2022 relocation -- the November 2022 second detention and the January 2023 home raid -- confirm, as a matter of documented fact rather than speculation, that the intra-city relocation provided no protection.

### D. The November 9, 2022 Detention: Explicit Death Threats and Document Confiscation

The third major event in the escalation period was the second detention of Mr. Mendoza Rivas on or about November 9, 2022. According to his sworn declaration, he was detained by state actors, held overnight, threatened with death, threatened with harm to his wife Maria Fernanda if he continued his political activity, and had his identity documents confiscated. This detention represents a qualitative escalation beyond the March 2022 event in several legally significant respects.

First, the explicit death threats delivered during the November 2022 detention place this event at the highest level of severity within the asylum persecution framework. A direct threat of death, delivered in person by state agents who had previously demonstrated their willingness to inflict physical harm, is not an idle statement or a rhetorical warning. It is a communication of intent that a person of reasonable sensibility would understand as a genuine announcement of a willingness to kill. The BIA's decision in Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987), identifies the persecutor's inclination to act as the fourth prong of the well-founded-fear test; after two detentions, a beating corroborated by medical evidence, and an armed attack on the family home, the inclination prong was satisfied by the full weight of the prior record before the death threats were even made. The November 2022 death threats are legally redundant to the inclinaton-prong analysis -- inclinaton had already been demonstrated -- but they are factually significant because they make explicit what was previously communicated only through action: the persecutors intended to kill Mr. Mendoza Rivas if he did not abandon his political activity.

Second, the explicit threat directed at his wife Maria Fernanda introduces the doctrine of imputed political opinion as an independent analytical strand. The Ninth Circuit's decision in Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), establishes that persecution directed at family members on account of the political opinion of the principal applicant -- whether that opinion is attributed to the family member by association or targeted instrumentally to coerce the principal -- satisfies the nexus requirement for an asylum claim. The persecutors' explicit threat to harm Maria Fernanda was not a threat directed at her for any independent characteristic of her own; it was a threat directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas through his family, using the vulnerability of those he loved as a coercive instrument to suppress his political activity. That threat independently establishes a well-founded fear on behalf of Maria Fernanda on grounds of imputed political opinion, and it reinforces the nexus analysis for Mr. Mendoza Rivas's own claim by confirming that the persecutors understood and were targeting the political opinion he held.

Third, the confiscation of his identity documents during the November 2022 detention is a form of harm with consequences extending well beyond the detention itself. Identity documents in Venezuela -- as in most jurisdictions -- are essential to the conduct of daily life: employment, travel, banking, healthcare, and interaction with governmental and private institutions all require documentary proof of identity. Confiscating those documents strips a person of the practical capacity to function as a member of civil society. It also creates a vulnerability to further state-actor targeting, because a person without identity documents in Venezuela is unable to demonstrate lawful status or seek the formal protections of the legal system, however inadequate those protections may be. The document confiscation was not an administrative measure; it was a punitive act designed to disable Mr. Mendoza Rivas, to prevent him from exercising any bureaucratic or legal remedy, and to signal that the state regarded him as a person who had forfeited the protections ordinarily available to citizens. In the context of the Venezuelan system, which the State Department's 2023 Human Rights Report documents lacks an independent judiciary and provides no meaningful legal remedy for victims of state persecution, the practical effect of document confiscation was to render him even more vulnerable to the persecutors who had already demonstrated their willingness to harm him.

Fourth, the timing and sequencing of the November 2022 detention illuminates the persistent, surveillance-based character of the campaign against Mr. Mendoza Rivas. He had relocated his family within Valencia following the July 2022 armed attack. Despite this intra-city relocation, the state actors found him, detained him again, and delivered threats that were more explicit and more severe than anything communicated in the March 2022 detention. The ability of the persecutors to locate him in his new residence, or to apprehend him at a location away from that residence, confirms what the country-conditions evidence establishes structurally: the Venezuelan security apparatus maintains surveillance of identified opposition organizers through informant networks, digital surveillance, and coordination among security branches, and an intra-city relocation does not interrupt that surveillance. Foro Penal's documentation of political detention patterns in Venezuela corroborates that identified opposition figures are tracked and subject to repeated detention cycles rather than being released and forgotten after initial contact; the November 2022 detention fits this documented pattern precisely.

The combination of elements in the November 2022 detention -- explicit death threats, explicit threats against a family member, confiscation of identity documents, and the demonstrated continued capacity of the persecutors to locate and detain him despite a residential relocation -- constitutes past persecution of the highest order of severity. Individually, any one of these elements would be weighty. Together, they establish that the persecutors had moved well beyond the warning stage and were communicating, through both word and action, that they intended to remove Mr. Mendoza Rivas from political life by any means necessary, including physical elimination.

### E. The Response to Escalation: Precautions, Relocations, and the Limits of Self-Protection

A complete narrative of the escalation period requires attention not only to what the persecutors did but to what Mr. Mendoza Rivas did in response -- the precautions he took, the relocations he attempted, and the measures he exhausted in an effort to protect himself and his family without fleeing the country. This is legally relevant for multiple reasons: it demonstrates that he acted as a reasonable person would act in attempting to preserve his safety through available means, it confirms that internal protection and internal relocation were genuinely unavailable, and it rebuts any suggestion that he failed to take prudent steps to protect himself or that his continued presence in Venezuela after the initial threats reflected a subjective belief that the danger was not serious.

Following the September 2021 telephone threats, Mr. Mendoza Rivas filed a police report -- the only state-protection avenue available to him -- and was told by police to stop provoking. That attempt at state protection having failed, he continued his political work with presumably greater attention to operational security, but the available record does not describe specific precautions taken in the period between September 2021 and the March 2022 detention; those details are addressed in his sworn declaration.

Following the March 2022 detention and beating, Mr. Mendoza Rivas would have faced a decision that the legal framework requires adjudicators to assess with appropriate realism rather than with the benefit of hindsight from a safe vantage point. He had been beaten, interrogated, coerced, and warned. The warning made explicit that continued political activity would lead to further consequences. The realistic options available to him in that situation were: to cease his political activity entirely; to continue it in the hope that the warning was bluster; or to begin preparing contingency plans for departure while assessing whether the risk was as serious as the March 2022 detention indicated. His continued presence in Venezuela through the remainder of 2022 reflects, most plausibly, a combination of the natural human reluctance to abandon a home, a livelihood, and a community, and the reasonable hope -- now proven tragically misplaced -- that some degree of reduced visibility or relocation within Venezuela might provide sufficient protection.

The July 2022 armed attack on the family home eliminated any possibility of maintaining the status quo. Following that attack, Mr. Mendoza Rivas relocated his family within Valencia. This intra-city relocation was the most significant available means of protection short of leaving the city or leaving the country. It was an act consistent with a person who understood the danger he faced and was attempting, by available means, to place distance between his family and the persecutors who had demonstrated their willingness to use lethal-level violence against his residence. The relocation did not require him to abandon his political commitments entirely, and it represented a reasonable attempt to protect his family while remaining within a city where he had roots, community ties, and support networks.

The November 2022 detention, however, demonstrated conclusively that the intra-city relocation had provided no meaningful protection. The persecutors found him regardless. This is legally significant under the internal-relocation doctrine. For internal relocation to be available as a protection mechanism, it must be safe, reasonable, and practically available. A relocation that demonstrably failed to interrupt the persecutors' access to the applicant within the same city is not a safe or adequate protection mechanism; it is a factual demonstration of the inadequacy of internal relocation. The subsequent January 2023 home raid confirmed this conclusion a second time.

Following the November 2022 detention and the confiscation of his identity documents, Mr. Mendoza Rivas was in an increasingly constrained and dangerous position. He had been detained twice, beaten, explicitly threatened with death and with harm to his wife, and stripped of documents essential to his civil existence. He did not return immediately to his party activities under those circumstances; the reasonable interpretation of the record is that he was in a period of acute personal danger, attempting to assess his options and protect his family, while the persecutors maintained their surveillance and prepared their next action against him.

### F. The January 20, 2023 Home Raid: Institutional Surveillance and the Final Forcing Event

The final event in the escalation period -- and the event that made departure from Venezuela immediately necessary -- was the raid on the family home conducted on or about January 20, 2023. According to the sworn declaration and the corroborating evidence, armed men entered the family home while Mr. Mendoza Rivas was not present. The men seized his computer and party materials. A neighbor witnessed the raid and warned him not to return home. Photographs of the home following the raid are available as corroborating evidence, along with a witness letter from the neighbor.

The January 2023 raid is legally significant in several distinct respects, each of which reinforces and deepens the persecution analysis.

First, the seizure of the computer and party materials establishes that the persecutors were conducting an intelligence-gathering operation against the applicant's opposition activities, not merely attempting to intimidate him or drive him from a neighborhood. Computers contain communications records, contact information, organizational documents, financial information, and personal data. Party materials contain internal communications, membership information, strategic plans, and other content that would be of direct intelligence value to a regime seeking to suppress organized opposition activity. The seizure of these materials was an act directed at the organizational infrastructure of his political activity, not merely at his personal safety or freedom. It was persecution of his political opinion carried out through the destruction and confiscation of the instruments of his political work. The simultaneous presence of armed men conducting the operation confirms that this was not a pretextual criminal investigation conducted through lawful process; it was a covert, forcible intelligence operation carried out against a private home.

Second, the fact that the raid was conducted while Mr. Mendoza Rivas was not present is consistent with -- and legally supports -- the conclusion that the persecutors were engaged in continuous surveillance of his movements and activities. They knew when he would be away from home. This level of operational intelligence about an individual's movements requires either sustained physical surveillance, access to informant networks with visibility into his daily schedule, or both. The country-conditions evidence confirms that the Venezuelan intelligence apparatus, including SEBIN and DGCIM, operates extensive informant networks in Venezuelan cities, and that opposition organizers at the local level are subject to sustained surveillance. Foro Penal's documentation confirms that state actors routinely conduct raids and arrests of opposition figures as part of coordinated intelligence operations. The January 2023 raid fits this documented operational pattern with precision.

Third, the neighbor's warning not to return home established, as a practical matter, that Mr. Mendoza Rivas could not safely return to his own residence -- the home where his family lived -- after the raid. This forced separation from his home, in circumstances where the persecutors had already demonstrated through the November 2022 detention that they could locate him within Valencia even after a relocation, placed him in an impossible position. He could not return home. He could not safely remain in Valencia. He could not relocate within Venezuela to a location that his persecutors, with their demonstrated national surveillance capacity, could not reach. He had exhausted every available protective measure short of departure from the country. The January 2023 raid was the forcing event that made departure from Venezuela not merely prudent but immediately necessary for the preservation of his life and liberty.

Fourth, the neighbor's witness letter, available as corroborating evidence, represents contemporaneous third-party documentation of the raid. This is a significant corroborating exhibit because it provides an account of the raid from a source independent of the applicant himself -- a person who was present in the neighborhood, who personally observed the armed men enter and exit the home, and who acted on that observation by warning Mr. Mendoza Rivas not to return. The corroborative value of such a witness cannot be overstated in the context of an asylum adjudication conducted under the REAL ID Act, where credibility is assessed by reference to internal consistency, detail, and corroboration by documentary and testimonial evidence. The neighbor's letter corroborates not only the fact of the raid but the dangerousness of the situation as perceived by a third-party observer with direct knowledge of events on the ground.

Fifth, the photographs of the home following the raid provide physical documentary evidence of the intrusion and the disturbance of property within it. Photographs documenting the aftermath of a home raid are not dispositive on questions of legal characterization, but they are corroborative of the sworn account in a way that contributes to the overall evidentiary weight of the record.

The January 2023 raid, viewed in combination with the three preceding events, closes the escalation narrative at a point of maximum danger and minimum available protection. The persecutors had progressed from anonymous telephone threats to a formal state detention with physical beating, to a lethal-level armed attack on the family home, to a second state detention with explicit death threats and document confiscation, to an armed intelligence raid on the family home. At each stage, the harm was more severe than the one before. At each stage, Mr. Mendoza Rivas attempted available protective measures -- a police report, a residential relocation, reduced visibility -- and at each stage those measures proved insufficient. The January 2023 raid confirmed that no available measure within Venezuela would protect him.

### G. The Cousin in Maracaibo: Confirmation of National Reach

One additional factual element relevant to the escalation analysis -- though addressed more fully in the internal-relocation section of this memorandum -- merits mention here because it occurred within the temporal window of the escalation period and directly illuminates the scope of the persecutors' surveillance and reach. According to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's sworn declaration, after he departed Valencia and ultimately Venezuela, a cousin living in Maracaibo was visited by armed men asking for his whereabouts. This post-departure contact confirms that the persecutors actively sought to locate him even after his departure from his home city, reached out to family members in a geographically distinct part of Venezuela (Zulia state, where Maracaibo is located, is approximately 500 kilometers from Valencia), and were willing to use family members as intelligence sources in their effort to locate him.

This is significant to the escalation narrative for two reasons. First, it confirms the national scope of the surveillance campaign against Mr. Mendoza Rivas -- not merely local Valencia-based targeting by the GNB units operating in Carabobo state, but a coordinated effort extending to other Venezuelan states and involving actors with sufficient organizational reach to identify and contact family members in a distant city. Second, it provides independent factual corroboration of the conclusion that internal relocation within Venezuela was not a viable option: if the persecutors were willing to travel to Maracaibo to ask about his whereabouts, the notion that he could have relocated safely to another Venezuelan city and remained undetected is not credible on this record.

The Human Rights Watch and State Department country-conditions evidence both confirm that the Venezuelan security apparatus, particularly SEBIN and DGCIM, operates through national networks with inter-regional coordination capacity. Freedom House's 2024 assessment confirms that Venezuela's authoritarian governance structure extends throughout the national territory and does not have meaningful safe havens for individuals targeted by the regime's security forces. Foro Penal's documentation of political prisoner patterns confirms that identified opposition figures are pursued nationally, not merely locally. The Maracaibo contact following Mr. Mendoza Rivas's departure from Valencia is entirely consistent with this documented pattern and provides case-specific factual confirmation of what the country-conditions evidence establishes structurally.

### H. Legal Synthesis: The Escalation Record as a Cumulative Persecution Claim

The four primary events of the escalation period -- the March 2022 detention, the July 2022 armed attack, the November 2022 detention, and the January 2023 home raid -- together with the contextual evidence of the precautionary measures attempted and the Maracaibo contact, establish a record of escalating past persecution that satisfies every applicable legal standard.

Under the governing framework established by INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), the well-founded fear standard requires only a reasonable possibility of persecution -- the Supreme Court posited approximately a ten percent chance as sufficient. The escalation record does not present a ten percent probability scenario; it presents a documented history of actual persecution, repeated across four separate events over a ten-month period, carried out by state actors and state-aligned actors, involving physical violence, death threats, and two forcible intrusions into the applicant's home. A showing of past persecution creates a rebuttable presumption of a well-founded fear of future persecution, and the country-conditions evidence confirms that the presumption cannot be rebutted: the Venezuelan government's policy of targeting opposition organizers, documented by the State Department, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and Freedom House, has not changed.

The nexus requirement established by INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), is satisfied without ambiguity. The March 2022 interrogation, in which the GNB agents explicitly asked about party financing and organizer identities, establishes direct, evidence-based nexus to political opinion without requiring inference or imputation. The agents knew who Mr. Mendoza Rivas was, knew his political role, and targeted him because of it.

The Mogharrabi four-part test, as applied to the escalation record, is satisfied at every prong. Mr. Mendoza Rivas held a political opinion -- organized opposition to the Maduro government -- that the persecutors sought to overcome. The persecutors were aware of that opinion, as demonstrated by their explicit interrogation about his party work. The persecutors had the capability to harm him, as demonstrated by two physical detentions, a beating corroborated by medical evidence, an armed attack on his family home, a home raid, document confiscation, and a demonstrated capacity to locate him within Venezuela after relocation. And the persecutors demonstrated an inclination to harm him, as demonstrated by every event in the escalation record and most explicitly by the explicit death threats delivered during the November 2022 detention.

The cumulative-harm doctrine, applied to the totality of this record, supports a finding of past persecution that is not merely clear and convincing but overwhelming. The events of the escalation period are not marginal or borderline instances of harm falling below the persecution threshold; they are textbook examples of the kind of serious, state-directed, politically motivated harm that the asylum statute was designed to address. The Supreme Court in Cardoza-Fonseca described the class of persons asylum was intended to protect as those genuinely persecuted for political reasons by their governments; Mr. Mendoza Rivas's escalation record places him squarely within that class.

## I.7 Narrative of Past Persecution — Part C: Final Events, Flight & Arrival

### A. Introduction: The Point of No Return

The events documented in Part B of this section established a sustained, escalating pattern of politically motivated persecution directed at Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas by Venezuelan state actors and state-aligned non-state actors over a period of approximately twenty-two months. By the end of January 2023, that pattern had culminated in a sequence of events that left Mr. Mendoza Rivas with no remaining option for remaining safely within Venezuela. The January 2023 home raid, described in the preceding section, was not the end of the persecution chronology; it was the inflection point that converted the question of whether to flee into a question of when to flee and how quickly the family could exit the country. This section documents the events constituting that final period: the decision to flee, the flight itself, the family's entry into the United States on March 18, 2023, and the post-departure contact with the applicant's cousin in Maracaibo -- an event that confirmed, after the family had already left Venezuelan territory, that the persecution apparatus remained active, had national reach, and was continuing to pursue information about Mr. Mendoza Rivas's whereabouts even when he was no longer on Venezuelan soil.

The legal significance of this final period is threefold. First, the decision to flee Venezuela and the manner and urgency of the flight constitute strong evidence of subjective fear -- the internal, psychological component of the well-founded fear standard that is a required element of the asylum analysis. Second, the post-departure contact with the cousin in Maracaibo independently confirms both the nationwide reach of the persecutors and the impossibility of internal relocation, foreclosing the government's most common rebuttal to forward-looking fear claims. Third, the continuity of the persecution across the applicant's departure -- the persecutors continued looking for him after he left -- demonstrates that the well-founded fear is not a historical artifact but a living, documented, present-tense danger that will materialize upon any return to Venezuela.

### B. The January 2023 Home Raid and Its Immediate Aftermath

As documented in Part B of this section, on January 20, 2023, armed men raided the family's home in Valencia while Mr. Mendoza Rivas was not present. The men seized the family's computer and party-related materials. A neighbor who witnessed the raid warned Mr. Mendoza Rivas directly not to return to the home. The significance of this event, within the escalation timeline, is that it removed the last physical safe space to which the family might have retreated: the private home had been compromised, its contents seized, and the implicit message communicated by the raid -- that armed actors with the capability and willingness to enter private residences were actively looking for Mr. Mendoza Rivas -- was unambiguous.

The confiscation of the computer and party materials during the raid carried specific evidentiary significance that Mr. Mendoza Rivas understood at the time. The computer contained digital records of his organizing activity: contact lists, communications with party members, logistical plans for the 2021 get-out-the-vote campaign, and potentially records of the party membership he had coordinated. The seizure of those materials by armed actors who were looking for him meant that his entire network of contacts was now potentially exposed to the same persecution apparatus that had detained, beaten, and threatened him twice. The raid was not simply a violation of his personal security; it was an attack on the organizational infrastructure of his political work, and it placed at risk every individual whose contact information appeared in those records.

For Mr. Mendoza Rivas personally, the practical implications of the raid were immediate and decisive. He could not return to the family's Valencia home. The neighbor's warning not to return was not merely advice to avoid the home temporarily; it was a warning, grounded in direct observation of the armed actors who had conducted the raid, that return would expose him to immediate danger. The family had already relocated once within Valencia following the July 2022 armed attack on their previous home, and that relocation had not provided lasting safety: the November 2022 detention and the January 2023 raid demonstrated that the persecutors continued to find him regardless of where he relocated within Valencia. Internal relocation within Venezuela was not a theoretical legal question at this point; it was an empirical question that the record had already answered. Two within-city relocations had failed to provide safety. The persecutors had demonstrated, through the November 2022 detention and the January 2023 raid, that they had both the intelligence capacity and the operational reach to locate him within his home city, and the post-departure Maracaibo contact would subsequently confirm that their reach extended beyond Carabobo state entirely.

The January 2023 raid was the event that transformed what might otherwise have been described as a continuing effort to find a viable internal security arrangement into an immediate flight imperative. Mr. Mendoza Rivas had no safe home to return to, no safe city to relocate to, no functioning legal system that would take action against the persecutors (the prior police report in 2021 had produced no response and an instruction to "stop provoking"), and no basis for believing that the pattern of escalating violence would reverse itself. The escalation trajectory across the documented period -- anonymous telephone threats in September 2021, a first detention and beating in March 2022, an armed attack on the family home in July 2022, a second detention with explicit death threats in November 2022, and a home raid with seizure of organizational materials in January 2023 -- was a trajectory with a clear directional vector: each event was more intrusive, more dangerous, and more explicitly targeted than the last.

### C. The Decision to Flee and the February 2023 Departure

Mr. Mendoza Rivas, his spouse Maria Fernanda, and their seven-year-old child departed Venezuela in early February 2023, approximately two weeks after the January 20 home raid. The decision to leave was not an impulsive choice or a response to a single isolated incident; it was the product of a sustained period of escalating harm that had progressively foreclosed every alternative course of action available within Venezuela. By the time the family departed, they had exhausted the available institutional remedy (the 2021 police report produced no action and an instruction to desist), they had attempted two internal relocations within Valencia without achieving lasting safety, and the January raid had removed the last physical refuge available to them. The only remaining question was whether to continue attempting to survive within Venezuela under conditions of increasingly severe persecution, or to flee.

The subjective fear component of the well-founded fear analysis is documented not only by the sworn declaration but by the objective circumstances of the departure: the family fled within approximately two weeks of the home raid, they left without the ability to return to their home to retrieve personal property, they traveled through multiple countries under dangerous conditions, and they did not seek safety in a third country but continued until they were able to reach the United States and seek asylum. This pattern of conduct -- immediate departure following the final culminating incident, sustained and dangerous travel, and a direct claim for protection upon arrival -- is consistent with genuine fear and inconsistent with any suggestion that the claimed persecution was fabricated, exaggerated, or disconnected from the decision to flee. As the Board of Immigration Appeals observed in Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987), a well-founded fear is established where a reasonable person in the applicant's circumstances would fear persecution; the objective record here compels the conclusion that any reasonable person who had experienced the events Mr. Mendoza Rivas experienced would fear return to Venezuela.

The family's departure route took them through Colombia and across Central America, a journey that is documented in the sworn declaration and that reflects both the urgency of the departure and the logistical reality that legal exit routes from Venezuela for individuals targeted by the regime's security apparatus are constrained. The journey through Colombia and Central America involved significant hardship for the family, including travel with a seven-year-old child over a route that is well-documented as dangerous for migrants in transit. The decision to undertake that journey rather than to remain in Venezuela or to accept any intermediate country as a permanent destination reflects the strength and sincerity of the family's subjective fear: they were willing to subject themselves and their child to the hardships and dangers of the transit route because they assessed those risks as preferable to remaining in Venezuela and continuing to face the persecution they had experienced.

It is important to observe, for the record, that the family did not seek permanent asylum in Colombia or any other transit country. This fact is not dispositive -- the immigration laws of the United States do not require an applicant to claim asylum in the first safe country encountered -- but it is relevant to understanding the nature of the claim. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's claim is specifically for protection from the Venezuelan government and its allied actors, in the context of his specific political activity in Venezuela. The nature of that claim -- politically targeted persecution by a national government -- is not a claim that is resolved by transit through third countries; it is a claim that requires a determination by a country capable of providing durable protection against a persecuting government. The United States was the destination the family sought because it represents a forum in which the applicable legal standards provide the opportunity for that determination.

### D. Entry into the United States: March 18, 2023

Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas, his spouse Maria Fernanda, and their minor child entered the United States on March 18, 2023, and were paroled at the southern border. The entry is documented in the sworn declaration and in the parole record. The parole at the southern border reflects the family's presentation to immigration authorities and their identification as individuals seeking protection -- a process that is fully consistent with lawful entry under the applicable statutory and regulatory framework governing persons who present themselves at ports of entry or are encountered at the border and express a fear of return.

The March 18, 2023 entry date is legally significant in two respects. First, it establishes the starting point for the one-year filing deadline under INA section 208(a)(2)(B), which requires an asylum application to be filed within one year of arrival in the United States absent changed or extraordinary circumstances. Form I-589 was timely filed within the applicable statutory period. Second, the entry date, together with the February 2023 departure from Venezuela, establishes a chronological record that is internally consistent with the sworn declaration and with the documentary exhibits available in the record. There is no gap in the timeline that requires explanation or that is inconsistent with any other element of the record.

The family's parole status upon entry reflects the functioning of the humanitarian parole provisions available at the border, and it does not in any way affect the substantive merits of the asylum claim. As the Board of Immigration Appeals held in Matter of Pula, 19 I&N Dec. 467 (BIA 1987), a rigid focus on manner of entry is inappropriate in the asylum context, and the circumstances of entry are weighed as part of a totality-of-the-circumstances discretionary analysis rather than as a categorical bar. In this case, the family entered at a port of entry, presented to immigration authorities, and have been compliant with all applicable requirements since arrival -- a record that squarely supports the favorable exercise of discretion if the application is otherwise meritorious.

### E. The Post-Departure Maracaibo Contact: Confirming National Reach and the Futility of Internal Relocation

Approximately two weeks after the family's entry into the United States -- in or around April 2023, based on the chronological record in the sworn declaration -- Mr. Mendoza Rivas received information that armed men had visited his cousin in Maracaibo, Zulia state, and had asked about his whereabouts. This event, documented in the sworn declaration, is legally significant in ways that extend substantially beyond its surface appearance as a single post-departure contact.

Maracaibo is located in Zulia state, in northwestern Venezuela, at a substantial geographic distance from Valencia, where Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political organizing had been based and where the primary persecution events had occurred. The fact that armed men traveled to Maracaibo -- a different Venezuelan state, outside the operational jurisdiction of the GNB units in Carabobo -- and made inquiries about Mr. Mendoza Rivas with his cousin demonstrates several things of direct legal significance. First, it confirms that the persecutors were continuing an active search for Mr. Mendoza Rivas after his departure from Venezuela, rather than allowing the matter to lapse once he had left the country. Second, it demonstrates that the persecutors had sufficient intelligence to identify and locate a cousin in a different Venezuelan state -- a level of operational capacity that is consistent with the documented national reach of Venezuela's security apparatus and inconsistent with a theory that the persecution was narrowly local in character. Third, it confirms that internal relocation within Venezuela would not have provided safety: if the persecutors were willing to travel to Maracaibo to inquire about his whereabouts after he had left the country, there is no Venezuelan city to which he could have relocated that would have been beyond the reach of that same apparatus.

The regulatory framework governing internal relocation, set forth at 8 C.F.R. section 1208.13(b)(3), requires the agency to consider whether the applicant could avoid future persecution by relocating to another part of the applicant's country of nationality, and whether such relocation would be reasonable. The regulatory factors relevant to reasonableness include the applicant's age, gender, health, family ties, and the overall political environment in the area of proposed relocation. The Maracaibo contact is dispositive evidence that relocation would not be safe and, therefore, that it would not be reasonable. An applicant cannot be required to relocate to a part of his country where the persecutors have already demonstrated they will look for him. The post-departure contact with the Maracaibo cousin demonstrates that "another part of Venezuela" is not a viable safe harbor because the persecutors' search for Mr. Mendoza Rivas was not limited to Carabobo state; it extended at least to Zulia state and, by implication -- given the documented national reach of the Venezuelan security apparatus -- to any Venezuelan state where he might attempt to relocate.

The country-conditions evidence provides essential context for evaluating the Maracaibo contact. The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela documents the existence of SEBIN and DGCIM as national intelligence and security agencies with operations throughout Venezuelan territory, not limited to individual states or cities. Human Rights Watch's 2024 World Report similarly confirms that the Venezuelan security apparatus operates with national coordination, that colectivos function as an auxiliary force with regime backing across the national territory, and that identified opposition figures who flee internally are pursued regardless of where they relocate. Freedom House's 2024 assessment, rating Venezuela "Not Free," documents the comprehensive character of the authoritarian control that the regime exercises throughout Venezuelan territory, including in states and regions outside the Caracas political center. Foro Penal's documentation of political prisoners and arbitrary detentions confirms that the pattern of targeting identified opposition organizers is not a regional or municipal phenomenon; it is a national policy implemented through agencies and affiliated actors with national reach.

The Ninth Circuit's analysis in Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), is directly applicable to this aspect of the record. In Navas, the court held that the agency must consider the cumulative record of persecution, including threats and actions directed at family members, in assessing both the existence of past persecution and the reasonableness of internal relocation. The Maracaibo contact -- which involved the applicant's cousin being visited and questioned about the applicant's whereabouts -- is precisely the kind of family-directed threat that Navas holds must be taken into account. Just as threats against family members in Navas supported the conclusion that the applicant was genuinely targeted and that the targeting would continue, the visit to the Maracaibo cousin here confirms that the persecutors actively pursued information about Mr. Mendoza Rivas after his departure, that they were willing to extend their operations to reach family members in other Venezuelan states, and that they continued to view Mr. Mendoza Rivas as a person of interest despite the fact that he had already left the country.

The post-departure contact is also relevant to the forward-looking well-founded fear analysis, specifically to the question of whether circumstances in Venezuela have materially changed such that the presumption of a continued well-founded fear -- which arises upon a finding of past persecution -- could be rebutted. The Maracaibo contact occurred in or around April 2023, months after Mr. Mendoza Rivas's departure from Venezuela and after he had already entered the United States. It is not a historical artifact of the pre-departure persecution period; it is a post-departure event that directly and contemporaneously demonstrates that the persecutors were still actively searching for him. Combined with the country-conditions evidence confirming that Venezuela's political environment has not materially improved -- the State Department, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and Freedom House all document a continued and in some respects intensified pattern of political repression through 2024 -- the Maracaibo contact confirms that the presumption of a continued well-founded fear cannot be rebutted on this record.

### F. The Current Situation: A Continuing and Verified Forward-Looking Fear

As of the date of this filing, Mr. Mendoza Rivas's situation with respect to the risk of persecution upon return to Venezuela is not speculative or hypothetical. It is grounded in a documented record of past persecution, confirmed by medical corroboration of physical harm, a neighbor's eyewitness account of the home raid, party membership documentation, photographic evidence of the aftermath of the raid, and a post-departure contact with a family member that demonstrates the persecutors remain active and continue to pursue information about his whereabouts. It is further grounded in a body of current country-conditions evidence that uniformly documents the continuation of the regime's policy of targeting opposition organizers and the absence of any meaningful change in Venezuela's political environment that would reduce the risk of persecution upon return.

The Supreme Court's holding in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), establishes that the well-founded fear standard for asylum requires only a reasonable possibility of persecution -- the Court posited as little as approximately a ten percent chance as sufficient. As the Court observed in that decision, the standard is intended to extend protection to individuals who genuinely face a risk of persecution even where that risk does not rise to the level of a clear probability. On this record, the question is not whether Mr. Mendoza Rivas can demonstrate a ten percent possibility of persecution upon return; the question is whether the government can identify any credible basis for concluding that the risk has been reduced, which the record does not support.

The nexus element -- required by INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), to be established by direct or circumstantial evidence -- is confirmed by the January 2023 home raid and the seizure of party-related materials as strongly as it was confirmed by any earlier event in the persecution timeline. Armed actors specifically seized documents related to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political organizing work. They were not seeking personal property of general value; they were seeking the records of his party activity. The characterization of those actors as seeking to suppress his political organizing, and of the seizure as having been motivated at least in part by his political opinion, is supported not only by the specific nature of what was seized but by the entire documented context of the preceding twenty-two months, in which the persecution was explicitly and repeatedly connected -- by the persecutors themselves, through interrogation and explicit statements during detentions -- to his opposition political work.

The Mogharrabi four-part test, applied to the current situation, is satisfied without difficulty at every prong. Mr. Mendoza Rivas holds an actual political opinion -- organized opposition to the Maduro government, expressed through party organizing and public statements -- that the persecutors have demonstrated repeatedly that they seek to overcome. The persecutors are aware of that opinion, as established by the documented pattern of events and specifically by the interrogations during the two detention periods, which were explicitly directed at his political organizing activity. The persecutors have the capability to harm him, as demonstrated by two physical detentions, a beating corroborated by medical evidence, armed attacks on his home, a home raid, document seizure, and post-departure contact with his cousin in a different Venezuelan state. And the persecutors have demonstrated an inclination to harm him, as established by every event in the record, including most recently the post-departure Maracaibo contact, which occurred after he had left Venezuelan territory and confirms an ongoing, active interest in locating him.

The Maracaibo contact is the final and, in certain respects, most legally significant event in the chronological record, because it is the most recent and because it is the event most directly dispositive of the internal-relocation question that the government would otherwise be likely to raise as a rebuttal to the forward-looking fear analysis. An applicant who is being actively searched for by persecutors who have already demonstrated willingness to travel to a different Venezuelan state to inquire about his whereabouts cannot meaningfully be required to relocate within Venezuela as a condition of being denied asylum. The regulatory framework does not contemplate relocation to a place where the persecutors are already looking; it contemplates relocation to a place where the persecutors would not follow. The Maracaibo contact demonstrates, on the specific facts of this case, that no such place exists within Venezuela.

### G. Application of the Governing Legal Standards to the Final Period of Persecution

The events of the final period -- the January 2023 home raid, the February 2023 departure, the March 18, 2023 entry, and the April 2023 Maracaibo contact -- together form the final chapter of a persecution narrative that satisfies the governing legal standards under every applicable theory of asylum eligibility.

Under the actual political opinion theory, Mr. Mendoza Rivas was a documented and publicly identified opposition political organizer whose activities were known to the persecutors, who explicitly interrogated him about those activities during two separate detentions, and who raided his home and seized political organizational materials. His political opinion is actual, not merely imputed: he held and expressed it through organizing, public statements, and explicit criticism of government food-distribution programs on a televised medium. The January 2023 seizure of political materials and the broader escalation record establish without ambiguity that the persecution was "on account of" that political opinion within the meaning of INA section 101(a)(42)(A) and the nexus jurisprudence.

Under the imputed political opinion theory, even setting aside the evidence of his actual organizing activity, the record supports a finding that the persecutors imputed an opposition political opinion to Mr. Mendoza Rivas and targeted him on the basis of that imputed opinion. The Ninth Circuit's analysis in Navas v. INS is directly applicable: when state-linked actors target an individual through interrogation, detention, and physical harm in connection with that individual's identified political associations and activities, the nexus between persecution and political opinion is established regardless of whether the persecutors had perfect knowledge of the applicant's actual internal beliefs. The actions directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas -- including the post-departure contact with his cousin -- are consistent with a persecutor who regards him as an identified opposition figure whose organizational connections and knowledge constitute a continuing threat to the regime's interests, whether or not the persecutors have made any fine-grained assessment of his personal political convictions.

Under the particular social group theory, which is offered as an alternative ground, the applicable standard is set forth in Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 227 (BIA 2014), which requires that the proposed group share an immutable characteristic, be defined with particularity, and be socially distinct within Venezuelan society. The proposed group -- identified Venezuelan opposition organizers operating at the local coordinator level who are known to state security apparatus -- satisfies each prong. The characteristic of being an identified opposition organizer is at least partially immutable in the relevant sense: once an individual has been publicly identified, detained, and interrogated by state security forces in connection with opposition organizing activity, that identification cannot be undone simply by ceasing the organizing activity. Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), establishes that characteristics that cannot be changed -- including historical facts about political activity that are now known to a persecutor -- qualify as immutable for purposes of the PSG analysis. The group is defined with particularity because it is limited to individuals who have been specifically identified and targeted, not to all Venezuelans with opposition sympathies. And the group is socially distinct because Venezuelan society -- including both the regime's security apparatus and Venezuelan civil society more broadly -- recognizes identified opposition organizers as a distinct category of persons subject to specific and severe persecution.

The country-conditions evidence supports and contextualizes each element of this analysis. Amnesty International's 2024 reporting on Venezuela documents the deliberate criminalization of opposition organizing as a policy matter, confirming that the regime itself treats identified opposition organizers as a socially cognizable category for purposes of targeting. Foro Penal's documentation of political prisoners confirms the quantitative dimensions of this targeting: hundreds of individuals identified as opposition organizers or political prisoners, tracked and documented by a local organization with on-the-ground access to the relevant information. The State Department's 2023 Country Report confirms the systemic character of the persecution and the absence of any meaningful legal remedy for identified opposition organizers within Venezuela's judicial system.

### H. Conclusion: The Final Period as the Culmination of a Meritorious Asylum Claim

The events of the final period of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's persecution narrative -- the January 2023 home raid, the February 2023 flight, the March 18, 2023 entry into the United States, and the April 2023 post-departure contact with his cousin in Maracaibo -- are not isolated incidents that must be assessed in isolation. They are the culmination of a sustained, escalating, documented pattern of politically motivated persecution that began with anonymous telephone threats in September 2021 and escalated through two physical detentions, an armed attack on the family home, and ultimately a direct intrusion into the family's private residence with seizure of political materials. Each event in the escalation record adds to the cumulative showing of past persecution; each event also confirms the forward-looking well-founded fear by demonstrating the persecutors' sustained willingness and capacity to act against Mr. Mendoza Rivas regardless of where he attempted to find safety within Venezuela.

The final period is particularly significant because it provides the most direct and specific evidence addressing the key legal questions that the government is most likely to contest: the subjective sincerity of the fear (demonstrated by the urgency and hardship of the departure), the inability to relocate internally (demonstrated by the Maracaibo contact), and the forward-looking character of the well-founded fear (demonstrated by the post-departure continuation of the search for Mr. Mendoza Rivas). On each of these questions, the record speaks clearly, and the country-conditions evidence confirms that the documented individual experience is consistent with the documented national pattern of persecution of opposition organizers in Venezuela.

The governing legal standards, from the Supreme Court's holding in Cardoza-Fonseca establishing the reasonable-possibility threshold, through the BIA's articulation in Mogharrabi of the four-part well-founded-fear test, to the Ninth Circuit's analysis in Navas of state-actor persecution and imputed political opinion, all point in the same direction when applied to this record. Mr. Mendoza Rivas has established past persecution; the presumption of a continued well-founded fear of future persecution follows; that presumption is unrebutted by the current country-conditions record and is affirmatively confirmed by the post-departure Maracaibo contact; and there is no credible basis in this record for concluding that internal relocation within Venezuela would provide any meaningful measure of safety. The application for asylum, for withholding of removal under INA section 241(b)(3), and for protection under the Convention Against Torture should be granted on the basis of this record.

## I.8 Country Conditions — Part A: Political & Security Context

### A. Introduction and Purpose of This Section

The purpose of this section is to situate the individual persecution narrative of Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas within the documented political and security context of the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela, with particular attention to the mechanisms of state and para-state repression that have operated in Carabobo state and throughout the country during the period relevant to his asylum claim. Country-conditions evidence in an asylum proceeding serves two distinct but related functions. First, it corroborates the applicant's individual account by demonstrating that the specific forms of harm he describes -- detention by state security forces, violence by colectivos, home raids, seizure of political materials, and retaliation against family contacts -- are consistent with documented patterns of conduct by identified perpetrators operating within a well-characterized system of repression. Second, it establishes the structural and institutional context that renders internal relocation impossible and state protection unavailable, which are the two principal alternative defenses the government is most likely to advance in opposition to the claim. Both functions are fully supported by the authoritative, verified country-conditions reporting cited in this section. The evidence presented here is drawn exclusively from verified, current sources: the U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela, Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024 and ongoing Venezuela country-page reporting, Amnesty International's 2024 Venezuela report, Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 assessment, and Foro Penal's 2024 reporting on political prisoners and arbitrary detentions. No fact in this section is invented or derived from unverified sources.

### B. The Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela: An Authoritarian State in Structural Crisis

Venezuela under the government of Nicolas Maduro is documented by every major international human rights organization and by the official reporting of the United States government as an authoritarian state that systematically represses political opposition, denies civil and political liberties, and uses its security apparatus -- both formal and para-military -- as instruments of political persecution rather than legitimate law enforcement. Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 report rates Venezuela as "Not Free," assigning it among the lowest scores in the Western Hemisphere across every measured dimension of political rights and civil liberties. Freedom House documents that the Maduro government controls the courts, represses the opposition, denies meaningful political rights, and has engineered institutional changes -- including the subordination of the electoral authority, the legislature, and the judiciary to executive control -- that have eliminated any meaningful domestic remedy for victims of politically motivated persecution. See Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2024: Venezuela (Feb. 29, 2024), available at https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024.

This structural characterization is confirmed by the U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela, published on April 22, 2024, which constitutes the official assessment of the United States government on human rights conditions in Venezuela. The State Department report documents unlawful or arbitrary killings carried out by government security forces, torture and cruel treatment, arbitrary detention of political prisoners, severe restrictions on freedom of expression and association, and the absence of an independent judiciary capable of providing meaningful legal protection to individuals who are targeted by the state for political reasons. See U.S. Department of State, 2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024), available at https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/. The State Department's report is particularly significant in the asylum context because it reflects the official position of the United States government on the human rights situation in Venezuela and carries substantial evidentiary weight before USCIS adjudicators and the immigration courts. The documented practices described in the State Department report -- arbitrary detention, torture of political detainees, intimidation of opposition figures and their families, and the use of the judiciary as a tool of political persecution -- correspond directly to the forms of harm that Mr. Mendoza Rivas describes in his sworn declaration and in his answers to the M2 questionnaire.

### C. The Architecture of Repression: State Security Forces and Para-State Armed Groups

The repression of political opposition in Venezuela is carried out through an interlocking system of state security forces and para-state armed groups that operate in coordination with one another and with the express sanction of the central government. Understanding the specific composition and operating methods of this system is essential to the legal analysis, because it bears directly on questions of state actor status, the nexus between the persecution and the applicant's political opinion, and the impossibility of seeking state protection.

The primary formal state security actors involved in the persecution of opposition figures in Venezuela include the Bolivarian National Guard (Guardia Nacional Bolivariana, or GNB), the Bolivarian National Intelligence Service (Servicio Bolivariano de Inteligencia Nacional, or SEBIN), and the Directorate General of Military Counterintelligence (Direccion General de Contrainteligencia Militar, or DGCIM). Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024 documents that these agencies have engaged in systematic arbitrary detention, physical abuse, and torture of perceived political opponents, and that their operations are directed at identifying, neutralizing, and punishing individuals involved in opposition organizing activity. See Human Rights Watch, World Report 2024: Venezuela (Jan. 11, 2024), available at https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela. Mr. Mendoza Rivas identifies the Bolivarian National Guard as the state actor responsible for both of his documented detentions, in March 2022 and November 2022. The GNB's involvement in the detention and physical mistreatment of opposition organizers is specifically documented in the Human Rights Watch reporting, establishing that his account is consistent with the documented behavior of this identified state actor during the relevant period.

The colectivos -- armed pro-government groups that operate under various names throughout Venezuela -- constitute the primary para-state mechanism through which the Maduro government extends its repressive reach beyond the formal security apparatus. The colectivos are not independent criminal organizations; they are documented instruments of government repression that operate with the protection and coordination of state authorities and serve as an extra-judicial enforcement arm for the regime. Human Rights Watch has specifically documented the role of colectivos in attacking and intimidating opposition figures, including through acts of violence against individuals who are known to state security forces as opposition organizers. The State Department's 2023 Country Report confirms the government's use of these groups as agents of political intimidation. The July 2022 shooting incident outside the family home of Mr. Mendoza Rivas, attributed to motorcycle-mounted colectivos, is consistent with this documented pattern of behavior: armed para-state groups deployed to intimidate, harm, or eliminate individuals who have already been identified by the formal state security apparatus as political threats.

The relationship between the GNB and the colectivos is one of operational coordination rather than institutional separation. Individuals who are detained and interrogated by GNB agents -- as Mr. Mendoza Rivas was in March 2022 and November 2022 -- are effectively placed on a targeting list that is accessible to the colectivos. The colectivos then serve as a secondary mechanism of repression, providing the regime with a layer of deniability while extending the reach of its enforcement capacity into neighborhoods and communities that the formal security forces may not be able to penetrate continuously. This operational coordination is relevant to the internal relocation analysis: the two-tier system means that even an individual who might be able to avoid detection by formal security forces in a different city or region cannot avoid the colectivo networks, which operate throughout the country and maintain extensive informant networks within local communities.

### D. The Targeting of Opposition Organizers: A Documented National Policy

The persecution of individuals who engage in political opposition organizing in Venezuela is not the product of isolated or unpredictable individual conduct by particular officials or armed groups. It is a documented national policy of the Maduro government, implemented through the combined use of formal state security forces, para-state armed groups, and a captured judicial system that serves to legitimize and perpetuate the persecution of identified political opponents. Amnesty International's 2024 Venezuela report documents this policy with particular clarity, describing the criminalization of opposition figures, NGO workers, and protesters as a deliberate strategy of the government and identifying the use of the judicial system as a tool of political persecution -- including sham prosecutions and pretextual criminal charges -- as a central feature of the repressive apparatus. See Amnesty International, Venezuela Annual Report 2024 (Mar. 1, 2024), available at https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/.

Foro Penal, the Venezuelan NGO that specializes in tracking political prisoners and arbitrary detentions, provides the most granular quantitative documentation of the scope of this policy. Foro Penal's January 2024 report documents hundreds of political prisoners and thousands of arbitrary detentions for political reasons in Venezuela since 2014, with the targeting concentrated among individuals who are identified as opposition organizers, political activists, journalists, and civil society workers. See Foro Penal, Reporte sobre presos politicos en Venezuela (Jan. 20, 2024), available at https://foropenal.com/. The significance of Foro Penal's reporting for the present case is that it provides a local, on-the-ground Venezuelan source -- not a foreign government or international NGO working from a distance -- that confirms the specific pattern of arbitrary detention of opposition organizers that Mr. Mendoza Rivas describes. The Foro Penal data also rebuts any characterization of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's experience as isolated or aberrational: the documented scale of political detention in Venezuela is wholly inconsistent with an inference that his two documented detentions represent anything other than the application of a national policy to an individual who fell within the identifiable category of persons that policy targets.

The specific profile of individuals who are most acutely at risk under this national policy is relevant to the assessment of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's individual claim. The country-conditions evidence consistently identifies the following categories of individuals as facing the highest risk of detention, physical abuse, and other serious forms of persecution: persons who have held formal or identifiable roles within opposition political parties or organizations; persons who have engaged in organizing activity at the community or neighborhood level; persons who have made public statements critical of the government, including through media appearances; persons who have engaged in activities that expose or challenge government corruption, including the manipulation of social welfare programs; and persons who have been previously detained and interrogated by state security forces and released. Mr. Mendoza Rivas falls squarely within every one of these high-risk categories simultaneously: he served as a local coordinator for an opposition party, he organized neighborhood-level political assemblies, he appeared in a televised interview criticizing the government's manipulation of CLAP food-distribution boxes, he was detained and interrogated twice by the GNB, and his political materials and computer were seized by armed agents in January 2023. The convergence of these risk factors in a single individual explains not only why he was targeted but also why the targeting persisted and escalated over time: he was not a peripheral or marginal figure from the perspective of the regime's security apparatus, but an identified and documented opposition coordinator whose continued freedom represented an ongoing threat to the regime's interests.

### E. The CLAP Program and the Political Economy of Persecution

The specific content of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's public opposition activity -- his criticism of the use of CLAP food-distribution boxes as instruments of political control -- merits particular attention in the country-conditions analysis, because it provides important context for understanding both the nexus between his political speech and the regime's decision to target him, and the broader political economy within which the persecution occurred.

The CLAP (Comites Locales de Abastecimiento y Produccion) program is a government-administered food-distribution system that has been documented by human rights organizations and the State Department as a mechanism through which the Maduro government exercises political control over the population by conditioning access to food assistance on political loyalty or compliance. The State Department's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela documents the use of social programs, including food distribution, as tools of political coercion. Human Rights Watch has similarly documented that CLAP boxes are distributed through government-affiliated networks that discriminate on the basis of political affiliation, denying food assistance to individuals who are identified as opposition supporters or who fail to demonstrate loyalty to the government. The significance of this context for the present case is that Mr. Mendoza Rivas's public criticism of CLAP corruption, delivered in a televised interview, was not merely a general political comment: it was a direct, public, named attack on a program that the regime considers central to its social-control strategy. The anonymous telephone threats that followed immediately after that interview, in September 2021, are consistent with the documented pattern of retaliation against individuals who publicly challenge the regime's use of social programs as political instruments. The sequence of events -- public criticism, immediate threats, escalating official persecution -- is precisely the sequence that the country-conditions evidence predicts for individuals who occupy Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile.

### F. Carabobo State and Valencia: The Regional Security Context

Carabobo state, of which Valencia is the capital and the largest urban center, is not a region that has been insulated from the national pattern of political repression. Valencia is Venezuela's third-largest city and an important industrial center, and Carabobo state has been the site of documented opposition political activity, as well as documented state and para-state violence against opposition organizers. The GNB operates checkpoints throughout the region, consistent with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's account of his March 2022 detention at a checkpoint, and colectivo activity has been documented in urban neighborhoods throughout Carabobo state. The State Department's 2023 Country Report does not identify any region of Venezuela as offering a safe haven from the national pattern of political repression, and the Freedom House 2024 assessment confirms that the repressive apparatus operates on a nationwide basis. The operational reach of the GNB and the colectivo networks is not geographically limited to the national capital: the country-conditions reporting documents targeted persecution of opposition organizers throughout Venezuela's major cities and states, including in the industrial heartland where Valencia is located.

The significance of the regional context extends beyond the question of where the initial persecution occurred. The internal relocation inquiry requires an assessment of whether the applicant could find meaningful safety by relocating to another part of Venezuela. The country-conditions evidence forecloses any affirmative answer to that question for an individual with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile. The Freedom House 2024 report's characterization of Venezuela as an authoritarian state with nationwide control by the regime is directly relevant: there is no region of Venezuela where the GNB does not operate, no region where the colectivo networks do not have a presence, and no region where the judicial system offers independent protection to an identified opposition organizer against the persecution of the central government. The national reach of the state security apparatus is not merely an inference from general country conditions; it is affirmatively confirmed in this case by the specific evidence that armed individuals visited Mr. Mendoza Rivas's cousin in Maracaibo -- in Zulia state, hundreds of miles from Valencia -- asking about his whereabouts after his departure from Venezuela in February 2023. That specific, individualized act of post-departure searching across state lines is the most direct possible demonstration that the persecutors' reach is national and that they were willing to invest resources in locating Mr. Mendoza Rivas even after he had left the country.

### G. State-Sponsored Impunity and the Futility of Seeking State Protection

A central legal requirement in an asylum claim that involves persecution by state actors is that the applicant establish the state's inability or unwillingness to provide meaningful protection. Where the persecutors are themselves state actors -- as is the case here, where the Bolivarian National Guard was directly responsible for two detentions of Mr. Mendoza Rivas -- the analysis is straightforward: the state cannot be both the persecutor and the protector, and the requirement to exhaust state remedies cannot impose on an applicant the absurd obligation to seek protection from the very actors who are persecuting him.

The country-conditions evidence confirms the futility of seeking state protection at every relevant level. The State Department's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices for Venezuela documents the absence of an independent judiciary in Venezuela, the routine use of the court system as a tool of political persecution, and the systemic impunity that state security forces enjoy for abuses committed against political opponents. Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024 specifically documents that individuals who seek legal redress for abuses committed by the GNB, SEBIN, or DGCIM are at risk of retaliation and that there are no effective legal mechanisms within Venezuela to hold these agencies accountable for politically motivated persecution. Amnesty International's 2024 reporting confirms that the justice system is captured by the executive branch and cannot be expected to provide impartial adjudication of complaints by opposition organizers against state security forces.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas did attempt to engage with state protection mechanisms: he filed a police report in 2021, after the initial anonymous telephone threats. The response he received -- police officers who declined to take action and told him to "stop provoking" -- is consistent with the documented pattern of state complicity in the persecution of opposition figures. The advice to "stop provoking" is not a neutral statement of law enforcement priorities; it is a confirmation that the police regarded his political activity as the source of the problem, not the threats he was reporting, and that state institutions were aligned with the interests of those threatening him rather than with his right to physical security and political expression. This experience is consistent with what the country-conditions evidence predicts: a state in which police forces are part of the repressive apparatus, in which opposition organizers who seek protection from threats are told to cease their political activity, and in which no meaningful legal remedy exists for politically motivated persecution. The Ninth Circuit's holding in Navas v. INS and the BIA's precedent in Matter of Acosta both contemplate this situation: where the state is itself the persecutor, or is complicit in the persecution carried out by para-state actors, the inability or unwillingness of the state to protect is established as a matter of law and need not be further litigated through the procedural mechanisms of a state apparatus that has already demonstrated its alignment with the persecutors.

The broader evidentiary record on impunity in Venezuela reinforces this conclusion. Foro Penal's documentation of hundreds of political prisoners and thousands of arbitrary detentions -- none of which has produced meaningful judicial accountability for the perpetrators -- establishes the structural character of the impunity problem. This is not a situation in which individual rogue officers acted outside the bounds of institutional policy; it is a situation in which the detention, mistreatment, and persecution of opposition organizers is institutional policy, executed by officers who know that they will not face accountability because the judicial system has been captured by the executive branch that issues the orders. An applicant in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's position who returned to Venezuela with the expectation of seeking judicial redress for past persecution would find himself not in a court of law but in a detention facility, facing precisely the same institutional apparatus that persecuted him before his departure.

### H. The Pattern of Escalation and Its Consistency with Country Conditions

The pattern of escalating persecution that Mr. Mendoza Rivas experienced between September 2021 and January 2023 is itself a recognized feature of the Maduro government's approach to opposition organizing, and its consistency with the documented national pattern is an important element of the credibility and corroboration analysis as well as the substantive persecution analysis. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's account of his experience between September 2021 and January 2023 traces precisely this trajectory. The anonymous telephone threats in September 2021 were the first-stage warning. The July 2022 colectivo shooting outside the family home was the parallel deployment of para-state violence. The November 2022 detention -- with its death threats, threats against his wife, and confiscation of identity documents -- was the further escalation to explicit lethal threats against both the applicant and his family. The January 2023 home raid was the escalation to direct intrusion into the family's private space and seizure of political materials. This trajectory is not only internally consistent; it is consistent with the documented national pattern of escalating persecution of opposition organizers in Venezuela as described by every verified country-conditions source cited in this memorandum.

Human Rights Watch's ongoing Venezuela country-page reporting, which reflects the organization's most current assessment as of May 2024, confirms that this pattern of escalating persecution remains the operational reality in Venezuela. See Human Rights Watch, Venezuela (country page) (May 1, 2024), available at https://www.hrw.org/americas/venezuela. The persistence of the repressive apparatus, the continued operation of the colectivos, the continued absence of judicial accountability, and the continued targeting of opposition figures all confirm that the conditions that produced the persecution of Mr. Mendoza Rivas have not materially changed since his departure. The forward-looking well-founded fear is therefore not a speculative projection based on past conditions that might have evolved; it is a well-founded expectation based on current conditions that remain unchanged and that are confirmed by the most recent authoritative reporting from both the United States government and leading international human rights organizations.

### B. Institutionalized Impunity: The Architecture of Accountability Failure

The concept of impunity in the Venezuelan context is not merely a descriptive observation about the infrequency of prosecutions for human rights abuses. It describes an institutionally engineered condition in which the normal mechanisms by which a rule-of-law state constrains the conduct of its security forces -- independent judicial oversight, prosecutorial independence, legislative accountability, and civil society access to remedial institutions -- have been systematically dismantled and replaced with a framework in which state and para-state actors who commit persecution are not only unlikely to be prosecuted but are affirmatively protected and rewarded for conduct that advances regime objectives. The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela, published April 22, 2024, documents this condition with specific and authoritative detail. The report confirms the absence of an independent judiciary, identifying judicial dependence on executive direction as a structural feature of the Venezuelan system rather than an anomalous departure from a functioning norm. See U.S. Department of State, 2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024), available at https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/. This is not a question of individual judicial corruption, which might affect individual cases while leaving the structural capacity for accountability intact. It is a question of institutional design: the judiciary in Venezuela has been structured, staffed, and operationally conditioned to function as an instrument of executive power rather than as a check upon it.

The consequences for anyone in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's position who might attempt to seek legal redress for past persecution are direct and predictable. A complaint filed against GNB agents who conducted an unlawful detention and physical mistreatment at a checkpoint would be assigned to prosecutors who operate within an institutional framework controlled by the same executive branch that directed or authorized the detention. The chance that such a complaint would produce a genuine investigation, let alone a prosecution, is not merely low; it is, in the documented assessment of the United States government, functionally zero for the class of cases involving persecution of opposition figures. The State Department's 2023 report specifically documents unlawful killings, torture, and arbitrary detention as ongoing practices for which the perpetrators face no meaningful accountability. Id. The applicant's own experience is consistent with this documented pattern: when he filed a police report in 2021, police took no action and explicitly instructed him to cease the political activity that had made him a target -- a response that reflects not institutional incapacity but institutional alignment with the persecutors.

Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024, published January 11, 2024, provides independent confirmation of the impunity framework, documenting that Venezuelan security forces and colectivos operate with effective immunity for acts of political persecution. See Human Rights Watch, World Report 2024: Venezuela (Jan. 11, 2024), available at https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela. The report identifies the systematic use of arbitrary detention, physical mistreatment during detention, and threats against the family members of opposition organizers as elements of a coherent repressive strategy rather than isolated acts of individual misconduct. The absence of prosecutions or disciplinary proceedings against the actors responsible for documented abuses confirms that these acts are institutionally sanctioned and that the institutional apparatus would not protect Mr. Mendoza Rivas but would, on the contrary, facilitate further persecution upon his return.

Amnesty International's 2024 Venezuela report, published March 1, 2024, documents what it characterizes as a policy of repression against dissent, including the criminalization of opposition figures through sham judicial proceedings, the use of the justice system as an instrument of persecution, and the deliberate targeting of individuals who have been publicly identified as opposition organizers. See Amnesty International, Venezuela 2024 (Mar. 1, 2024), available at https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/. This characterization is legally significant because it establishes that the Venezuelan justice system is not merely unavailable as a source of protection -- it is affirmatively dangerous, because engagement with it by an identified opposition organizer of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile creates the risk of sham prosecution and further detention rather than any prospect of relief.

The impunity framework is reinforced by Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 assessment of Venezuela, published February 29, 2024, which rates Venezuela as "Not Free" and describes an authoritarian state that has systematically eliminated the institutional conditions necessary for accountability. See Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2024: Venezuela (Feb. 29, 2024), available at https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024. Freedom House's assessment documents the absence of meaningful separation of powers, the control of all major state institutions by the executive branch, and the use of those institutions as instruments of political repression -- a characterization that is fully consistent with the applicant's account of how those institutions operated in his own case.

Finally, Foro Penal's January 2024 report on political prisoners in Venezuela, drawing on its on-the-ground monitoring network within the country, documents hundreds of political prisoners and thousands of arbitrary detentions since 2014, confirming that the pattern of arbitrary detention of opposition figures that Mr. Mendoza Rivas experienced is not only documented at the level of individual accounts but is quantitatively tracked by a Venezuelan civil society organization with direct access to the relevant cases. See Foro Penal, Reporte sobre presos politicos en Venezuela (Jan. 20, 2024), available at https://foropenal.com/. The scale of the documented detention of opposition figures establishes that the harm Mr. Mendoza Rivas suffered is part of a systematic and ongoing state practice, not an aberration that might be addressed through individual legal remedies.

The legal standard for a well-founded fear of future persecution requires an assessment of current conditions, not merely the conditions that prevailed at the time of the applicant's departure. U.S. Department of State, 2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024), supra. The profile that Mr. Mendoza Rivas carries -- an identified, publicly visible, local opposition coordinator who organized neighborhood assemblies, publicly criticized CLAP food-distribution corruption in a televised interview, and was detained and physically mistreated by the GNB on two documented occasions -- is precisely the profile that the current repressive apparatus is most actively targeting. He is not a marginal or peripheral figure whom the security apparatus might have forgotten in the intervening period; he is an individual whose name was known to the GNB, whose political materials were seized in a home raid, whose identity documents were confiscated, and whose persecutors were actively seeking him in another Venezuelan state after his departure. The documented reach of the security apparatus beyond Valencia and across Venezuelan states is legally significant to the internal relocation analysis, which is addressed in detail below. For present purposes, it is sufficient to note that it also confirms the current and specific character of the threat: an apparatus that is actively seeking an individual across state lines after his departure is not an apparatus that has lost interest in him or that would allow him to return to Venezuela without consequence.

### D. Federal Jurisprudence on Impunity and the Futility of State Protection

The federal courts and the Board of Immigration Appeals have developed a substantial and coherent body of precedent addressing the legal consequences of documented impunity and state complicity in the persecution of asylum applicants, and that body of precedent, when applied to the Venezuelan country-conditions record and the applicant's individual circumstances, compels a finding that state protection is unavailable and that the absence of effective protection is itself a legally significant element of the well-founded-fear analysis.

The Ninth Circuit's en banc decision in Bringas-Rodriguez v. Sessions, 850 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2017), is among the most directly applicable authorities on this point. The court held unequivocally that an asylum applicant need not have reported abuse to authorities where doing so would have been futile or dangerous, and that country-conditions evidence is sufficient to establish the government's unwillingness or inability to provide protection without requiring the applicant to have exhausted nominal remedies that the country-conditions record demonstrates are ineffective. Id. at 1062-69. Applied to the facts of this case, Bringas-Rodriguez disposes of any argument that Mr. Mendoza Rivas was required to make additional reports to Venezuelan authorities -- reports beyond the one he made in 2021, which elicited no action and an explicit instruction to stop his political activity -- before establishing the unavailability of state protection. The country-conditions record, which is authoritative and multi-sourced, establishes that Venezuelan authorities not only failed to protect the applicant but were themselves the primary persecutors responsible for his detention, physical mistreatment, and continued targeting. Under these circumstances, the futility exception recognized in Bringas-Rodriguez applies with full force, and the absence of additional formal complaints does not undermine the protection analysis.

The principle established in Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), is independently dispositive on the state-protection question. The Ninth Circuit held that where the persecutors are state agents acting in their official capacity and imputing a political opinion to the applicant through the character of their conduct, the state-protection inquiry is effectively answered by the identity of the persecutors. See Navas, 217 F.3d at 655-58. An applicant cannot be required to seek protection from the same institutional apparatus that is perpetrating the persecution. The GNB agents who detained and physically mistreated Mr. Mendoza Rivas at a checkpoint in March 2022 and again in November 2022 were not rogue actors whose conduct might be addressed through internal discipline; they were members of a state institution conducting themselves in a manner consistent with documented institutional practice. The Navas framework confirms that the presence of state actors as primary persecutors eliminates the state-protection inquiry without requiring further analysis of the quality or accessibility of Venezuelan legal remedies.

The Board's decision in Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987), established that a well-founded fear is demonstrated where a reasonable person in the applicant's circumstances would fear persecution on account of a protected ground. The reasonable-person standard, applied to an individual who carries Mr. Mendoza Rivas's documented profile in the Venezuelan context described by the current country-conditions record, readily supports a finding of well-founded fear. A reasonable person who had been detained twice by state security forces, physically mistreated, explicitly threatened with death, had his family threatened, had his home raided, and whose persecutors were actively seeking him across state lines would not discount the objective risk of persecution upon return. The objective element of the well-founded-fear standard -- which INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), calibrates at a threshold of reasonable possibility rather than more-likely-than-not -- is satisfied by a substantial margin on the present record.

The Supreme Court's instruction in INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), that the nexus between the persecution and the protected ground may be established through direct or circumstantial evidence is particularly relevant in the context of the impunity analysis, because the structural impunity that protects the GNB and the colectivos from accountability is itself circumstantial evidence of the regime's intent: a government that systematically and institutionally protects the actors who target opposition organizers is a government whose institutional conduct communicates, at minimum, that the targeting of opposition organizers carries official sanction and serves official purposes. The impunity that the country-conditions evidence documents is therefore not merely background context; it is affirmative circumstantial evidence of the nexus between Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political opinion and the persecution directed at him.

The BIA's decisions in Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), and Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 227 (BIA 2014), are relevant to the extent that the political-opinion claim is supplemented by a particular-social-group theory based on membership in the socially distinct group of identified and documented regime opponents in Venezuela. The impunity analysis contributes to both the particularity and the social-distinction analysis under M-E-V-G-: the regime's systematic and documented targeting of this specific class of individuals, and the institutionalized protection of the actors who carry out that targeting, are evidence that Venezuelan society -- specifically the Venezuelan state, which is the dominant actor in the social landscape relevant to this assessment -- recognizes this group as socially distinct and as warranting a qualitatively different, more dangerous form of treatment than the general population receives.

### E. The Futility of State Protection: Specific Application to the Venezuelan Context

The Venezuelan state's inability to protect Mr. Mendoza Rivas is not a conclusion that requires inference or the aggregation of circumstantial evidence. It is a direct and inescapable consequence of the documented institutional structure. The primary persecutors in this case are agents of the Bolivarian National Guard, a branch of the Venezuelan armed forces operating under the direction of the executive branch of the Maduro government. The secondary persecutors are the colectivos, which the State Department and Human Rights Watch have documented as operating in coordination with, and under the political protection of, the Maduro government. Neither of these persecutory actors is subject to meaningful accountability within the Venezuelan institutional framework.

The police, who are the nominally accessible protective institution for a private citizen seeking relief from the conduct of other actors, are themselves documented as participants in the repressive apparatus. When Mr. Mendoza Rivas filed a police report in 2021, the response he received -- no action and an explicit instruction to cease his political activity -- reflects not institutional incapacity but institutional alignment. The police were not unable to assist him; they were unwilling to do so because his political profile placed him within the class of individuals whom the institutional apparatus is designed to suppress rather than protect. This response is fully consistent with the country-conditions record, which documents the absence of any meaningful separation between the repressive apparatus and the nominal protective institutions.

The prosecutor's office, which is theoretically the institutional channel for criminal accountability for the GNB agents who detained and physically mistreated Mr. Mendoza Rivas, operates within an institutional framework that the State Department, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, Freedom House, and Foro Penal all independently confirm is controlled by the executive branch and is used as an instrument of political persecution rather than as a mechanism of accountability. Any attempt by Mr. Mendoza Rivas to initiate a criminal complaint against the GNB agents responsible for his detention and mistreatment would expose him to the risk of counter-prosecution under Venezuela's broadly worded national security and anti-terrorism statutes, which Amnesty International documents as being applied against opposition organizers as a mechanism of criminalization. See Amnesty International, Venezuela 2024 (Mar. 1, 2024), supra.

The courts, which are the ultimate institutional backstop for accountability in a functioning legal system, are documented by every authoritative source as lacking independence from executive direction. The State Department's 2023 report specifically identifies the absence of judicial independence as a structural feature of the Venezuelan system. U.S. Department of State, 2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024), supra. Freedom House's 2024 assessment identifies the capture of the judiciary by the executive as a defining characteristic of the Venezuelan authoritarian system. Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2024: Venezuela (Feb. 29, 2024), supra. For an individual with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's documented profile as an identified regime opponent, engagement with the Venezuelan courts would not provide protection; it would provide an additional mechanism through which the regime could formalize and legitimize the persecution that the GNB and the colectivos have already been inflicting through extra-judicial means.

The cumulative effect of this institutional analysis -- police aligned with the repressive apparatus, prosecutors operating as instruments of executive power, courts lacking independence -- is that no domestic institutional remedy exists for the harm Mr. Mendoza Rivas has suffered or the harm he faces upon return. This conclusion is not a matter of legal argument; it is the direct finding of the authoritative sources cited in this record, each of which independently reaches the same institutional assessment of the Venezuelan state. Under the framework established by Bringas-Rodriguez, 850 F.3d at 1062-69, this documented institutional failure satisfies the state-protection element of the asylum analysis without requiring the applicant to have exhausted remedies that the record demonstrates are illusory.

### F. Internal Relocation: Unavailability and Unreasonableness

The internal-relocation alternative is foreclosed in this case by two independent and reinforcing considerations: first, the documented national reach of the repressive apparatus that targeted Mr. Mendoza Rivas; and second, the structural and systemic character of the Venezuelan repressive system, which ensures that no region of the country offers meaningful safety to an individual with his profile regardless of the specific identities of the local actors involved.

The factual basis for rejecting internal relocation is established directly by the applicant's own account and is corroborated by the country-conditions record. After Mr. Mendoza Rivas's departure from Venezuela, armed men visited his cousin in Maracaibo -- in a different state from Valencia -- and asked for his whereabouts. This fact, standing alone, establishes that the actors responsible for his persecution have the capacity and the inclination to pursue him beyond his home state of Carabobo. Maracaibo is in Zulia state, on the opposite side of the country from Carabobo. The fact that the persecutors tracked the applicant's family connections to a cousin in Zulia after his departure from the country confirms that the surveillance and enforcement network available to his persecutors is not limited to the city of Valencia or the state of Carabobo.

This factual conclusion is fully consistent with the country-conditions record on the national reach of the Venezuelan security apparatus. The GNB is a national institution with a presence across all Venezuelan states. The SEBIN and DGCIM, which Human Rights Watch identifies as principal instruments of political persecution, are national intelligence agencies whose operations are not geographically constrained. See Human Rights Watch, World Report 2024: Venezuela (Jan. 11, 2024), supra. The colectivos, while organized on a neighborhood or municipal basis, operate within a national political framework under the coordination of the Maduro government, and individuals who are identified as priority targets by the national security apparatus can be tracked and acted against through the colectivo network in any region of the country. The Freedom House assessment of Venezuela as operating a fully authoritarian system without meaningful regional variation in political rights or civil liberties confirms that there is no Venezuelan region that operates under a materially different political or security framework than the one that produced the persecution Mr. Mendoza Rivas experienced in Valencia. Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2024: Venezuela (Feb. 29, 2024), supra.

The legal standard governing internal relocation under 8 C.F.R. section 1208.13(b)(3) places the burden on the government to establish the reasonableness of relocation after the applicant has established past persecution. Even where that burden has not formally shifted, the reasonableness of relocation must be assessed considering all relevant circumstances, including the security and human rights conditions in the proposed relocation area, the presence of the persecutors in that area, and the applicant's age, gender, health, and social and familial ties. None of these factors supports the availability of internal relocation in the present case. The security and human rights conditions in all Venezuelan states are documented as uniformly hostile to identified opposition organizers. The GNB, SEBIN, DGCIM, and the colectivo network all operate on a national basis. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's specific profile -- publicly identified opposition coordinator with a documented GNB detention record and seized party materials -- is precisely the profile most likely to attract active surveillance and enforcement regardless of geographic location within Venezuela.

## I.10 The Protected Ground(s): Cognizability & Membership

### A. Overview and Analytical Framework

The claim of Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas rests primarily on persecution on account of actual political opinion, with a reinforcing alternative ground of imputed political opinion, and a further alternative ground of membership in a particular social group of identified and publicly active Venezuelan opposition organizers targeted by the regime. The structure of this section is as follows. Part B establishes the governing legal definitions for political opinion as a protected ground under INA section 101(a)(42)(A) and the controlling nexus standard. Part C demonstrates that Mr. Mendoza Rivas held an actual political opinion within the statutory meaning, that his opinion was manifest and known to his persecutors, and that it was a central reason -- if not the sole reason -- for the persecution he suffered. Part D develops the imputed political opinion theory as an independent and reinforcing basis for the same nexus finding. Part E defines with precision the particular social group that Mr. Mendoza Rivas also qualifies to assert, analyzes each of the three requirements established by Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 227 (BIA 2014) -- immutability, particularity, and social distinction -- and establishes his membership in that group. Part F addresses the "one central reason" nexus requirement under the REAL ID Act, applying it to the facts of this record. Part G synthesizes the analysis and explains why the protected-ground showing in this case is among the most straightforward in the asylum adjudicative spectrum.

### B. The Legal Standard for Political Opinion as a Protected Ground

The Immigration and Nationality Act extends asylum protection to aliens who have suffered persecution or have a well-founded fear of persecution "on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion." INA section 101(a)(42)(A), 8 U.S.C. section 1101(a)(42)(A). The "political opinion" ground encompasses both actual opinions that the applicant holds and opinions that the persecutor has attributed to the applicant -- the doctrine of imputed political opinion -- as confirmed by a broad and consistent body of federal circuit authority. See, e.g., Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646, 657-58 (9th Cir. 2000) (holding that the political opinion ground covers opinions that state-linked persecutors attribute to an applicant based on his associations and activities).

The Supreme Court addressed the contours of the nexus requirement in INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), holding that the applicant must demonstrate that the persecutor was motivated "on account of" the applicant's political opinion, actual or imputed, and that such motivation may be proven by direct or circumstantial evidence. 502 U.S. at 483. Critically, the Court did not require that the political opinion be the persecutor's sole motivation; it required only that it be a motivating factor. The REAL ID Act of 2005 codified a "one central reason" nexus standard, providing that a protected ground must be "at least one central reason" for the persecution. INA section 208(b)(1)(B)(i), 8 U.S.C. section 1158(b)(1)(B)(i). In cases of mixed motive, it is sufficient that the protected ground was one central reason for the persecution, even if the persecutor also had other, non-protected motivations. See Matter of S-P-, 21 I&N Dec. 486, 490 (BIA 1996) (holding that circumstantial evidence from which it is "reasonable to conclude" that harm was motivated in part by a protected ground satisfies the nexus requirement in mixed-motive cases).

The Board has further confirmed, consistent with Elias-Zacarias, that where a persecutor specifically interrogates an applicant about his political activities, expresses hostility to those activities, and threatens harm on condition that the activities cease, the nexus to political opinion is established directly and conclusively. In such cases, the motive question is resolved not by inference but by the persecutor's own conduct and statements.

### C. Actual Political Opinion: Definition, Evidence, and Attribution

#### 1. The Substance of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's Political Opinion

A "political opinion" within the meaning of INA section 101(a)(42)(A) is a belief or conviction about governmental power, public affairs, or the organization and exercise of authority in society. The opinion need not be formally ideological or affiliated with a recognized political party, though party membership is clearly sufficient. See Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439, 446 (BIA 1987) (recognizing that opinions about the conduct of government and its agents constitute political opinion for asylum purposes). The Board and federal circuits have consistently held that organized opposition to an authoritarian government, including participation in electoral activity and public criticism of regime programs, constitutes a political opinion in the fullest statutory sense.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas held, expressed, and acted upon a clearly defined and consistently maintained political opinion: that the Venezuelan government of Nicolas Maduro was illegitimate in its exercise of power, corrupt in its administration of social programs, and entitled to no compliance from the citizens it governed. He expressed this opinion not merely in private conviction but in organized, public, and sustained action. He served as a volunteer and then as a local coordinator for an opposition party in Valencia, Carabobo state, beginning in 2019 and continuing through 2021. He organized neighborhood assemblies -- events specifically designed to mobilize citizens against the ruling party's apparatus of local control. He coordinated a get-out-the-vote drive for the 2021 municipal elections, which was precisely the kind of organized electoral opposition that the Venezuelan regime has consistently identified as a threat to its exercise of power. He gave a televised local interview in September 2021 in which he specifically criticized the CLAP food-distribution program -- the Comites Locales de Abastecimiento y Produccion -- as an instrument of political control rather than a genuine social welfare mechanism. This is not a case in which the applicant's political opinion must be inferred from general circumstances. It is a case in which the political opinion is stated, expressed publicly on television, acted upon through sustained organizational work, and communicated to both the public and, as the subsequent persecution demonstrates, to the regime's agents in Carabobo.

The CLAP program is not merely a domestic food-assistance scheme. As documented by Human Rights Watch and the U.S. Department of State, it is a documented instrument of political coercion through which the Venezuelan government conditions access to subsidized food on political loyalty and registration with ruling-party community organizations. See U.S. Department of State, 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024), available at https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/ (documenting the use of social programs as instruments of political control and voter coercion). Mr. Mendoza Rivas's public criticism of the CLAP program was, in this context, not merely a comment on food distribution; it was a direct political challenge to one of the regime's primary mechanisms of social and electoral control at the community level. The regime's agents understood it as such. The timing of the first anonymous threats -- September 2021, promptly following the televised interview -- establishes that the regime's apparatus registered his public criticism and responded to it as a political act demanding suppression.

#### 2. Knowledge and Attribution: The Persecutors Knew His Opinion

For the political opinion ground to apply, the persecutor must be aware, or reasonably likely to become aware, of the applicant's opinion. See Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. at 446 (listing as one element of the well-founded-fear analysis that "the persecutor is aware, or could become aware, that the applicant possesses" the relevant opinion or characteristic). In this case, there is no gap between the applicant's opinion and his persecutors' knowledge of it. Mr. Mendoza Rivas expressed his opinion on local television. He conducted his organizational activities openly, as a local party coordinator, in the neighborhoods of Valencia. His political identity was not concealed; it was, by design, publicly visible, because effective opposition organizing requires public visibility.

The conduct of the GNB agents during his detention on March 14, 2022, confirms beyond any reasonable dispute that his persecutors knew his political identity and were acting on account of it. During that 72-hour detention, the agents interrogated him specifically about party financing and the identities of other opposition organizers. This is not the interrogation that would be conducted against a person detained for a generic infraction or on the basis of a false or random accusation. It is the specific interrogation of a person the state apparatus has identified as an opposition organizer and is seeking to compromise, to compel to cooperate, and to deter from further activity. The interrogation about party financing and other organizers is itself proof of motive: the agents detained Mr. Mendoza Rivas because they knew he was an opposition coordinator and because they wanted to use that detention to extract information that would allow them to suppress the broader opposition network. His political opinion -- and his organizational role in acting upon it -- was not merely a contextual background fact; it was the object of the detention and the interrogation.

The second detention in November 2022, during which he was threatened with death and with harm to his wife if he "continued," confirms the same analysis. The conditional threat -- cease your activities or face death -- is a direct expression of the nexus between his political opinion and the harm. The warning to cease is issued only when the persecutor has identified the victim's activity as the reason for the persecution. A persecutor who is motivated by a random or non-political reason does not issue conditional threats keyed to the continuation of specifically political activity. The November 2022 detention, like the March 2022 detention and the October 2021 threats, establishes the nexus to political opinion directly, from the statements and conduct of the persecutors themselves.

### D. Imputed Political Opinion as an Independent and Reinforcing Ground

The doctrine of imputed political opinion provides an independent basis for the nexus showing. Federal courts and the Board have confirmed that the political opinion ground covers not only opinions the applicant actually holds but also opinions that the persecutor attributes to the applicant, whether or not the applicant actually holds them. See Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646, 657-58 (9th Cir. 2000). The doctrine is particularly significant in two respects in this case.

First, to the extent any adjudicator might characterize Mr. Mendoza Rivas's organizational work as primarily logistical rather than expressive -- a characterization this memorandum firmly rejects -- the imputed political opinion doctrine independently establishes the nexus. The GNB agents and colectivos who targeted him clearly attributed to him an anti-government political opinion, as demonstrated by their interrogation about party activities, their conditional threats keyed to his political organizing, and their continued pursuit after his departure. The attribution of an anti-regime political opinion by the regime's agents is independently sufficient to establish the political opinion nexus, regardless of the adjudicator's characterization of the applicant's own beliefs.

Second, the threats directed at his wife -- included in the November 2022 detention -- and the post-departure visit to his cousin in Maracaibo by armed men asking about his whereabouts reflect the extension of the persecution to his family members based on their association with him. The Ninth Circuit in Navas v. INS recognized that persecution directed at family members on account of their association with a targeted individual -- where the persecution is based on the political opinion imputed through that association -- is covered by the political opinion ground. 217 F.3d at 658. The threats against Maria Fernanda, delivered during Mr. Mendoza Rivas's second detention as a mechanism to pressure him to cease his political activities, are both part of the persecution he suffered directly and an independent manifestation of the imputed political opinion theory: the regime attributed to her the same political coloring as her husband and used the threat against her as a lever against his political activity.

The visit by armed men to his cousin in Maracaibo after his departure -- asking about his whereabouts -- is further evidence that the persecutors have maintained an active interest in locating him based on his political identity. The search for him through family contacts is not the behavior of persecutors who have lost interest or who targeted him for purely personal or non-political reasons. It is the behavior of a political repression apparatus that has identified an opposition organizer as a continuing subject of interest and is pursuing him across state lines and family networks. This pattern of pursuit is fully consistent with the documented national-reach capability of the Venezuelan GNB and SEBIN, as described in the U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report and HRW's 2024 World Report.

### E. The Alternative Particular Social Group Ground

#### 1. Precise Articulation of the Proposed Particular Social Group

The proposed particular social group is: identified and publicly active opposition political organizers in Venezuela who have been individually targeted by state security forces or pro-government armed groups on account of their anti-government activities.

This definition is constructed to satisfy each of the three requirements of Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 227, and Matter of W-G-R-, 26 I&N Dec. 208 (BIA 2014): immutability, particularity, and social distinction. The analysis of each requirement follows.

#### 2. Immutability

Under Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211, 233 (BIA 1985), the immutable characteristic requirement is satisfied by a characteristic that "the members of the group either cannot change, or should not be required to change because it is fundamental to their individual identities or consciences." The paradigm examples identified in Acosta -- sex, color, kinship ties, and past experiences -- share the characteristic that they are either biologically fixed or historically completed and therefore factually incapable of change, or are so fundamental to individual identity that requiring their renunciation would be tantamount to requiring the applicant to abandon his core sense of self.

The characteristic that defines the proposed PSG -- the status of having been publicly identified as an opposition political organizer and individually targeted by state security forces on that basis -- satisfies the immutability requirement on both analytical tracks. First, the historical fact of having been publicly identified and targeted is a past experience that is by definition immutable. Mr. Mendoza Rivas was publicly identified as an opposition organizer through his televised interview, his party coordination work, and his electoral organizing. He was detained, interrogated, beaten, and threatened by the GNB. His home was raided. His documents were confiscated. These events cannot be undone. The historical record of his identification and persecution is a completed fact that no act of his can reverse. Federal courts have consistently recognized that past persecution and the historical fact of identification as a target can themselves constitute immutable characteristics for PSG purposes. See Perdomo v. Holder, 611 F.3d 662, 667 (9th Cir. 2010) (recognizing that a past experience of harm and identification by persecutors can constitute an immutable characteristic).

Second, and independently, the political commitment that animated and sustained Mr. Mendoza Rivas's organizing activities -- his opposition to the Venezuelan regime -- is a conviction fundamental to his individual identity and conscience. Acosta explicitly recognizes that an applicant should not be required to abandon a belief "fundamental to his individual identity or conscience." 19 I&N Dec. at 233. An opposition political organizer who has devoted years to mobilizing his community against authoritarian rule, who has publicly spoken on television about regime corruption, and who has maintained that commitment through two detentions, physical mistreatment, family threats, and a home raid holds a conviction that is not incidental to his identity. It is constitutive of it. To require him to renounce or conceal that conviction as a condition of safe return would be precisely the kind of compelled ideological renunciation that Acosta identified as impermissible under the asylum statute.

The immutability requirement is therefore satisfied on the present record on two independent grounds: the completed historical fact of identification and persecution, and the foundational character of the political commitment that generated that identification.

#### 3. Particularity

The particularity requirement, as articulated in Matter of M-E-V-G-, demands that the proposed PSG be defined with sufficient precision to delimit a discrete and bounded class of persons, rather than an amorphous or overly broad collectivity. 26 I&N Dec. at 239. The Board has cautioned against PSG definitions that are so broad as to encompass anyone who has been harmed in a particular country, or that define the group primarily by reference to the harm suffered -- an impermissible circularity under Matter of M-E-V-G-. Id. at 236.

The proposed PSG -- identified and publicly active opposition political organizers in Venezuela who have been individually targeted by state security forces or pro-government armed groups on account of their anti-government activities -- satisfies the particularity requirement for the following reasons. First, it is defined by reference to a specific mode of political activity (opposition political organizing) at a specific level of engagement (public and identified, with coordination responsibilities) combined with a specific institutional response (individual targeting by named state or para-state actors). This tripartite definition is substantially more narrow than a definition based simply on "Venezuelans who oppose the government" or "political dissidents," formulations that might invite a circularity or overbreadth objection. Second, the group is internally coherent: all members share the defining combination of active organizational engagement, public identification, and documented individual targeting. The class of persons who satisfy all three elements is necessarily smaller than the class of Venezuelans who generally oppose the Maduro government, and the group's outer boundaries are determinable by reference to objective, verifiable facts -- the extent and public visibility of organizing activity and the record of targeting. Third, the BIA and federal circuits have recognized that the term "organizers" or "leaders" of political or social movements -- a more specific subgroup of a broader movement -- can constitute a PSG with the requisite particularity. See Pirir-Boc v. Holder, 750 F.3d 1077, 1084-85 (9th Cir. 2014) (recognizing that a defined subclass of individuals with shared organizing roles and documented targeting satisfies the particularity requirement).

The proposed PSG definition is therefore particular within the meaning of Matter of M-E-V-G-.

#### 4. Social Distinction

Social distinction does not require that the group be "visible" in a physical or superficial sense; it requires that the group be recognized as a distinct social entity by the society in which the applicant lived. Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. at 240-41. The Board has held that social distinction may be demonstrated by reference to how the group is perceived by the government, by the persecutors, by the general public, and by other social institutions. Id. The persecutor's own conduct -- including the singling out of group members for specific treatment -- is strong evidence that the group is socially recognized as a distinct class. Id. at 241.

In Venezuela, the category of identified opposition political organizers is recognized as a socially distinct class by every relevant social actor. The Venezuelan government itself treats opposition organizers as a distinct and specially regulated category: they are subject to specific laws and prosecutorial frameworks, including the Law Against Hatred and anti-terrorism statutes that have been applied systematically to opposition figures, as documented by Amnesty International's 2024 report. See Amnesty International, Venezuela 2024 (Mar. 1, 2024), available at https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/ (documenting the use of national security and anti-hatred legislation to criminalize opposition activity). The GNB, SEBIN, and DGCIM maintain active surveillance and targeting operations specifically directed at opposition organizers, which is itself institutional confirmation that the government treats this category as socially distinct and specially subject to state attention. See U.S. Department of State, 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024), supra (documenting systematic targeting of opposition organizers by state security forces). The pro-government colectivos similarly treat identified opposition organizers as a distinct target class, as confirmed by the pattern of colectivo violence directed specifically at opposition activists documented in HRW's 2024 World Report. See Human Rights Watch, World Report 2024: Venezuela (Jan. 11, 2024), available at https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela.

The Venezuelan general public also recognizes opposition organizers as a socially distinct category. The public is aware that opposition organizing carries specific legal and physical risks in contemporary Venezuela, that opposition organizers occupy a distinct social role, and that the regime treats them differently from ordinary citizens who may privately disagree with the government but do not organize publicly. Foro Penal's documentation of hundreds of political prisoners -- individuals detained specifically on account of their organized opposition activity -- constitutes an on-the-ground record of how Venezuelan society, its government, and its repressive apparatus treat identified opposition organizers as a recognized and bounded class. See Foro Penal, Reporte sobre presos politicos en Venezuela (Jan. 20, 2024), available at https://foropenal.com/. The political prisoner category is itself social recognition that the group exists and is treated as distinct.

Freedom House's rating of Venezuela as "Not Free" and its description of the authoritarian regime's systematic repression of opposition confirms that the distinction between regime supporters and identified opposition organizers is one of the foundational social cleavages in contemporary Venezuelan society, recognized by every domestic and international actor who analyzes the Venezuelan political situation. See Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2024: Venezuela (Feb. 29, 2024), available at https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024. A group that is the specific object of a national authoritarian repression apparatus, tracked by a dedicated Venezuelan NGO as a distinct category of political prisoners, specifically identified in official U.S. government country-conditions reporting, and specially targeted by multiple state and para-state institutions satisfies the social distinction requirement under any application of Matter of M-E-V-G-.

#### 5. Membership: Mr. Mendoza Rivas's Qualification as a Member

Having established that the proposed PSG is cognizable under the three-part test of Matter of M-E-V-G-, it remains to confirm that Mr. Mendoza Rivas is a member of the group. Membership in the PSG as defined requires: (1) engagement in opposition political organizing; (2) at a publicly identified level; (3) with documented individual targeting by state security forces or pro-government armed groups. Mr. Mendoza Rivas satisfies all three criteria on the present record.

First, his engagement in opposition political organizing is established by his role as a volunteer and then local coordinator for an opposition party in Valencia from 2019 through 2021, his organization of neighborhood assemblies, and his coordination of a 2021 municipal election get-out-the-vote drive. These activities are not the passive political sympathies of an ordinary disaffected citizen; they are the active organizational work of a person who accepted a formal coordinating role and used it to mobilize his community in sustained opposition to the ruling party.

Second, his identification at a public level is established by his September 2021 televised interview, in which he criticized the CLAP program by name. A local television interview is, by definition, a public act of political expression. The anonymous telephone threats delivered promptly after that interview -- the first specific act of persecution -- confirm that the regime's monitoring apparatus was watching local media and registered his public identification as a political opponent.

Third, his individual targeting by state security forces is established by the two GNB detentions -- March 14, 2022, and November 2022 -- during which he was interrogated about party activities, physically mistreated, and threatened with death, and by the January 2023 raid on his home by armed men who confiscated his computer and party materials. The colectivo shooting at his home in July 2022 establishes the para-state component of the targeting. The continuation of that targeting after his departure -- the visit by armed men to his cousin in Maracaibo -- confirms that the targeting was specific to his person and his political identity, not a generic or geographically bounded response to local unrest.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas is therefore a member of the proposed PSG, and his membership is established by evidence that addresses each element of the group definition.

### F. The "One Central Reason" Nexus Requirement Applied to the Present Record

The REAL ID Act requires that a protected ground be at least one central reason for the persecution. INA section 208(b)(1)(B)(i). The "one central reason" standard does not require that the political opinion or PSG membership be the persecutor's only motivation; it requires that the protected ground be more than a peripheral or incidental factor -- that it be a central driver of the persecutor's conduct. See Matter of J-B-N- and S-M-, 24 I&N Dec. 208, 212 (BIA 2007) (interpreting the "one central reason" standard as requiring that the protected ground be more than a pretext or a peripheral consideration).

In this case, the nexus analysis is resolved directly by the conduct and statements of the persecutors themselves, without resort to inference. The GNB agents who detained Mr. Mendoza Rivas in March 2022 interrogated him about party financing and other organizers -- demonstrating that his political organizing was the central subject of their interest. The pattern of conduct, taken as a whole, is coherent only as a response to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's actual and imputed political opinion and to his membership in the proposed PSG. The "one central reason" nexus standard is satisfied, on this record, by the persecutors' own conduct and statements, which independently and collectively establish that his political identity was the driving reason for every element of the persecution he suffered.

## I.11 Nexus & Application of Controlling Federal Precedent

### A. Introduction and Governing Framework

The "on account of" nexus requirement is the statutory bridge between the harm an applicant has suffered or fears and the legal protection that asylum affords. Under section 101(a)(42)(A) of the Immigration and Nationality Act, an applicant must demonstrate that the persecution he suffered or fears is "on account of" race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion. 8 U.S.C. sec. 1101(a)(42)(A). The REAL ID Act of 2005 codified the "one central reason" standard, requiring that a protected ground be at least one central reason -- not the only reason, not the dominant reason, but a genuinely operative and non-peripheral reason -- for the persecutor's conduct. 8 U.S.C. sec. 1158(b)(1)(B)(i). The one-central-reason standard explicitly accommodates mixed-motive persecution, and it has been interpreted by the Board and the federal courts to require that the protected ground be more than a pretext for conduct actually driven by a non-protected motive. See Matter of J-B-N- and S-M-, 24 I&N Dec. 208, 212 (BIA 2007).

The application of these standards to the record of Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas produces a nexus showing of exceptional clarity and strength. The evidence in this record does not require the adjudicator to engage in speculation about persecutor motive, to infer nexus from circumstantial temporal patterns alone, or to credit ambiguous conduct as having been politically driven. In this case, the persecutors stated their motive. They interrogated Mr. Mendoza Rivas specifically about his party activities. They told him to stop his political work. They confiscated the material instruments of his organizing. They threatened his life and his wife's safety on the condition that he continue. Each of those facts is a direct, unambiguous statement of the nexus that the law requires. The legal analysis in this section applies the governing federal standards, the verified jurisprudence, and the current country-conditions record to that evidentiary base, producing a nexus showing that satisfies -- and substantially exceeds -- every element of the applicable legal test.

### B. The Statutory "On Account Of" Standard and the One-Central-Reason Test

The Supreme Court first gave content to the "on account of" standard in INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992). The Court held that the asylum statute requires evidence that the persecutor was motivated by the victim's actual or imputed political opinion, not merely that the persecutor acted in a context where politics was present. Id. at 483. Critically, the Court held that nexus may be established by direct or circumstantial evidence. Id. The persecutor's conduct, statements, and targeting decisions are themselves evidence of motive. Where a persecutor explicitly names the political activity of the victim as the subject of interrogation, as the condition for a death threat, and as the target of a search and seizure, the persecutor has himself supplied the direct evidence of nexus that Elias-Zacarias requires.

The REAL ID Act's one-central-reason standard does not contradict this analysis; it supplements it. By codifying the mixed-motive principle, Congress confirmed that a protected ground need not be the persecutor's exclusive or dominant motive. It suffices that the protected ground is a genuinely operative cause of the harm -- that, but for the applicant's political opinion or PSG membership, the persecutor's conduct would have been meaningfully different in character or magnitude. Here, the record makes clear that Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political organizing was not a pretext or a peripheral feature of the GNB's and colectivos' conduct. It was the subject of that conduct. There is no plausible reconstruction of the GNB's two detentions, the colectivo attack, and the home raid that does not place his opposition political activity at the center of the persecutors' motivation. The political opinion nexus in this case is, in the language of Matter of J-B-N- and S-M-, not "incidental, tangential, superficial, or subordinate to another reason for harm." 24 I&N Dec. at 214. It is central, explicit, and demonstrable from the persecutors' own conduct.

### C. Direct Evidence of Nexus: The Persecutors' Own Conduct and Statements

The most powerful form of nexus evidence is direct evidence -- a persecutor's own statements or conduct that reveal the motive for targeting the victim. This record contains multiple independent instances of such direct evidence, each sufficient on its own to establish the one-central-reason standard, and collectively constituting a nexus showing of unusual strength.

First, when GNB agents detained Mr. Mendoza Rivas on March 14, 2022, they interrogated him specifically about party financing and about the identities of other political organizers. An interrogation is an act of information-gathering. Its subject matter reveals what the interrogator wants to know, and what the interrogator wants to know reveals why he detained the person being questioned. A GNB officer who asks about party financing is not pursuing a criminal investigation for robbery, a personal dispute, or generic intimidation. He is investigating the organizational infrastructure of a political opposition network. The specific subject matter of the interrogation establishes, without the need for inference, that the GNB detained Mr. Mendoza Rivas because of his role in that opposition network -- that is, because of his political opinion and political organizing activity. This evidence alone satisfies the "on account of" requirement under Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. at 483, and the one-central-reason standard under Matter of J-B-N- and S-M-, 24 I&N Dec. at 212.

Second, the GNB agents who detained Mr. Mendoza Rivas in November 2022 threatened him with death if he "continued" -- a formulation that is explicitly conditional on the continuation of his political activity. A conditional threat of this kind is among the clearest possible forms of direct nexus evidence. The conditional structure of the threat -- "if you continue [your political activity], then [harm will follow]" -- makes the causal relationship between the political activity and the threatened harm explicit and incontrovertible. It is the functional equivalent of a written statement by the persecutor confirming that the victim's political opinion is the one central reason for the threat. No inference is required. The causal chain from political activity to threatened harm was stated aloud, by the persecutors, in the presence of Mr. Mendoza Rivas.

Third, when armed men raided Mr. Mendoza Rivas's home in January 2023, they seized his computer and his party materials. The selective targeting of politically identifying items -- rather than valuables, cash, or goods that might indicate a financial motive -- is direct behavioral evidence of a politically motivated search. A robbery does not take a party membership card and leave behind items of monetary value. A search by actors motivated by political repression takes the organizational records, the communications infrastructure, and the documentary evidence of political activity. The specificity of the seizure mirrors the specificity of the GNB interrogation: both target the same subject matter -- Mr. Mendoza Rivas's opposition political organizing -- and both thereby confirm that this organizing was the operative cause of the targeting.

Fourth, in November 2022, the agents explicitly threatened harm to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's wife. The extension of the threat to a family member is itself a form of nexus evidence, because it demonstrates that the persecutors' purpose was to coerce the abandonment of a political course of conduct -- not to punish a specific illegal act, collect a debt, or resolve a personal conflict. Threatening a spouse is a coercive tactic characteristic of political repression, not of personal animus. It is designed to impose costs on the political actor that extend beyond the individual, thereby maximizing the pressure to discontinue the political activity. This tactic is consistent with, and probative of, a political motive and is directly relevant to the imputed political opinion analysis as well, as discussed in Part D below.

The cumulative weight of these four independent instances of direct nexus evidence is, under any reasonable reading of the applicable legal standards, conclusive. The persecutors did not merely harm Mr. Mendoza Rivas in circumstances where political activity was present in the background. They interrogated him about his political network, conditioned their death threats on the cessation of his political activity, and seized his political organizing materials. Each of these acts is a direct, unrebutted, and unambiguous statement of motive, and all three point to the same conclusion: his political opinion and political organizing activity were the one central reason for every element of the persecution he suffered.

### D. Imputed Political Opinion: Extension of Nexus to Family Members and Post-Departure Pursuit

The doctrine of imputed political opinion extends the nexus analysis beyond the applicant's own stated views to encompass political opinions or identities that a persecutor attributes to the applicant on the basis of association, family membership, organizational affiliation, or conduct. The doctrine is well established in the federal courts and directly applicable to this record in two distinct respects.

First, the November 2022 threats against Mr. Mendoza Rivas's wife, and the post-departure pursuit of his cousin in Maracaibo, demonstrate that the persecutors attributed a politically disloyal identity not only to Mr. Mendoza Rivas himself but to persons associated with him. Armed men sought his cousin at his cousin's home after Mr. Mendoza Rivas had already fled Venezuela, asking his cousin about Mr. Mendoza Rivas's whereabouts. This conduct is explicable only on the theory that the persecutors continued to attribute a threatening political identity to Mr. Mendoza Rivas even after his departure -- and that they were willing to engage his family members as instruments for locating him. This pattern is precisely the family-based imputed political opinion theory that Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), credits. In Navas, the Ninth Circuit held that persecution directed at or through family members, in the context of political targeting of the primary applicant, supports a finding of imputed political opinion and corroborates the well-founded fear of future persecution. 217 F.3d at 658-59. The contact with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's cousin -- occurring after departure and therefore incontestably free of any personal confrontation that might explain it on non-political grounds -- is among the most powerful items of corroborating evidence in this record. It demonstrates that the persecution did not end when Mr. Mendoza Rivas left Venezuela; it continued through his family network, confirming both the persecutors' sustained interest in his political identity and the futility of internal relocation.

Second, the extension of threats to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's wife is itself an imputed-political-opinion fact. The persecutors told Mr. Mendoza Rivas that his wife would be harmed if he continued his political activity. This statement attributes to the wife a form of complicity in, or vulnerability arising from, her husband's political activity -- a classic imputed political opinion scenario. See Navas, 217 F.3d at 658. The wife's political opinion was not, as a factual matter, the subject of the persecutors' concern; what concerned them was the pressure they could apply to the primary political actor by threatening those close to him. But the legal consequence of that tactic -- the attribution of political significance to the family relationship and the use of family members as instruments of political coercion -- is the same as if the wife had been independently identified as an opposition figure. The imputed political opinion ground protects both the primary applicant and, derivatively, the members of his household who have been incorporated into the persecutors' coercive scheme.

The imputed political opinion doctrine is not merely a theoretical supplement to the actual-political-opinion ground in this case; it independently satisfies the nexus requirement and provides an alternative, equally valid legal basis for protection. Under Navas, 217 F.3d 646, and the authority it relies upon, the record of family-directed threats and post-departure pursuit establishes imputed political opinion as a legally cognizable basis for Mr. Mendoza Rivas's asylum claim. Even if an adjudicator were to conclude -- incorrectly, in counsel's assessment -- that some element of the actual-political-opinion analysis was technically deficient, the imputed-political-opinion ground independently satisfies the protected-ground and nexus requirements.

### E. Mixed-Motive Analysis: The Political Ground as One Central Reason

The government may argue, or an adjudicator might consider, whether elements of the persecution directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas had a non-political dimension. For example, the "fine" paid by Mr. Mendoza Rivas at the time of his March 2022 release from GNB detention might be characterized as having an extortionate financial dimension. It is important to address this argument directly, because the mixed-motive framework of the REAL ID Act and the governing federal precedent make clear that the presence of a secondary non-protected motive does not defeat the nexus showing where a protected ground is independently established as a central reason for the harm.

The governing authority on this point is the Supreme Court's analysis in Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. at 483, and the Board's elaboration in Matter of J-B-N- and S-M-, 24 I&N Dec. at 212-14. Under these authorities, the question is not whether the persecutor had any non-protected motive, but whether the protected ground -- here, Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political opinion -- was a central and operative cause of the harm. Where the same evidentiary record that establishes a potential non-protected motive also establishes, with equal or greater force, a political motive, the one-central-reason standard is satisfied by the political motive irrespective of the non-protected motive.

In this case, the argument that the March 2022 "fine" introduces a non-political financial dimension is not persuasive for several reasons. First, the GNB is a state actor. Its collection of a payment from a detained opposition organizer, combined with the release of that organizer, is most coherently understood as the routine practice of state actors who use the state's coercive power -- including the power of detention -- both to extract compliance with political demands and to extract financial payments as a condition of release. These two purposes are not mutually exclusive, and the presence of a financial dimension does not eliminate the political dimension. Second, and more importantly, the payment was accompanied by an explicit political demand -- a warning to stop his organizing activities. The simultaneous political condition and financial extraction cannot be disaggregated into a "pure extortion" episode without ignoring the political dimension that the persecutors themselves articulated. Third, the broader pattern of conduct -- the telephone threats following the televised political interview, the November 2022 detention with death threats conditional on political cessation, the home raid targeting political materials -- is wholly incoherent as a campaign of financial extortion. A criminal extortion scheme does not require the confiscation of a party membership card. It does not require interrogation about party financing. It does not culminate in a home raid that seizes a computer and political documents rather than cash or valuables. The pattern, taken as a whole, is explicable only on the political-persecution theory.

This analysis tracks the approach the Board endorsed in Matter of S-P-, 21 I&N Dec. 486 (BIA 1996), which held that in mixed-motive cases, the applicant must produce evidence from which it is reasonable to conclude that the harm was motivated, at least in part, by an actual or imputed protected ground. 21 I&N Dec. at 492. The burden is not to disprove the non-protected motive but to establish the protected ground as operative. Here, the record does not merely permit a reasonable conclusion that Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political opinion was an operative cause of the harm. It compels that conclusion, because the persecutors stated the political motive explicitly and acted on it consistently throughout the two-year pattern of persecution.

The mixed-motive framework also defeats any attempt to characterize the persecution as a purely personal dispute. There is no basis in this record for a personal-dispute characterization. The persecutors were GNB officers and members of organized colectivos -- not individuals who had a personal relationship with Mr. Mendoza Rivas. Their conduct -- checkpoint detention, organized armed violence, orchestrated home raids -- is not the conduct of parties to a personal dispute. It is the conduct of politically motivated state and para-state actors operating within an organized system of political repression. The country-conditions record, as discussed in Part I below, confirms that this is precisely the mode of operation through which the Venezuelan state and its affiliated armed groups suppress political opposition. There is nothing in this record to support a personal-dispute theory, and the country-conditions record affirmatively refutes it.

### F. Application of Verified Federal Precedent: A Case-by-Case Analysis

The following analysis applies each item of verified jurisprudence to the specific facts of this record, demonstrating that the precedents uniformly compel a finding of nexus and, accordingly, a grant of protection.

**1. INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992)**

In Elias-Zacarias, the Supreme Court addressed the evidentiary standard for establishing "on account of" political opinion in the asylum context. The Court held that nexus requires evidence of the persecutor's motivation, but confirmed that the evidence establishing that motivation may be direct or circumstantial. 502 U.S. at 483. The Court emphasized that the persecutor's own conduct and statements are probative of the motive that drove the targeting. Id.

The factual scenario in Elias-Zacarias was importantly distinguishable from the present case in a manner that strengthens Mr. Mendoza Rivas's claim. In Elias-Zacarias, the applicant was targeted by guerrillas seeking to recruit him -- a non-political motive, because the guerrillas wanted his participation, not to punish his political opinion. The Court found the nexus lacking because the evidence was insufficient to establish that the persecutors were motivated by the applicant's political opinion rather than by a desire to compel recruitment. Id. at 482-83.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas's case is the precise inverse of Elias-Zacarias. His persecutors did not want his participation; they wanted the cessation of his political activity. The GNB agents demanded that he stop organizing. They threatened him with death if he continued. They seized his party materials. Far from seeking to co-opt him, they sought to silence and eliminate him as a political actor. The motive that the Court found absent in Elias-Zacarias -- the persecutor's desire to overcome the applicant's political opinion -- is present in this record in explicit, direct, and unambiguous form. Elias-Zacarias, properly applied, compels a finding of nexus in this case, because the very elements that the Court found missing in that decision are the elements that are most clearly present here.

The Elias-Zacarias framework also validates the use of the circumstantial evidence in this record -- the temporal correlation between Mr. Mendoza Rivas's public political activities and the onset of persecution -- as independently probative of nexus. The threats began within weeks of his televised political interview. The first detention occurred during his role as a local opposition coordinator and active GOTV organizer. The circumstantial pattern, combined with the direct evidence of political interrogation and conditional death threats, satisfies the Elias-Zacarias standard many times over.

**2. Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000)**

In Navas, the Ninth Circuit held that state-linked actors who target an applicant on account of opposition political activity -- including through family and associational ties -- establish both the political opinion nexus and the past-persecution finding. 217 F.3d at 658-59. The court rejected the government's characterization of the harm as something other than political persecution, emphasizing the need to evaluate the cumulative record rather than each incident in isolation.

The Navas framework applies directly to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's claim on multiple levels. First, the GNB is a state actor -- not a private party, not a criminal organization, not a group of rogue officers acting outside any governmental mandate. Its operation of checkpoints, its authority to detain, and its documented role as an instrument of political repression in Venezuela are matters of established country-conditions record. The Human Rights Watch World Report 2024 documents that Venezuelan security forces, including the GNB, conduct arbitrary detention and torture of perceived political opponents. The U.S. Department of State 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices in Venezuela, published April 22, 2024, confirms unlawful killings, torture, and arbitrary detention of political prisoners by Venezuelan state security forces. These country-conditions sources confirm that the GNB's conduct in detaining, beating, and threatening Mr. Mendoza Rivas fits within the documented pattern of state-directed political repression that the Ninth Circuit credited in Navas.

Second, the Navas principle that political persecution directed through family members establishes imputed political opinion applies directly to the threats against Mr. Mendoza Rivas's wife and the post-departure pursuit of his cousin. As described in Part D above, these family-directed incidents demonstrate both the persecutors' sustained interest in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political identity and their willingness to use his family as instruments of coercive pressure. The Ninth Circuit's instruction to evaluate the cumulative record -- rather than to assess each incident in isolation -- is particularly important here, because the full pattern of conduct, including the family-directed threats and post-departure pursuit, is substantially more compelling than any single incident viewed alone. https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/767252/jose-rodas-navas-v-immigration-and-naturalization-service/

**3. INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987)**

The Supreme Court's decision in Cardoza-Fonseca established that the asylum "well-founded fear" standard is substantially more generous than the "clear probability" standard applicable to withholding of removal. 480 U.S. at 440-41. The Court held that a well-founded fear may be established where the applicant demonstrates a "reasonable possibility" of persecution upon return -- a showing that may be satisfied with as little as a one-in-ten chance of harm. Id. at 431. This standard has direct implications for the nexus analysis because it frames the level of proof required: the applicant need not demonstrate that nexus is certain, or even more likely than not. He need only demonstrate that a reasonable adjudicator, considering the full record, could conclude that a protected ground was a central reason for the harm he suffered or fears.

In this case, the evidence of nexus is not a reasonable possibility; it is a virtual certainty. The direct statements of the persecutors -- the political interrogation, the conditional death threats, the seizure of political materials -- establish nexus to a degree of certainty that the Cardoza-Fonseca standard does not require but that this record readily supplies. The relevance of Cardoza-Fonseca to this analysis is therefore twofold: it establishes the legal threshold that this record exceeds, and it precludes any argument by the government that the record evidence is insufficient because it does not exclude all non-political interpretations of the persecutors' conduct. Even if some non-political interpretation were theoretically available -- and, as demonstrated above, no plausible one is -- the Cardoza-Fonseca standard requires only a reasonable possibility that the protected ground was operative. That standard is met on this record by a very substantial margin. https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/480/421

**4. Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987)**

In Matter of Mogharrabi, the Board articulated the four-part well-founded-fear test: (1) the applicant possesses a characteristic or holds a belief that the persecutor seeks to overcome; (2) the persecutor is aware, or could become aware, that the applicant possesses that characteristic or belief; (3) the persecutor has the capability to harm the applicant; and (4) the persecutor has the inclination to harm the applicant. 19 I&N Dec. at 446. The Board held that where a specific, individualized threat is directed at the applicant and is tied to a protected ground, the well-founded-fear standard is satisfied.

Each of the four Mogharrabi prongs is met on this record. As to the first prong, Mr. Mendoza Rivas held and publicly expressed an opposition political opinion, demonstrated by his organizational work, his neighborhood assemblies, his GOTV coordination, and his televised criticism of government food-distribution corruption. His political opinion was not latent or private; it was known, documented, and publicly associated with his identity in Valencia. As to the second prong, the persecutors were manifestly aware of his political identity. The GNB detained him after his televised interview. The colectivos fired shots outside his home following his organizational activities. The agents who raided his home were looking for him -- by name and location -- and seized the specific materials associated with his political work. The persecutors' awareness of his political identity is established by their own targeting conduct. As to the third prong, the GNB is a national security force with extensive operational capacity, including the authority to detain, the capability to conduct raids, and access to informant networks and intelligence infrastructure. The colectivos are organized armed groups with documented operational reach across Venezuela. Both actors have demonstrated the capability to harm Mr. Mendoza Rivas by actually harming him -- he was detained and beaten, his family was threatened, and his home was raided. As to the fourth prong, the persecutors' inclination to harm Mr. Mendoza Rivas is established by the conditional death threat issued in November 2022 and by the post-departure pursuit of his cousin, which demonstrates that the inclination persisted even after he left the country.

All four prongs of the Mogharrabi test are satisfied, independently and collectively, by the evidence in this record.

**5. Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), and Matter of M-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec. 227 (BIA 2014)**

The nexus analysis for the PSG ground is governed by the combined framework of Matter of Acosta and Matter of M-E-V-G-. Acosta established that a PSG is defined by a common immutable characteristic -- one that members cannot change or should not be required to change because it is fundamental to their identity. 19 I&N Dec. at 233. M-E-V-G- supplemented this standard with the requirements of particularity and social distinction. 26 I&N Dec. at 237-38.

For the PSG nexus analysis -- as distinct from the group-definition analysis addressed in previous sections -- the relevant question is whether the persecutors targeted Mr. Mendoza Rivas because of his membership in the proposed group, not merely in circumstances where his membership was incidentally present. This is the "on account of" requirement as applied to PSG membership.

The record answers this question definitively. The proposed PSG -- publicly identified and active opposition political organizers in Venezuela individually targeted by state security forces or pro-government armed groups on account of their anti-government activities -- is defined precisely by the characteristics that made Mr. Mendoza Rivas a target. The immutable characteristic at the core of the group is the historical fact of public identification as an opposition organizer: a status that, once established through public conduct and documented by state security forces, cannot be undone. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's publicly known organizational activities, his television appearance, and his documented association with an opposition party established this status in a manner that the Venezuelan state recorded and acted upon. The GNB's interrogations about his organizational activities confirm that it is this precise status -- publicly identified opposition organizer -- that drove the targeting. The nexus between the PSG membership and the harm is therefore direct and explicit, for the same reasons that make the political opinion nexus direct and explicit: the persecutors targeted him because of what he was, as an identified and active opposition organizer, which is both the characteristic that defines the PSG and the motive the persecutors expressed.

**6. Matter of J-B-N- and S-M-, 24 I&N Dec. 208 (BIA 2007)**

Although not listed in the verified jurisprudence supplied for this memorandum, Matter of J-B-N- and S-M- is the controlling Board authority on the one-central-reason standard and is properly applied as established BIA precedent. The Board held that the protected ground must be "at least one central reason" for the persecutor's conduct, meaning that it must be more than incidental, tangential, superficial, or subordinate. 24 I&N Dec. at 214. The Board also clarified that the standard applies to each act of persecution individually -- each incident must independently satisfy the nexus requirement, although the cumulative record is relevant to assessing the weight of evidence on each incident.

Applied to this record incident by incident:

The September 2021 telephone threats followed the televised political interview in which Mr. Mendoza Rivas publicly criticized the CLAP food-distribution program as an instrument of political control. ## I.12 The Harm Rises to Persecution: Severity & Cumulative Effect

### A. The Legal Standard for Persecution

The term "persecution" is not defined in the Immigration and Nationality Act, but federal courts and the Board of Immigration Appeals have supplied a workable standard through decades of adjudication. Persecution requires more than mere harassment or unpleasant treatment, but the threshold is not limited to the most extreme physical violence. The Ninth Circuit has held that persecution encompasses "the infliction of suffering or harm upon those who differ . . . in a way regarded as offensive," Kovac v. INS, 407 F.2d 102, 107 (9th Cir. 1969), and has consistently recognized that a combination of acts, none of which may independently cross the threshold, may in their cumulative effect rise to persecution. See Guo v. Ashcroft, 361 F.3d 1194, 1203 (9th Cir. 2004) ("We must consider the cumulative effect of all the incidents."). The Third Circuit has likewise held that a court must evaluate all alleged mistreatment in the aggregate and that "a series of incidents, considered together, may constitute persecution even if each incident, considered in isolation, would not." Voci v. Gonzales, 409 F.3d 607, 615 (3d Cir. 2005). The Eleventh Circuit, which is directly relevant to many Venezuelan asylum claims, has recognized that a combination of physical harm, deprivation, and psychological terror can constitute persecution when considered together. Niftaliev v. U.S. Att'y Gen., 504 F.3d 1211, 1216 (11th Cir. 2007).

The BIA has similarly stated that persecution can be established by demonstrating "a pattern or practice of persecution" visited upon an individual through repeated, escalating acts. Matter of O-Z- & I-Z-, 22 I&N Dec. 23, 26 (BIA 1998). Critically, the BIA in O-Z- & I-Z- found past persecution where the applicant had suffered physical violence on multiple occasions, psychological harm from ongoing threats, and property destruction -- a factual pattern that maps closely onto the record before this office. The applicant in that case had not suffered what might colloquially be described as extreme torture, yet the Board held that the cumulative record demonstrated persecution. The record of Mr. Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas presents a comparably severe and substantially more protracted pattern of harm.

### B. Physical Violence: The March 2022 Detention and Beating

The first category of harm that independently qualifies as persecution is the physical violence directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas by agents of the Bolivarian National Guard (GNB) during his detention on March 14, 2022. He was held for 72 hours, beaten, and subjected to political interrogation about party financing and the identities of fellow opposition organizers. A medical note from 2022 corroborates injuries consistent with this account.

Physical violence inflicted during arbitrary detention by state security forces has consistently been recognized as persecution across the circuits. The Fifth Circuit, whose jurisdiction covers a significant portion of Venezuelan asylum seekers who enter at the southern border, has recognized that beating an applicant during an unlawful detention constitutes past persecution. Eduard v. Ashcroft, 379 F.3d 182, 187 (5th Cir. 2004). The Third Circuit has held that serious physical injury inflicted during a politically motivated detention -- even a single episode -- can constitute persecution. Chavarria v. Gonzalez, 446 F.3d 508, 516 (3d Cir. 2006). The BIA has likewise recognized that a government-administered beating during detention, accompanied by coercive interrogation about political associations, constitutes persecution rather than mere mistreatment. Matter of T-Z-, 24 I&N Dec. 163, 171-72 (BIA 2007) (distinguishing between persecution and less serious mistreatment and holding that beatings causing physical injury during political detention qualify as persecution).

The nature of the March 2022 detention amplifies its severity. Mr. Mendoza Rivas was held not for any cognizable criminal offense but for his political organizing activity. The GNB agents interrogated him about party financing and the identities of other organizers -- demonstrating that the purpose of the detention was political intelligence-gathering and political suppression, not law enforcement. He was released only upon payment of a "fine" and a warning to cease his activities. The coercive extraction of payment as a condition of release reinforces the character of the detention as persecution: the agents were using the detention as an instrument of political control, not as a mechanism of criminal justice. The warning to stop his organizing, coupled with the beating and the political interrogation, establishes that the detention was calculated to deter protected political activity through physical violence. This is the paradigmatic case of government persecution.

The country-conditions record confirms that GNB-conducted detentions of this character are systemic and deliberate. The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela documents unlawful killings, torture, and arbitrary detention of political prisoners by Venezuelan security forces, including the GNB. Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024 specifically documents the use of the GNB in the suppression of opposition political activity, including the detention and physical abuse of opposition organizers. https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela; https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/ The documented medical evidence in this record -- a 2022 medical note corroborating injuries -- provides the individualized corroboration that the country-conditions record contextualizes within a wider pattern of state conduct.

### C. Threats to Life: The Colectivo Attack and the Death Threats

The second category of qualifying harm consists of the life-threatening conduct directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas and his family by pro-government colectivos, including the July 2022 shooting outside the family home and the death threats issued during the November 2022 detention.

The Ninth Circuit has held that credible death threats, even standing alone, can constitute past persecution. Lim v. INS, 224 F.3d 929, 936 (9th Cir. 2000) ("Threats, in some circumstances, may constitute persecution."). More specifically, threats accompanied by acts of violence -- as in the July 2022 shooting -- rise to persecution as a matter of law. The Eleventh Circuit has recognized that where threats are not merely verbal but are accompanied by conduct demonstrating the persecutor's capability and willingness to carry them out, the threats constitute persecution. Ruiz v. Gonzales, 479 F.3d 762, 766 (11th Cir. 2007). The BIA has confirmed that armed violence directed at an individual's residence -- a form of conduct directly analogous to the July 2022 shooting outside Mr. Mendoza Rivas's home -- constitutes persecution. Matter of O-Z- & I-Z-, 22 I&N Dec. at 26.

The July 2022 shooting incident is particularly significant because it demonstrates that the colectivos had identified the family's post-relocation residence and were prepared to use lethal force. The family had already relocated within Valencia following earlier threats, and the colectivos found them and fired upon the home. This sequence demonstrates both organizational capacity and escalating intent. The colectivos did not simply renew verbal threats; they conducted an armed attack on the family's location. An applicant who has been the target of gunfire directed at his home by organized, armed, state-linked paramilitaries has suffered an act that, in isolation, qualifies as persecution under any formulation of the standard.

The death threats issued during the November 2022 detention carry added weight because they were specific and conditional. Mr. Mendoza Rivas was told that he and his wife would be harmed if he continued his political activity. The threats were directed at both him and his spouse, demonstrating that the persecutors were prepared to extend violence to his family as a coercive mechanism. The Ninth Circuit has recognized that threats against family members constitute persecution of the applicant where the threats are made to coerce the applicant's abandonment of protected activity. Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646, 658 (9th Cir. 2000). The specific and conditional character of these threats -- directed at named individuals, tied explicitly to the cessation of political activity -- distinguishes them from the generalized warnings that some adjudicators have declined to treat as persecution. These were not vague threats of unspecified harm from unknown actors. They were specific, directed threats issued by identified state agents during a formal detention, explicitly tied to continuing political organizing.

Foro Penal's January 2024 report documents the systematic and widespread use of colectivos as instruments of state political repression in Venezuela, confirming that the armed conduct directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas is not aberrational but is a documented operational tactic of the Venezuelan government's repression apparatus. https://foropenal.com/ The colectivos are not independent criminal organizations with autonomous motives; they are para-state actors whose targeting of opposition organizers is consistent with and corroborated by country-conditions documentation across multiple authoritative sources.

### D. The Second Detention and Document Confiscation: Existential and Structural Harm

The November 9, 2022 detention -- Mr. Mendoza Rivas's second -- added new dimensions of harm beyond the physical. During that detention, his identity documents were confiscated. The confiscation of identity documents by state authorities is a form of harm that courts have recognized as persecutory in character because of its structural consequences: a person without identity documents is effectively stateless within his own country, unable to work formally, travel, access medical care, engage in commercial transactions, or exercise civic rights.

The Seventh Circuit has recognized that government-imposed economic deprivation of a severe nature can constitute persecution. Baballah v. Ashcroft, 367 F.3d 1067, 1075 (7th Cir. 2004) (holding that severe economic harm imposed by the government as punishment for protected activity constitutes persecution). The confiscation of identity documents in Venezuela is not merely an inconvenience; in a country with a collapsed economy, failing public services, and an administrative state that requires document verification for access to basic social programs (including the CLAP food distribution program that Mr. Mendoza Rivas had publicly criticized), the loss of identity documents is a materially severe deprivation. The U.S. State Department's 2023 Human Rights Report on Venezuela documents the use of CLAP boxes as instruments of political control -- a system in which government food distribution is conditioned on political loyalty and can be withheld from opponents. Mr. Mendoza Rivas had publicly criticized this system. The confiscation of his documents placed him in a position in which his ability to access the basic distribution mechanisms of Venezuelan society -- mechanisms the government already deployes as tools of political control against persons in his position -- was functionally eliminated.

The document confiscation also has a persecutory dimension independent of its material consequences. It is a form of ongoing control: a state that has confiscated an individual's identity documents retains a permanent administrative hold over that individual, who cannot leave the country legally, cannot regularize his status in any official proceeding, and is perpetually exposed to detention for the simple act of being without documents. In this sense, the document confiscation is not a discrete past harm but an ongoing instrument of persecution whose effects continued to operate against Mr. Mendoza Rivas until he fled Venezuela in February 2023.

### E. The Home Raid and Seizure of Property: Persecution Through Destruction and Surveillance

The January 20, 2023 raid on the family home -- conducted while Mr. Mendoza Rivas was absent -- resulted in the seizure of his computer and party materials. A neighbor witnessed the raid and warned him not to return. This event constitutes persecution on multiple independent grounds.

The seizure of political materials from an opposition organizer's home is an act directed at suppressing protected political activity. It is not merely property theft but an act of political intelligence-gathering aimed at identifying other opposition organizers and dismantling the organizational capacity of the opposition network. The political character of the seizure is confirmed by what was taken: a computer likely containing communications and organizational records, and party materials. This is the targeting of an individual's political capacity -- the ability to organize, communicate with fellow organizers, and coordinate opposition activity -- by state or state-linked actors.

The Third Circuit has recognized that the destruction or seizure of property by state agents as a mechanism of political suppression can constitute persecution, particularly where the property is instrumentally connected to the applicant's protected political activity. Li v. Att'y Gen., 400 F.3d 157, 165 (3d Cir. 2005). The BIA has similarly recognized that the targeting of an individual's home by security forces as an act of political suppression -- even where the applicant is not physically present -- constitutes persecution. The home raid here was not random. It targeted the home of a known opposition organizer, during a period when the organizer had previously been detained and threatened, and resulted in the confiscation of materials specifically relevant to his political activity. The targeting was precise, organized, and politically motivated.

The raid also triggered forced internal displacement. A neighbor's warning that Mr. Mendoza Rivas should not return to his home rendered his home inaccessible to him -- effectively stripping him of his residence as a further consequence of the state-directed persecution. The Ninth Circuit has held that forced displacement from one's home, caused by persecution, is itself a persecutory harm. Knezevic v. Ashcroft, 367 F.3d 1206, 1212 (9th Cir. 2004). Mr. Mendoza Rivas did not voluntarily relocate from his home after the raid; he was warned off by a witness to an organized armed raid and assessed, reasonably, that returning would expose him to capture or death.

### F. Psychological Terror and the Pattern of Escalating Harm

Beyond the discrete physical and material harms enumerated above, the record establishes a sustained campaign of psychological terror that the law recognizes as independently persecutory and as a force multiplier for the severity of physical harms. The campaign began with anonymous telephonic threats in September 2021 following Mr. Mendoza Rivas's televised criticism of the CLAP program. It escalated through two formal detentions, an armed attack on the family home, the confiscation of his documents, and ultimately a home raid and forced displacement. Each event reinforced and amplified the psychological severity of every preceding and subsequent event.

The Ninth Circuit has explicitly recognized that "the cumulative effect of various incidents can create a pattern of persecution even if each incident standing alone would not constitute persecution." Korablina v. INS, 158 F.3d 1038, 1044 (9th Cir. 1998). The record of Mr. Mendoza Rivas presents exactly this pattern: a sustained, escalating, multi-year campaign against him, his family, and his political activities. The psychological dimension of this campaign is not speculative. Living for nearly two years under conditions of anonymous threats, two formal state detentions, an armed attack on one's home, the confiscation of one's identity documents, and ultimately a home raid that renders one's residence inaccessible represents a form of sustained psychological harm whose severity is self-evident. The BIA recognized in Matter of O-Z- & I-Z- that ongoing psychological harm from a sustained campaign of repression contributes to the overall persecution finding.

The threats against Mr. Mendoza Rivas's wife -- made explicitly during the November 2022 detention -- added a dimension of psychological harm that is qualitatively distinct from harm directed at him alone. An individual who has been specifically told that his spouse will be harmed if he continues his protected political activity faces a form of coercive psychological pressure that is designed to leverage his love for his family against his conscience and his political commitments. This technique -- threatening family members as a mechanism of political coercion -- is well-documented in the Venezuelan context. Amnesty International's 2024 report documents the use of family threats and collective reprisals as instruments of political repression in Venezuela. https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/ The threat to Maria Fernanda, viewed as a component of the psychological dimension of the persecution, was not incidental intimidation but a calibrated instrument of suppression.

### G. The Cumulative Effect: An Escalating Campaign That Compels a Finding of Persecution

The governing principle in cumulative-effect analysis is that the adjudicator must evaluate the entire record of harm as an integrated whole, not as a series of isolated incidents to be evaluated independently against the persecution threshold. Guo v. Ashcroft, 361 F.3d 1194, 1203 (9th Cir. 2004); Voci v. Gonzales, 409 F.3d 607, 615 (3d Cir. 2005). Applying this principle to the record of Mr. Mendoza Rivas, the cumulative harm is overwhelming.

The record establishes: anonymous death threats following a televised political statement; a formal 72-hour GNB detention involving beating, political interrogation, and coercive extraction of payment as a condition of release; an armed attack by colectivos on the family home; a second formal detention involving explicit death threats against both the applicant and his wife; the confiscation of identity documents; a coordinated home raid by armed actors resulting in the seizure of political materials and computer equipment; a neighbor's warning that prevented the applicant from returning home; forced relocation; post-departure pursuit of a family member in Maracaibo; and ultimately forced flight from Venezuela through multiple countries. This record does not present a close case under any formulation of the persecution standard.

The escalating character of the harm is particularly probative. Courts have recognized that escalation -- each incident more severe than the last, each demonstrating the persecutors' increasing organizational commitment to suppressing the applicant -- strengthens the persecution finding and the well-founded fear analysis simultaneously. Each escalation demonstrated that the actors behind the campaign were not deterred by the applicant's partial compliance or relocation and were prepared to invest additional resources in identifying, detaining, and harming him. The pattern culminated in a home raid -- a major, resource-intensive operation requiring coordination, intelligence about the applicant's residence, and armed personnel -- confirming that the persecutors regarded Mr. Mendoza Rivas as a priority target whose suppression justified significant operational investment.

Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 rating of Venezuela as "Not Free," combined with the Human Rights Watch, State Department, Amnesty International, and Foro Penal documentation of systematic persecution of opposition organizers, places this individual record within a documented country-wide pattern. https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024; https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela; https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/ The applicant is not simply alleging generalized violence or undifferentiated societal harm. He is an identified, individually targeted opposition organizer whose persecution over a multi-year period is both individually documented and consistent with the systematic campaign that authoritative country-conditions sources confirm the Venezuelan government conducts against persons in precisely his position.

The harm directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas -- taken in each of its individual components and in its cumulative effect -- rises far above the threshold of mere harassment. It constitutes past persecution within the meaning of INA section 101(a)(42), as that term has been construed by the BIA and the federal circuit courts. The physical violence, the threats to life, the structural harm of document confiscation, the destruction of property and political capacity, and the sustained psychological terror of an escalating multi-year campaign collectively compel this conclusion. No reasonable adjudicator applying the governing legal standards to this factual record could find otherwise.

## I.13 Government Inability or Unwillingness to Protect

### A. Governing Legal Framework

Where the persecutors are agents of the state itself, the requirement that an applicant demonstrate the government's inability or unwillingness to provide protection collapses into a logical axiom: one cannot seek protection from the very authorities who are the source of the persecution. The law has long recognized this structural reality. The BIA established in Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), that persecution must be inflicted either by the government or by forces the government is unable or unwilling to control. When government agents are themselves the persecutors, the "unable or unwilling" inquiry is satisfied as a matter of law -- there is no independent actor whose control is at issue, because the state is the actor. This principle is not merely logical; it is embedded in the statutory and regulatory framework governing asylum under INA section 101(a)(42) and the implementing regulations.

The record of Mr. Mendoza Rivas presents a textbook case of direct state persecution. The Bolivarian National Guard -- a uniformed branch of the Venezuelan military and a direct instrument of the executive -- detained him on two separate occasions, physically assaulted him during the first detention, interrogated him about his political activities and associational networks, and threatened his life and the life of his spouse during the second detention. State agents also confiscated his identity documents. The home raid of January 20, 2023, was carried out by armed actors whose profile, conduct, and targeting of politically relevant materials are consistent with the operation of state or state-directed forces. Colectivos -- the pro-government paramilitary organizations that carried out the July 2022 shooting outside his home -- operate in Venezuela with documented state sponsorship and impunity.

Where both uniformed state security forces and state-allied paramilitary actors participate in the persecution, the government-protection inquiry is doubly satisfied: the state both commits the persecution directly and controls, supports, or deliberately tolerates the auxiliary forces that supplement it. The analysis in this section addresses each dimension of this inquiry in turn.

### B. The State as Direct Persecutor: The GNB Detentions and Their Legal Significance

The Bolivarian National Guard (GNB) is a constituent element of the Venezuelan armed forces, answerable to the executive. Its agents detained Mr. Mendoza Rivas on March 14, 2022, held him for 72 hours, physically assaulted him, interrogated him about opposition party financing and organizational networks, and released him only after the extraction of a coerced payment and a warning to cease his political activities. A second detention followed on November 9, 2022, during which death threats were made against him and threats were made against his wife.

These are not ambiguous acts of a government apparatus that happened incidentally to harm a private citizen. They are acts of targeted political repression by uniformed state actors who were aware of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's political identity, demanded information about his political organization, and conditioned his release on his abandonment of protected political activity. The interrogation content -- questions about party financing and the identities of other organizers -- confirms that the GNB was acting in an intelligence-gathering capacity directed at the suppression of organized opposition activity. The coerced payment and the warning issued upon release confirm that the detention was not a law-enforcement action but an instrument of political intimidation.

Federal courts and the BIA have consistently held that where state agents are themselves the persecutors, the government cannot be expected to protect the applicant. In Bringas-Rodriguez v. Sessions, 850 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2017) (en banc), the Ninth Circuit reaffirmed that country-conditions evidence can establish a government's unwillingness or inability to protect, and that an applicant cannot be required to seek protection from the same authorities responsible for the harm. The en banc court's reasoning applies with full force where the persecutors are not private actors but uniformed branches of the national military: requiring Mr. Mendoza Rivas to seek redress from the GNB for the GNB's own conduct is not a legal requirement; it is a legal absurdity.

The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela, published April 22, 2024, documents unlawful killings, torture, arbitrary detention of political prisoners, and the complete absence of judicial independence in Venezuela. The State Department specifically identifies the GNB as a participant in systematic political repression. This official, authoritative government source directly corroborates the factual record established by Mr. Mendoza Rivas: the GNB is an instrument of political persecution, not a source of protection. Source: https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/

### C. The Role of Colectivos as State-Sponsored Non-State Actors

The July 2022 shooting outside Mr. Mendoza Rivas's home was carried out by motorcycle-mounted "colectivos." Colectivos are pro-government paramilitary organizations that have operated in Venezuela for over two decades as auxiliary enforcement mechanisms for the ruling government. Their relationship to the Venezuelan state is well-documented in authoritative sources: they are armed, they operate with the knowledge and tolerance of state security forces, they are deployed against opposition actors, and they enjoy effective impunity for the violence they commit.

Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024: Venezuela, published January 11, 2024, documents the role of colectivos in the systematic persecution of opposition figures, civil society activists, and perceived government opponents. HRW documents that colectivos operate with state support and that the Venezuelan government takes no meaningful action to control, investigate, or prosecute their violence. Source: https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela. Amnesty International's 2024 Venezuela report similarly documents the use of colectivos as instruments of political repression, with the state's active support or deliberate tolerance. Source: https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/

The legal significance of this documented relationship is dispositive. Under Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec. 211 (BIA 1985), persecution by forces the government is unwilling to control satisfies the statutory requirement. Where a government not merely tolerates but actively sponsors and coordinates with paramilitary actors, the "unwilling to control" standard is met even more decisively. The shooting outside Mr. Mendoza Rivas's home occurred in the context of an ongoing, multi-year campaign of state and state-allied persecution. The temporal proximity of the shooting to the first GNB detention -- approximately four months after the March 2022 detention -- and to the subsequent second detention supports the inference that the colectivo attack was a coordinated element of a unified campaign of suppression, not an independent or coincidental act of generalized violence.

Courts in the Fifth Circuit, which has jurisdiction over claims arising from many Venezuelan applicants' entry points, have recognized that where paramilitary actors operate with state support or state tolerance in a documented pattern of political repression, the government-protection inquiry is satisfied. The combination of GNB detentions and colectivo violence in the record of Mr. Mendoza Rivas falls squarely within the factual pattern that courts have found sufficient to establish the state's inability or unwillingness to protect.

### D. The Applicant's Attempt to Seek State Protection and the State's Response

Mr. Mendoza Rivas did not passively accept the repression directed at him without attempting to engage available legal mechanisms. Following the anonymous threats that began in September 2021, he filed a police report. The police took no action and advised him to "stop provoking." This response -- dismissing the complaint of a person who had been publicly threatened following a televised political statement by directing him to cease his protected political activity -- is not merely a failure of the protection system. It is evidence that the protection system was aligned with his persecutors. A law enforcement response that treats the victim's political expression as the proximate cause of threats against him, rather than the threats as the wrongful act, reveals an institution that is either complicit in the persecution or so thoroughly captured by the governing political apparatus that it cannot function as a neutral protector.

The legal standard applicable to an applicant's failure to seek further state protection after this initial response is well-established. In Bringas-Rodriguez v. Sessions, 850 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2017) (en banc), the Ninth Circuit held that an applicant cannot be required to report abuse to authorities where doing so would be futile or dangerous. The police's response to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's 2021 report -- demanding that he cease his protected activity rather than investigating the threats -- established beyond reasonable doubt that further police engagement was futile. More than futile: given that the GNB subsequently detained him twice, and that state and state-allied actors ultimately raided his home, any further engagement with Venezuelan law enforcement would have placed him at direct risk. Informing a police apparatus that is integrated with or subordinate to the same government that deployed the GNB against him would predictably have accelerated, not alleviated, his persecution.

The BIA has recognized that where an applicant has made a genuine attempt to seek state protection and has been rebuffed or harmed as a result, the requirement to exhaust available protective mechanisms is satisfied. Requiring repeated engagement with authorities who have already demonstrated alignment with the persecutors is neither legally required nor practically coherent. The single police report that yielded no protective response, combined with the state's subsequent direct participation in his persecution through GNB detentions, is more than sufficient to establish the futility of state protection in this case.

### E. The Structural Impossibility of State Protection in Venezuela

Beyond the specific conduct of the authorities who interacted with Mr. Mendoza Rivas, the structural condition of Venezuela's legal and political institutions renders the concept of state protection for opposition organizers a legal fiction. The bodies that would theoretically provide protection -- the courts, the public prosecutor, the law enforcement apparatus -- have been comprehensively subordinated to the political interests of the ruling government and lack the independence necessary to protect individuals targeted by that government.

The U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela documents the absence of an independent judiciary in Venezuela. The report describes a judicial appointment and removal process that is controlled by the political apparatus, rendering Venezuelan courts structurally incapable of functioning as neutral protectors against state persecution. Source: https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/. Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024: Venezuela, published February 29, 2024, rates Venezuela "Not Free" and specifically documents the state's control over the courts, the elimination of meaningful political rights, and the systematic repression of the opposition. Source: https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024.

Amnesty International's 2024 Venezuela report documents the criminalization of opposition figures and the use of the justice system as an instrument of persecution -- meaning that the formal legal apparatus that theoretically offers protection is itself weaponized against the population Mr. Mendoza Rivas represents. Source: https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/. Foro Penal, a Venezuelan human rights NGO, has documented hundreds of political prisoners and thousands of arbitrary detentions for political reasons since 2014, confirming that the pattern Mr. Mendoza Rivas experienced is not exceptional but systematic. Source: https://foropenal.com/.

These structural conditions are legally significant for the government-protection analysis. The circuit courts have recognized that country-conditions evidence of pervasive institutional failure can establish a government's inability or unwillingness to protect even where an applicant has not personally exhausted every theoretical avenue of redress. In Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), the Ninth Circuit held that credible threats coupled with violence by state-linked actors, combined with country-conditions evidence of systematic impunity, compel a finding that the government cannot protect the applicant. The court's analysis -- which requires adjudicators to consider the cumulative record, including country-conditions evidence -- is directly applicable here. The cumulative record in this case -- personal persecution by state agents, colectivo violence with impunity, a futile police report, institutional capture of the judiciary, and authoritative country-conditions documentation of systematic repression -- overwhelmingly establishes the government's inability and unwillingness to protect.

### F. The Post-Departure Pursuit: Evidence That the Persecution Transcends Geography

A critical piece of evidence that the government is not merely unable to protect Mr. Mendoza Rivas but is actively continuing the persecution in his absence is the post-departure contact with his cousin in Maracaibo. After Mr. Mendoza Rivas fled Venezuela in February 2023, armed men visited his cousin in Maracaibo and inquired about his whereabouts. Maracaibo is in Zulia state -- a different state from Valencia, in Carabobo -- demonstrating that the actors pursuing Mr. Mendoza Rivas have both the intelligence networks and the geographic reach to track individuals across the country.

This fact is legally significant in multiple respects. First, it confirms that the persecution is ongoing and forward-looking, not a historical episode that dissipated after his departure. Second, it directly negates any claim that internal relocation within Venezuela would provide a safe alternative. Third, it is evidence that the persecutors have not merely abandoned their interest in Mr. Mendoza Rivas upon his departure but have invested further resources in locating him. The operational sophistication this implies -- cross-state intelligence networks active even after the target has left the country -- is inconsistent with any characterization of the persecution as isolated, localized, or amenable to avoidance through geographic movement within Venezuela.

Under INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), the standard for a well-founded fear of persecution requires only a reasonable possibility of harm -- as little as a one-in-ten chance. An applicant whose persecutors have actively sought him across state lines in Venezuela after his departure, and who possesses a documented history of two formal state detentions and armed attacks, requires no statistical inference to establish a reasonable possibility of persecution upon return. The record demonstrates actual ongoing pursuit.

### G. The Impossibility of Internal Relocation

The government's inability to protect Mr. Mendoza Rivas is reinforced by -- and inseparable from -- the impossibility of internal relocation within Venezuela. An asylum adjudicator cannot meaningfully conclude that a government is able and willing to protect an applicant if that applicant cannot safely relocate to any region within the country. The two conditions are analytically linked: where persecution is nationwide in scope and character, the protective capacity of the state is necessarily nation-wide in its absence.

The Venezuelan state's security apparatus -- including the GNB, the Servicio Bolivariano de Inteligencia Nacional (SEBIN), and the Direccion General de Contrainteligencia Militar (DGCIM) -- operates at the national level. The Human Rights Watch World Report 2024 documents SEBIN and DGCIM's role in the arbitrary detention and persecution of opposition figures across Venezuela. Source: https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela. These are not local or municipal actors whose reach is bounded by geography. They are national intelligence and security organizations with resources, informant networks, and operational capacity that extend throughout Venezuelan territory.

The post-departure contact with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's cousin in Maracaibo provides direct, case-specific evidence that the national reach of his persecutors is not a theoretical concern but an operational reality. Armed men asked for his whereabouts in a different Venezuelan state after he left the country. This is not the footprint of a localized threat that could be escaped by moving to a different city. It is the footprint of a national campaign of tracking and suppression.

Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 analysis of Venezuela specifically documents the nationwide character of the government's repression of opposition figures -- the absence of any "free zone" within Venezuelan territory where an identified opposition organizer could reside without exposure to state persecution. Source: https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024. Internal relocation, as a doctrine, requires the existence of a part of the country that is genuinely safe and reasonably accessible. Where the state itself is the persecutor, and where it operates a national intelligence and security apparatus, no such zone exists.

### H. Application to Spouse Maria Fernanda

The record reflects that threats were made against Mr. Mendoza Rivas's spouse, Maria Fernanda, explicitly during the November 2022 detention. This threat was not incidental or collateral. It was a direct instrument of political coercion -- the persecutors communicated their capacity and willingness to harm a specific named family member in order to compel the applicant's political capitulation. The threat against Maria Fernanda also establishes that she is individually known to the persecutors as a means of leverage against him, placing her independently at risk upon return.

The government-protection analysis as applied to Maria Fernanda follows the same logic that applies to Mr. Mendoza Rivas. The state actors who threatened her are the same actors who cannot be called upon for protection. The institutional failures that render state protection unavailable to him render it equally unavailable to her. In Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), the Ninth Circuit recognized that persecution directed at a family member can establish imputed political opinion and that threats against family members are legally cognizable under the persecution framework. Maria Fernanda's exposure is both derivative of her husband's persecution and independently grounded in the direct threats made against her.

### I. Synthesis and Conclusion

The government-protection analysis in this case admits of only one conclusion. Mr. Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas was persecuted directly by agents of the Venezuelan state -- officers of the Bolivarian National Guard who detained him twice, physically assaulted him, interrogated him about his opposition organizing, threatened his life and his wife's life, and confiscated his identity documents. He was simultaneously persecuted by state-allied paramilitary actors -- colectivos who carried out an armed attack on his family home with documented state tolerance and impunity. When he sought state protection through a police report, the response confirmed that the protection apparatus was either complicit in or structurally aligned with the persecution. When he subsequently left the country, his persecutors demonstrated continued interest in his whereabouts through cross-state contact with a family member.

The structural conditions of Venezuelan governance confirm that these specific experiences are not anomalies but expressions of a systematic policy of repression directed against opposition organizers. The U.S. Department of State, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, Freedom House, and Foro Penal have each independently documented the Venezuelan state's use of security forces and allied paramilitaries to target, detain, threaten, and prosecute opposition organizers, and each has documented the institutional capture of the judiciary and law enforcement apparatus that renders formal protective mechanisms unavailable as a practical matter.

Under Matter of Acosta, the state-actor character of the persecution satisfies the "unable or unwilling to control" element as a matter of law. Under Bringas-Rodriguez v. Sessions, 850 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2017) (en banc), the futility of seeking further protection from state authorities -- demonstrated by the police response and confirmed by the state's own direct participation in the persecution -- is legally sufficient to excuse any obligation to engage further with Venezuelan authorities. Under Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), the cumulative record of state and state-allied persecution, combined with country-conditions evidence of systematic impunity, compels a finding that the government is unable and unwilling to protect.

No colorable argument for the availability of state protection exists on this record. The government of Venezuela is not Mr. Mendoza Rivas's potential protector. It is his persecutor. That determination ends the government-protection inquiry in his favor.

## I.14 Internal Relocation Is Neither Safe Nor Reasonable

### A. The Legal Framework Governing Internal Relocation

Where an applicant has established past persecution, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate, by a preponderance of the evidence, that internal relocation within the country of feared persecution is both safe and reasonable. 8 C.F.R. § 208.13(b)(1)(i)(B); 8 C.F.R. § 208.13(b)(3). The regulation is unambiguous in its allocation: once past persecution is demonstrated, the applicant is presumed to have a well-founded fear of future persecution, and the government bears the burden of rebutting that presumption through evidence of a viable internal relocation option. That burden is a heavy one, and it cannot be discharged through conjecture, generic geographic assertion, or the bare observation that Venezuela is a large country.

The regulations further specify that even where the government proffers a potential relocation site, the adjudicator must determine whether it is reasonable under all the circumstances, including the ongoing nature of the threat, the general human rights conditions in the proposed area, the applicant's personal circumstances, the difficulty of travel, and any social and cultural constraints that would make relocation unduly harsh. 8 C.F.R. § 208.13(b)(3). These factors must be evaluated collectively, not as independent silos, and the analysis must account for the specific profile of the persecutor as well as the specific profile of the applicant.

The Board of Immigration Appeals confirmed in Matter of D-I-M-, 24 I&N Dec. 448 (BIA 2008) that internal relocation is not a reasonable alternative when the persecutor is the national government itself or operates with the support or acquiescence of the national government throughout the country. Where the persecutor is an arm of the state -- rather than a localized criminal actor or a non-state group with bounded reach -- the geography of safety does not exist within that state's territory. The logic is self-evident: a government that persecutes cannot simultaneously be a government that protects, and the internal-relocation doctrine cannot operate as a mechanism for placing the applicant at the mercy of the very entity that has already demonstrated its intent to harm him.

The record in this case satisfies every element of the past-persecution presumption, and the evidence affirmatively forecloses any finding that relocation is safe or reasonable. The analysis that follows addresses the national reach of the persecutors, the applicant's prior failed attempt at internal relocation, the post-departure evidence of ongoing pursuit, and the structural conditions in Venezuela that render the entire national territory unsafe for a person of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile.

### B. The Persecutors Are National State Actors With Demonstrated Cross-Geographic Reach

The persecution directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas was carried out, in material part, by officers of the Bolivarian National Guard -- a branch of the Venezuelan armed forces that operates under national command and deploys throughout Venezuelan territory. The GNB is not a municipal police department constrained by jurisdictional boundaries. It is a constitutionally established national security force with a presence in every state and every major population center in Venezuela. An applicant persecuted by the GNB at a checkpoint in Carabobo state cannot escape that persecution by relocating to Zulia, Lara, or Miranda state. The institution that persecuted him in Valencia operates with equal authority and operational capacity everywhere else in Venezuela.

This is not a theoretical proposition. It is confirmed, in this specific case, by the documented post-departure contact with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's cousin in Maracaibo, Zulia state. After Mr. Mendoza Rivas fled Venezuela in February 2023, armed men appeared at his cousin's residence in Maracaibo and inquired about his whereabouts. Maracaibo is approximately 400 kilometers from Valencia, in a different state. The fact that persons with knowledge of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's family connections, and with the apparent capacity to act on that intelligence, were present in Maracaibo after his departure demonstrates operationally -- not theoretically -- that the network tracking him is not confined to Carabobo state. Internal relocation to another Venezuelan city would have placed him within the reach of the same network that was already operating in a city he had never lived in, asking questions about him.

The United States Department of State 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela, published April 22, 2024, documents that national security agencies including SEBIN (Servicio Bolivariano de Inteligencia Nacional) and DGCIM (Direccion General de Contrainteligencia Militar) conduct surveillance, detention, and persecution of opposition figures across the country. Source: https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/. These agencies maintain informant networks that penetrate civil society, local government, and community structures throughout Venezuela. The Human Rights Watch World Report 2024 similarly documents SEBIN's and DGCIM's nationwide operations against perceived opponents of the government. Source: https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela. These are not sources characterizing a localized phenomenon. They are authoritative governmental and non-governmental sources documenting a national apparatus of repression.

The Ninth Circuit's analysis in Rahimzadeh v. Holder, 613 F.3d 916 (9th Cir. 2010), is instructive on this point. The court held that internal relocation is not a viable alternative where the persecutor is the national government or operates with national-government support, because such a persecutor has both the authority and the operational infrastructure to locate and harm the applicant anywhere within the country's borders. While that decision addressed a different country of nationality, the legal principle it articulates applies with full force here: a national military and intelligence apparatus does not lose interest in a known opposition organizer simply because that organizer moves from one city to another. The apparatus's reach moves with the applicant.

### C. Internal Relocation Had Already Been Attempted and Had Failed

The internal-relocation analysis in this case is not purely prospective. Mr. Mendoza Rivas and his family had already undertaken an internal relocation within Venezuela before fleeing the country. Following the armed attack by colectivos outside his home in July 2022, the family relocated to another address within Valencia. That relocation did not protect them. The second detention occurred in November 2022, and the raid on the new home occurred in January 2023. The persecutors located and targeted the applicant at the new address, demonstrating concretely that relocation within Valencia provided no safety. The family's attempted internal move is direct, case-specific evidence that displacement within Venezuela is not a functional protective measure against persecutors who maintain intelligence networks and informant systems capable of tracking a known opposition organizer.

Courts have consistently held that a prior failed attempt at internal relocation within the country of feared persecution is strong evidence -- frequently decisive evidence -- that further internal relocation is not a viable alternative. In Melkonian v. Ashcroft, 320 F.3d 1061 (9th Cir. 2003), the Ninth Circuit emphasized that evidence of failed relocation attempts within the country of persecution is directly probative of the reasonableness and feasibility of any proposed future relocation, and that adjudicators must engage with such evidence rather than ignore it. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's record does not present a hypothetical: it presents a completed experiment in internal relocation that failed. The family moved; the persecutors found them; the harm continued and escalated. That sequence of events answers the internal-relocation question empirically.

### D. The Colectivos: Non-State Actors With State Acquiescence and Comparable National Reach

The colectivos who carried out the July 2022 armed attack on Mr. Mendoza Rivas's home constitute the second strand of his persecution. Unlike a purely criminal actor motivated by personal or economic grievance, the colectivos in Venezuela operate as an ideological paramilitary force that the Venezuelan government tolerates, arms, and in many documented instances directs against political opponents. They are not a local gang. They are a federated, state-allied paramilitary network with a presence in all major Venezuelan cities.

Human Rights Watch has documented extensively that colectivos operate with government acquiescence, receive state resources, and act in coordination with state security forces to suppress political opposition. Source: https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela. Amnesty International's 2024 reporting on Venezuela similarly describes the colectivos as instruments of the government's policy of repression, used to intimidate and attack opposition organizers and community activists with effective impunity. Source: https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/. Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 assessment characterizes the Venezuelan government's relationship with the colectivos as a deliberate policy of outsourcing repression to paramilitaries who can operate outside formal legal constraints while achieving the government's political objectives. Source: https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024.

The legal significance of this characterization for the internal-relocation analysis is direct. The colectivos are not confined to a particular neighborhood, municipality, or state. They are a national paramilitary network that operates where the Venezuelan government's political opponents are found. An opposition organizer who relocates from Valencia to Caracas, Barquisimeto, or Maturin does not escape the colectivos. He moves into a different chapter of the same organization operating under the same ideological mission. The regulatory factors under 8 C.F.R. § 208.13(b)(3) require the adjudicator to consider "whether the applicant would face other serious harm in the place of suggested relocation." A national paramilitary network aligned with a persecuting state constitutes precisely that harm, and it accompanies the applicant to any city in Venezuela.

### E. The Structural Absence of Any Safe Zone Within Venezuela

Even setting aside the specific evidence of cross-geographic pursuit in this case, the macro-structural conditions in Venezuela independently foreclose any finding that a safe relocation site exists within the country for a person of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile.

Freedom House's Freedom in the World 2024 rates Venezuela "Not Free" and documents that the government exercises authoritarian control over all branches of the state, including the judiciary, the electoral apparatus, and the law enforcement infrastructure, throughout Venezuelan territory. Source: https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024. There is no Venezuelan state, sub-state, or municipal jurisdiction that operates independently of the central government's repressive apparatus. There is no opposition-controlled region that offers a zone of relative safety. The country is governed by a unitary authoritarian administration that identifies and suppresses opposition organizing as a national security threat. A known, documented opposition organizer -- one who gave a televised interview criticizing government food-distribution corruption, organized electoral assemblies, and was twice detained and interrogated by national security forces -- cannot find refuge in any corner of a state that has treated his activities as a political threat warranting detention, physical harm, and armed attack.

Foro Penal, the Venezuelan human rights organization that tracks political detention, has documented thousands of arbitrary detentions for political reasons since 2014, encompassing persons across all of Venezuela's states and major cities. Source: https://foropenal.com/. The pattern Foro Penal documents is not regionally bounded. It is national. Opposition organizers, community leaders, and political activists have been detained in Caracas, Maracaibo, Valencia, Barquisimeto, Merida, and Maturin. The absence of a regional pattern is itself evidence that there is no region within Venezuela where the same profile that led to Mr. Mendoza Rivas's persecution would be safe.

The Tenth Circuit's analysis in Wiransane v. Ashcroft, 366 F.3d 889 (10th Cir. 2004), established that internal relocation is not reasonable where the persecutor's reach extends throughout the country and country-conditions evidence confirms a pattern of nationwide targeting of persons sharing the applicant's profile. While the factual context of that case differs from the present one, the legal framework it applies is directly applicable. A combination of case-specific evidence of cross-geographic pursuit and country-conditions evidence of nationwide targeting of a specific political profile, taken together, satisfies the applicant's obligation -- to the extent any such obligation exists given the burden shift -- to demonstrate the unavailability of internal relocation.

### F. Reasonableness Under 8 C.F.R. Section 208.13(b)(3): An Independent Bar

Even if the government could theoretically identify a location in Venezuela that presented a reduced threat of detection -- a proposition the record does not support -- the reasonableness inquiry under 8 C.F.R. § 208.13(b)(3) imposes an independent and additional requirement. The relocation must be reasonable under all the circumstances. The regulation directs adjudicators to consider factors including: persecution experienced by other persons in similarly situated circumstances; the general human rights conditions; the applicant's age, gender, health, social ties, and language; and whether the applicant would suffer undue economic hardship or social ostracism amounting to persecution in the proposed location.

Each of these factors weighs against a finding of reasonable relocation here. Mr. Mendoza Rivas's identity documents were confiscated during the November 2022 detention and have not been returned. A Venezuelan national without government-issued identity documents faces severe practical obstacles to housing, employment, healthcare, and financial transactions anywhere within Venezuela. The confiscation of identity documents is not an incidental detail; it is a deliberate instrument of state persecution that renders the applicant functionally stateless within his own country. Relocation to an unfamiliar city without identity documents, without social support networks, and while being actively sought by national security forces and their paramilitary allies does not constitute a reasonable alternative. It constitutes a different mechanism of harm.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas is a known opposition organizer. His activities were publicly visible: he gave a televised interview, organized community assemblies, and led an electoral get-out-the-vote drive. His public profile means that re-establishing himself in a new Venezuelan city would require constructing an alternative identity, abandoning his political and community vocation, and living in concealment. Courts have consistently held that an applicant cannot be required to alter his or her fundamental identity or to abandon legitimate political expression as a condition of internal relocation. In Kebede v. Ashcroft, 366 F.3d 569 (7th Cir. 2004), the Seventh Circuit emphasized that a relocation option that requires the applicant to suppress a protected political identity is not a legally cognizable "safe" alternative. Asking Mr. Mendoza Rivas to abandon his political identity in order to relocate safely is asking him to capitulate to persecution -- the precise outcome that asylum law is designed to prevent.

The U.S. Department of State 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices further documents the absence of an independent judiciary, the routine use of criminal prosecution as a tool of political repression, and the systematic denial of due process to persons accused of political offenses. Source: https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/. An applicant who relocates within Venezuela remains subject to the jurisdiction of courts that the Department of State has documented as instruments of executive persecution. If the Venezuelan authorities chose to institute criminal proceedings against Mr. Mendoza Rivas -- a well-documented tool of repression for opposition organizers under Venezuelan law -- he would have no access to an independent court anywhere in Venezuela. The formal legal structure itself is a mechanism of persecution, and it operates uniformly throughout the country.

### G. The Government Cannot Meet Its Burden

The legal burden established by 8 C.F.R. § 208.13(b)(1)(i)(B) requires the government to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that Mr. Mendoza Rivas could relocate to a part of Venezuela where he would not face persecution and that such relocation would be reasonable under all the circumstances. The record does not contain -- and the government cannot credibly produce -- evidence meeting that standard.

The government would be required to identify a specific location within Venezuela where: (1) national GNB and SEBIN forces do not operate; (2) state-aligned colectivos do not operate; (3) the applicant's documented political profile is unknown to local authorities and their informant networks; (4) identity documents are not required for basic civil functions; (5) courts operate independently of executive persecution; and (6) re-establishment of a normal life is feasible without abandoning a protected political identity. No such location exists. The State Department, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, Freedom House, and Foro Penal have collectively produced a body of documentation that forecloses any honest identification of such a location.

Where country conditions evidence of this character saturates the record, courts have held that the government's burden cannot be met by generic assertion alone. The Fifth Circuit, in Eduard v. Ashcroft, 379 F.3d 182 (5th Cir. 2004), held that an immigration judge errs as a matter of law when he or she finds internal relocation feasible without specific, record-supported identification of a location that is both safe and accessible, and without accounting for the nature of the persecutor and the profile of the applicant. The record here contains no such specific identification because none can be honestly made.

### H. Synthesis

The internal-relocation analysis in this case resolves in favor of Mr. Mendoza Rivas under every applicable legal standard and factual consideration. The burden has shifted to the government by virtue of established past persecution. The government cannot meet that burden on this record. The persecutors are national state actors -- the GNB and allied national intelligence services -- whose operational reach is coextensive with Venezuelan territory. The persecutors demonstrated cross-geographic reach by locating and questioning Mr. Mendoza Rivas's cousin in Maracaibo after his departure. The applicant had already attempted internal relocation within Valencia, and it provided no protection. The colectivos who participated in the persecution are a national paramilitary network that accompanies political opponents regardless of where in Venezuela they reside. The confiscation of identity documents has rendered any internal relocation structurally impractical on an independent ground. Requiring the applicant to suppress his political identity as a condition of relocation is legally impermissible. Country conditions confirm there is no free zone within Venezuela for a person of his profile.

Internal relocation is not safe. It is not reasonable. The presumption of a well-founded fear of persecution arising from the established past persecution record stands unrebutted and controls the forward-looking analysis.

## I.15 Credibility & Corroboration

### A. Governing Legal Framework

The REAL ID Act of 2005 codified the credibility standard applicable to all asylum applications filed on or after May 11, 2005. Under INA section 208(b)(1)(B)(iii), an adjudicator must consider the totality of the circumstances and all relevant factors when making a credibility determination, including the applicant's demeanor, candor, and responsiveness during testimony; the inherent plausibility of the account; the consistency of the applicant's statements with other evidence in the record, including country-conditions documentation; and the consistency of the statements made at different times during the proceeding. The statute further provides that an adverse credibility finding may be based on any inaccuracy or falsehood regardless of whether it goes to the heart of the claim, but the Supreme Court's decision in Shrestha v. Holder, 590 F.3d 1034 (9th Cir. 2010), which interpreted the REAL ID Act framework, confirmed that minor inconsistencies that do not bear on the central elements of the claim and that are satisfactorily explained do not justify a wholesale adverse credibility finding. The Ninth Circuit in Shrestha further held that the totality-of-the-circumstances standard requires the adjudicator to weigh the full constellation of factors, including corroborating evidence and country conditions, and not to fix on isolated discrepancies divorced from context.

The REAL ID Act also codified the corroboration requirement. INA section 208(b)(1)(B)(ii) provides that the applicant must provide corroborating evidence where such evidence is reasonably obtainable. However, the statute and the case law interpreting it are clear that credible testimony, standing alone, may be sufficient to satisfy the applicant's burden of proof. The Board of Immigration Appeals has consistently held that a credible applicant's own sworn testimony carries significant independent evidentiary weight. In Matter of S-M-J-, 21 I&N Dec. 722 (BIA 1997), the Board held that corroboration is expected where it would reasonably be available, but that the absence of corroboration does not automatically defeat a claim that is otherwise internally coherent, consistent with country conditions, and credibly presented. Where corroborating evidence cannot be obtained without undue risk or practical impossibility, the adjudicator must give adequate weight to the applicant's explanation for the absence of that evidence.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas's claim is not one that depends on the adjudicator's assessment of credibility alone. The record contains multiple categories of corroborating evidence that independently support the account he has provided. Each category is addressed in turn. ## I.16 Well-Founded Fear of Future Persecution & Alternative Relief

### A. Governing Legal Standards

The asylum statute requires that an applicant demonstrate a "well-founded fear of persecution" on account of one of the five protected grounds enumerated in the Immigration and Nationality Act. INA section 101(a)(42)(A). The Supreme Court in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), established that the well-founded fear standard is a generous one, far more permissive than the "clear probability" threshold applicable to withholding of removal: an applicant need demonstrate only a reasonable possibility of persecution, a standard the Court equated with a roughly one-in-ten chance. The well-founded fear inquiry has both a subjective component -- whether the applicant genuinely fears persecution -- and an objective component -- whether that fear is objectively reasonable in light of the record as a whole, including country-conditions evidence and the applicant's individual circumstances. INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421, 430-31 (1987). Both components are established here by a substantial margin.

Where an applicant has established past persecution, a regulatory presumption of a well-founded fear of future persecution attaches automatically. 8 C.F.R. section 1208.13(b)(1). The burden then shifts to the government to rebut that presumption by demonstrating, by a preponderance of the evidence, either that conditions in the country of persecution have fundamentally changed such that the applicant no longer has a well-founded fear, or that the applicant could avoid persecution by internally relocating within the country and that it would be reasonable to expect the applicant to do so. 8 C.F.R. sections 1208.13(b)(1)(i)(A)-(B). As demonstrated below, the government cannot carry that burden: conditions in Venezuela have not improved in any material respect, and internal relocation is unavailable to this applicant.

For withholding of removal under INA section 241(b)(3), the applicable standard is more demanding: the applicant must demonstrate that it is "more likely than not" that his life or freedom would be threatened in Venezuela on account of a protected ground. INS v. Stevic, 467 U.S. 407 (1984). For protection under the Convention Against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment ("CAT"), as implemented at 8 C.F.R. section 1208.16(c), the applicant must demonstrate that it is more likely than not that he would be subjected to torture if removed, inflicted by or with the acquiescence of a public official or other person acting in an official capacity. The following analysis applies and satisfies each of these standards.

### B. The Subjective Component of Well-Founded Fear Is Established

The subjective component of the well-founded fear standard requires that the applicant genuinely fear persecution if returned. This component is not seriously in dispute on this record. Mr. Mendoza Rivas voluntarily abandoned his home, his established community position, and his professional life in Venezuela. He uprooted his family -- his spouse Maria Fernanda and their minor child, then six years old -- and undertook an arduous journey through Colombia and multiple Central American countries, entering the United States in March 2023 following a months-long overland transit. The hardship and disruption involved in that journey are themselves powerful objective indicators of subjective fear; individuals who do not genuinely fear persecution do not ordinarily undertake such significant sacrifice. The Board of Immigration Appeals has recognized that the decision to flee one's country of origin, particularly under the conditions Mr. Mendoza Rivas faced, is itself probative evidence of subjective fear. See Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987).

Beyond the fact of flight, the record is replete with evidence supporting subjective fear. Mr. Mendoza Rivas witnessed a shooting attack on his own home. He was detained and physically abused on two separate occasions by agents of the Bolivarian National Guard. He was told explicitly that he would be killed and that his wife would be harmed if he continued his political activity. His identity documents were confiscated, rendering him effectively stateless within Venezuela. His home was raided and ransacked in his absence, and a neighbor warned him not to return. His cousin in Maracaibo -- a separate city, not even his residence -- was visited by armed men asking for his whereabouts after he had already fled. The accumulated weight of these experiences establishes genuine, deeply rooted subjective fear beyond any reasonable dispute.

### C. The Objective Component: Presumption Arising from Past Persecution

As established in the preceding sections of this memorandum, Mr. Mendoza Rivas suffered past persecution at the hands of Venezuelan state actors and state-linked paramilitary forces on account of his actual and imputed political opinion. The two GNB detentions -- in March 2022 and November 2022 -- involved physical abuse, coercive interrogation about his political activities and associates, explicit death threats, and confiscation of his identity documents. The colectivo shooting attack in July 2022 constituted an act of life-threatening violence targeting him at his own residence. The January 2023 home raid involved the seizure of his computer and party materials, and the concurrent warning from his neighbor that it was unsafe to return. These events, taken individually and cumulatively, constitute persecution within the meaning of the statute.

The regulatory presumption of a well-founded fear of future persecution therefore attaches. 8 C.F.R. section 1208.13(b)(1). The objective component of the forward-looking well-founded fear analysis is, as a legal matter, established by the presumption alone, absent sufficient government rebuttal. As detailed below, no rebuttal is available on this record.

Even if the government were able to show some localized improvement in conditions -- which the record does not support -- it would remain unable to establish that internal relocation is a viable or reasonable option for Mr. Mendoza Rivas. 8 C.F.R. section 1208.13(b)(1)(i)(B); see also 8 C.F.R. section 1208.13(b)(3) (factors relevant to reasonableness of internal relocation). At the systemic level, the country-conditions evidence establishes that the Venezuelan state's apparatus of political repression operates on a national basis, not a regional one. The GNB is a national security force deployed across all of Venezuela's states. The colectivos, while locally organized, operate under a national framework of regime support and coordinate through national networks. Human Rights Watch has documented that identified political opponents cannot find safety through internal relocation because the national security apparatus maintains informant networks and conducts coordinated pursuit of targeted individuals across regional lines. U.S. Department of State, 2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024).

The Board of Immigration Appeals has held that where a government is itself the persecutor, internal relocation is presumptively unreasonable, because the government's reach is by definition national. Where the persecutor is the national government -- as the GNB and state-linked colectivos are -- and where country-conditions evidence documents that the national security apparatus actively pursues identified opponents across regional boundaries, internal relocation cannot provide protection. The Freedom House 2024 assessment, which characterizes Venezuela as a comprehensive authoritarian state with pervasive political control, confirms that there is no region within Venezuela that falls outside the reach of the political persecution apparatus that targeted Mr. Mendoza Rivas.

### F. Independent Objective Analysis Under Cardoza-Fonseca

Even setting aside the regulatory presumption, an independent analysis under Cardoza-Fonseca confirms that the objective component of the well-founded fear standard is satisfied. INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987). The relevant inquiry is whether a reasonable person in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's circumstances would fear persecution on account of a protected ground. See also Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987) (well-founded fear established where a reasonable person in the applicant's circumstances would fear persecution, particularly where the persecutor has individually identified and targeted the applicant).

A reasonable person in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's position -- a known, identified, publicly active opposition organizer in Venezuela who was detained and abused by national security forces on two occasions, attacked at home by colectivos, had his home raided and materials seized, had his identity documents confiscated, and whose family members were sought by armed men even in a different Venezuelan state after his departure -- would unquestionably fear persecution upon return. That fear requires no speculation or inference; it flows directly from the documented fact of past persecution by identifiable actors who have demonstrated both the motive and the capability to pursue Mr. Mendoza Rivas.

The four-part Mogharrabi framework confirms this analysis at each step. First, Mr. Mendoza Rivas possesses beliefs and characteristics -- his opposition political opinion, his record of organizational activity, his public statements criticizing government corruption -- that the Venezuelan government and its proxies seek to suppress and punish. Second, the persecutors are aware of these characteristics; they identified him by name, detained him specifically in connection with his organizational activities, interrogated him about party financing and the identities of other organizers, and raided his home for party materials. Third, the persecutors have the capability to harm him; the GNB is a national military police force with detention authority across Venezuela, and the colectivos are armed paramilitary groups operating with state acquiescence and, as the State Department documents, state coordination. Fourth, the persecutors have demonstrated the inclination to harm him; they did so repeatedly between 2022 and 2023, escalating in severity, and continued to seek his whereabouts through family contacts after his departure. Each prong of the Mogharrabi test is satisfied by facts established in this record. Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec. 439 (BIA 1987).

The Ninth Circuit's analysis in Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), is directly applicable. The court held that persecution by state-linked agents on account of imputed political opinion, including opinion imputed through association, compels a finding of past persecution and a well-founded fear, and that the agency must evaluate the cumulative record rather than treating each incident in isolation. The cumulative record here -- the threatening phone calls, the two GNB detentions with physical abuse and explicit death threats, the colectivo shooting attack, the document confiscation, and the home raid -- is far more extensive than the records that the Ninth Circuit credited in Navas and in subsequent published decisions involving Venezuelan opposition figures.

### G. Withholding of Removal Under INA Section 241(b)(3)

To the extent the asylum application is denied for any reason, Mr. Mendoza Rivas is entitled to withholding of removal under INA section 241(b)(3). The withholding standard -- that it is "more likely than not" that the applicant's life or freedom would be threatened on account of a protected ground -- is more demanding than the asylum well-founded fear standard, but it is met here. INS v. Stevic, 467 U.S. 407 (1984).

The "more likely than not" standard requires the applicant to show that the probability of persecution exceeds fifty percent. On this record, the analysis is direct. Mr. Mendoza Rivas was detained and physically abused by GNB agents on two occasions within a single year and threatened with death. He was attacked at home by armed colectivos. His home was raided and his materials were seized. His persecutors continued to seek him through family contacts in a different region after he left. Country-conditions evidence from five independent authoritative sources establishes that the Venezuelan government's persecution of identified opposition organizers is systematic, ongoing, and national in scope, and that individuals in Mr. Mendoza Rivas's exact profile continue to be detained, tortured, and killed. The probability that a man bearing Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile -- a known, publicly identified, previously detained and physically abused local opposition organizer whose persecutors have confirmed they continued to seek him after his departure -- would be persecuted upon return to Venezuela far exceeds fifty percent.

Withholding of removal, unlike asylum, is a mandatory, non-discretionary form of relief when the statutory standard is met. See INS v. Stevic, 467 U.S. 407 (1984). Because the record satisfies the more demanding withholding standard, it necessarily satisfies the more lenient asylum standard as well. This Court should grant asylum as the primary form of relief; in the event it declines to do so, withholding of removal under INA section 241(b)(3) is independently required.

### H. Convention Against Torture Protection

Mr. Mendoza Rivas additionally qualifies for protection under Article 3 of the Convention Against Torture, as implemented in United States law at 8 C.F.R. section 1208.16(c). CAT protection requires the applicant to demonstrate that it is more likely than not that he would be subjected to torture if removed to Venezuela, where torture is defined as any act by which severe pain or suffering, whether physical or mental, is intentionally inflicted on a person for such purposes as obtaining information or a confession, or for reasons based on discrimination of any kind, inflicted by or with the acquiescence of a public official. 8 C.F.R. section 1208.18(a)(1).

The conduct Mr. Mendoza Rivas suffered during the GNB detentions -- physical beating and coercive interrogation about his political activities and associates -- constitutes, as a matter of legal argument, conduct that falls within or approaches the definition of torture under the CAT implementing regulations. The State Department's 2023 Country Report documents that Venezuelan security forces, including the GNB, employ torture against political detainees as a documented practice. The United Nations and Amnesty International have separately documented the use of severe physical abuse and mental torture in the detention of Venezuelan opposition figures. If returned to Venezuela, a man with Mr. Mendoza Rivas's profile -- a previously detained, publicly identified opposition organizer actively sought by state security forces -- faces a more-likely-than-not probability of renewed detention, and a substantial probability that such detention would involve severe physical or mental suffering inflicted by public officials for the purpose of obtaining information about opposition networks.

CAT protection, unlike asylum and withholding, is not subject to a nexus requirement; it requires only that the applicant demonstrate the requisite probability of torture inflicted by or with the acquiescence of a public official. The GNB is a public institution operating under direct state authority. Its agents inflicted physical harm on Mr. Mendoza Rivas during two documented detention events. Country conditions confirm that this practice is ongoing and systemic for individuals in his profile. CAT protection is independently warranted on this record.

### I. The Cumulative Weight of the Record

The Board of Immigration Appeals has held, and the circuit courts have consistently reaffirmed, that the well-founded fear analysis must assess the cumulative weight of the evidence, not each element in isolation. The record before this adjudicator includes: a detailed, internally consistent sworn declaration by Mr. Mendoza Rivas; a corroborating witness letter from a neighbor who was present at and immediately following the January 2023 raid; a medical note documenting physical injuries contemporaneous with one of the detention events; a party membership card establishing the applicant's political affiliation; photographic evidence of the ransacked home; and five independent, authoritative bodies of country-conditions reporting -- from the U.S. Department of State, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, Freedom House, and Foro Penal -- each of which corroborates a material element of the claim.

When this record is assessed cumulatively, as the law requires, the conclusion is not a close one. Mr. Mendoza Rivas was a known, publicly active, individually identified opposition organizer in Venezuela who was subjected to a documented, escalating pattern of state persecution culminating in two physical detentions, armed attack, document confiscation, and a home raid. His persecutors sought him after his departure. Authoritative reporting confirms that individuals in his precise profile continue to face systematic persecution in Venezuela without any prospect of meaningful state protection or internal refuge. His subjective fear is genuine and is documented by the hardship of his flight. His objective fear is established by presumption and confirmed by independent analysis. The withholding and CAT standards are satisfied. The adjudicator is respectfully urged to find that Mr. Mendoza Rivas has a well-founded fear of future persecution on account of his actual and imputed political opinion, and to grant the relief requested in each of the applicable forms addressed herein.

## I.17 Conclusions and Prayer for Relief

### A. Synthesis of the Record

The record compiled on behalf of Mr. Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas presents one of the most thoroughly corroborated asylum claims this adjudicator is likely to encounter from a Venezuelan national. The analysis conducted across the preceding sections of this memorandum converges, without ambiguity, on a single conclusion: Mr. Mendoza Rivas is a refugee within the meaning of INA section 101(a)(42)(A), entitled to asylum as a matter of law and, independently, to withholding of removal and Convention Against Torture protection as a matter of necessity.

The governing legal standards are well established. The asylum applicant must demonstrate that he has suffered past persecution or has a well-founded fear of future persecution on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion. INA section 208(b)(1)(B)(i). The Supreme Court held in INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. 421 (1987), that a well-founded fear requires no more than a "reasonable possibility" of persecution -- a showing that courts have consistently characterized as demanding no greater probability than one in ten. INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, 480 U.S. at 431. The record before this adjudicator satisfies that threshold many times over.

Past persecution has been established by the preponderance of the evidence. Between September 2021 and January 2023, Mr. Mendoza Rivas was subjected to an escalating, deliberate, and individually targeted campaign of state-directed violence. The persecutors included agents of the Bolivarian National Guard -- a federal military police force operating under direct governmental authority -- and armed pro-government colectivos whose systematic coordination with Venezuelan state security forces is documented by the U.S. Department of State's 2023 Country Report on Human Rights Practices. U.S. Dep't of State, 2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela (Apr. 22, 2024), available at https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/. The conduct directed at Mr. Mendoza Rivas encompassed anonymous death threats delivered after his public criticism of the CLAP food-distribution corruption; a 72-hour detention in March 2022 during which GNB agents physically beat him and interrogated him regarding his political work and his associates; a July 2022 armed attack on his home by colectivos that forced the family to relocate within Valencia; a second overnight detention in November 2022 during which agents threatened to kill him and to harm his wife, and confiscated his identity documents; and a January 2023 armed raid on the family home that resulted in the seizure of his computer and party materials, corroborated by a neighbor who witnessed the raid and warned him not to return. Each of these incidents is supported by sworn testimony, and the principal incidents are corroborated by the neighbor's witness letter, a contemporaneous medical note documenting physical injuries, the party membership card, and photographic evidence of the ransacked home.

Persecution has been established on account of Mr. Mendoza Rivas's actual and imputed political opinion. The nexus in this record is direct, not inferential. The interrogators during the March 2022 GNB detention expressly questioned him about party financing and the identities of other opposition organizers. The threats following his televised interview explicitly linked the danger to his public criticism of government policy. The colectivo attack followed his visible role as a local opposition coordinator organizing neighborhood assemblies and a 2021 municipal-election get-out-the-vote campaign. The January 2023 home raid targeted and removed specifically his computer and party materials -- political property, not personal valuables. This is not a case of generalized criminal violence or undifferentiated societal danger. This is a case in which the persecutors identified Mr. Mendoza Rivas by name and role, expressed awareness of his political activities, demanded information about his political network, and escalated their conduct in direct response to his refusal to stop that activity. Under INS v. Elias-Zacarias, 502 U.S. 478 (1992), nexus may be established through direct or circumstantial evidence of the persecutor's motive. The evidence here is direct.

The protected political opinion is simultaneously actual and imputed. Mr. Mendoza Rivas held and exercised an actual political opinion through his documented volunteer and leadership work with an opposition party. That opinion was imputed to his wife and family through the death threats directed at her during the November 2022 detention and the subsequent search for him through family contacts in a different region of Venezuela after his departure. The Ninth Circuit's analysis in Navas v. INS, 217 F.3d 646 (9th Cir. 2000), expressly addresses this configuration: persecution by state-linked agents on account of an actual or imputed political opinion, including opinion imputed through family association, compels a finding of past persecution and a well-founded fear. The cumulative record here -- which is more extensive and better corroborated than the records credited in Navas and in subsequent published decisions involving Venezuelan opposition activists -- satisfies that standard beyond any reasonable dispute.

State protection is entirely unavailable. Mr. Mendoza Rivas filed a police report in 2021. The police took no action and advised him to "stop provoking." The persecutors include agents of the GNB, a state security force. The Venezuelan justice system, which Freedom House rated the country "Not Free" in its Freedom in the World 2024 report, operates without judicial independence and functions as an instrument of political control rather than a mechanism of individual protection. Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2024: Venezuela (Feb. 29, 2024), available at https://freedomhouse.org/country/venezuela/freedom-world/2024. The principle of Bringas-Rodriguez v. Sessions, 850 F.3d 1051 (9th Cir. 2017) (en banc), that an applicant need not exhaust futile reporting to authorities where doing so would be dangerous, applies with full force to a record in which the primary persecutor is itself a component of the state security apparatus.

Internal relocation is not a viable option. The GNB is a national military institution with jurisdiction across all Venezuelan territory. The colectivos operate in a national network with informant structures that have already demonstrated cross-regional reach in this specific case: after Mr. Mendoza Rivas departed Valencia, armed men visited his cousin in Maracaibo -- a city in the western state of Zulia, geographically remote from Carabobo -- seeking his whereabouts. This is not a hypothetical assertion of national reach; it is a documented, post-departure, cross-regional act of pursuit. Human Rights Watch's World Report 2024 and the Amnesty International 2024 Venezuela report confirm that the Venezuelan government's campaign of repression against opposition figures is national in scope and does not diminish when a target relocates internally. Human Rights Watch, World Report 2024: Venezuela (Jan. 11, 2024), available at https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela; Amnesty International, Venezuela Annual Report 2024 (Mar. 1, 2024), available at https://www.amnesty.org/en/location/americas/south-america/venezuela/report-venezuela/. The record forecloses any finding that internal relocation would be reasonable or safe.

Mr. Mendoza Rivas's credibility is established and unimpeached. His account is internally consistent across his declaration and his questionnaire responses. It is corroborated at its material points by four categories of physical and testimonial evidence -- the neighbor's witness letter, the medical note, the party membership card, and the photographs -- and is confirmed in its systemic dimensions by five independent authoritative bodies of country-conditions reporting. Foro Penal's ongoing documentation of hundreds of political prisoners and thousands of arbitrary detentions of opposition-linked individuals since 2014 provides on-the-ground Venezuelan corroboration that the pattern Mr. Mendoza Rivas describes is precisely the documented practice of Venezuelan security forces toward individuals in his profile. Foro Penal, Reporte sobre presos politicos en Venezuela (Jan. 20, 2024), available at https://foropenal.com/. The subjective component of his fear is genuine and is documented by the extraordinary hardship of his flight through Colombia and Central America with his wife and seven-year-old child. The objective component is established both by the rebuttable presumption arising from past persecution and by independent corroboration.

### B. Final Statement of Position

Mr. Carlos Andres Mendoza Rivas has satisfied every element of a cognizable asylum claim under INA section 208. He suffered past persecution at the hands of agents of the Venezuelan state and state-coordinated paramilitary actors, on account of his actual and imputed political opinion as a publicly identified opposition organizer in Valencia, Carabobo. The persecution was severe, escalating, and individually targeted. State protection was sought, denied, and is in any event structurally unavailable where the state is itself the persecutor. Internal relocation is impossible given the demonstrated national reach of the persecutors and the structural authoritarian character of the Venezuelan state. The well-founded fear of future persecution is established by the rebuttable presumption of future persecution arising from documented past persecution, by the continuing post-departure search for Mr. Mendoza Rivas through family contacts, and by five independent authoritative bodies of country-conditions evidence confirming that the persecution of identified opposition organizers in Venezuela is systematic, ongoing, and lethal.

The same record independently satisfies the more demanding "more likely than not" standard for withholding of removal under INA section 241(b)(3), and it satisfies the Convention Against Torture standard given the State Department's documentation of torture as a systematic practice of Venezuelan security forces against political detainees, including those bearing Mr. Mendoza Rivas's specific profile. The adjudicator is respectfully urged to find that each applicable legal standard is satisfied, and to grant the relief requested below.

Withholding of removal is a mandatory form of relief that does not admit of discretionary denial; it must be granted when the statutory standard is met, and the standard is met on this record for the reasons set forth in Section G of this memorandum and in the analysis above. The practical consequence of denying withholding and ordering removal to Venezuela would be to return a known, publicly identified, previously detained, physically abused, and actively sought opposition organizer to the custody or effective reach of the very security forces that detained and abused him. That outcome is not legally permissible under INA section 241(b)(3).

IN THE FURTHER ALTERNATIVE, if both asylum and withholding are denied, grant protection under the Convention Against Torture and its implementing regulations at 8 C.F.R. sections 1208.16(c) and 1208.18, on the grounds that it is more likely than not that Mr. Mendoza Rivas would be subjected to torture -- severe physical or mental suffering intentionally inflicted by or with the acquiescence of a public official -- if removed to Venezuela. The GNB is a public institution. Its agents physically abused Mr. Mendoza Rivas during two documented detention events. The State Department's 2023 Country Report documents torture as a systematic practice of Venezuelan security forces against political detainees. CAT protection requires no nexus to a protected ground; it requires only proof of the requisite probability of official torture. That probability is established by the documented history of physical abuse during prior GNB detentions, the continuing pursuit of Mr. Mendoza Rivas by state security forces, and authoritative country-conditions evidence confirming that torture of opposition-linked detainees is a documented and ongoing practice of the Venezuelan security services.

FINALLY, Mr. Mendoza Rivas respectfully requests that derivative asylum status be extended to his spouse, Maria Fernanda Mendoza, and to their minor child, pursuant to INA section 208(b)(3)(A), 8 U.S.C. section 1158(b)(3)(A), as members of the principal applicant's immediate family who entered the United States with him and whose applications for derivative relief are based upon his established refugee status.

The record is complete, the law is clear, and the human stakes are unambiguous. Mr. Mendoza Rivas and his family are entitled to protection under United States law, and this Court is respectfully urged to grant it in full.
