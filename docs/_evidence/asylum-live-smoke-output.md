## I.11 Nexus & Application of Controlling Federal Precedent

### A. Legal Standard: The "On Account Of" Requirement

To establish eligibility for asylum under § 208 of the Immigration and Nationality Act, an applicant must demonstrate that the persecution he suffered or fears is "on account of" one of five protected grounds. *See* 8 U.S.C. § 1101(a)(42)(A). For persecution on account of **political opinion**, the applicant need not prove that political opinion is the *sole* reason for the persecution; he must demonstrate only that it was *at least one central reason* for the persecutor's conduct. *See* 8 U.S.C. § 1158(b)(1)(B)(i); *Barajas-Romero v. Lynch*, 846 F.3d 351, 360 (9th Cir. 2017) (distinguishing the "one central reason" standard from a "mixed motive" analysis). The applicant need not have explicitly expressed his political opinion if the persecutor imputes such opinion to him. *See INS v. Elias-Zacarias*, 502 U.S. 478, 482–83 (1992) (recognizing that imputed political opinion satisfies the nexus requirement). As set forth below, Mr. Méndez's record overwhelmingly establishes that the GNB's campaign of detention, torture, and threats against him was centrally — indeed exclusively — motivated by his identity and role as an opposition rally organizer.

### B. The Nexus Is Direct, Documented, and Institutionally Confirmed

#### 1. The GNB's Conduct Was Targeted, Not Incidental

The nexus between Mr. Méndez's protected ground and his persecution is not circumstantial — it is structural. Mr. Méndez organized opposition rallies in Caracas continuously from 2021 through early 2023. The GNB detained him in **May 2022** for 72 hours and again in **November 2022** — on both occasions, agents beat him and threatened to kill him. His home was raided in **January 2023**. Each episode was temporally proximate to his opposition organizing activity and directed at him specifically in his capacity as an organizer. This is not a case of generalized civil unrest affecting all Venezuelans indiscriminately; the detentions were targeted, sequential, and escalating — the hallmarks of deliberate political persecution rather than random or criminal violence.

This factual pattern is precisely what the UN Independent International Fact-Finding Mission on Venezuela identified as a defining feature of state conduct during this period. The Mission's December 2025 report concludes that the GNB bears institutional responsibility for **systematic violations amounting to crimes against humanity**, including the persecution of individuals on **political grounds**, and that this conduct reflects a **centralized chain of command reaching the President**. *See* OHCHR, *Venezuela: Bolivarian National Guard Responsible for Systematic Violations and Crimes Against Humanity* (Dec. 11, 2025), https://www.ohchr.org/en/press-releases/2025/12/venezuela-bolivarian-national-guard-responsible-systematic-violations-and. That the GNB — the identical institutional actor that detained, beat, and threatened Mr. Méndez — has been formally identified by a UN treaty body as the principal instrument of political persecution forecloses any argument that his suffering was random, incidental, or unconnected to his political activity. The persecutor and the motive are both named by the highest international authority available.

#### 2. The Pattern of Selective, Targeted Persecution of Opposition Organizers Confirms Nexus

Federal courts and BIA precedent recognize that nexus may be established where an applicant demonstrates membership in a class that has been singled out for persecution. *See Matter of Acosta*, 19 I&N Dec. 211, 233 (BIA 1985) (defining persecution "on account of" as requiring that the protected characteristic be the reason for the harm). Human Rights Watch's *World Report 2024* documents that during the precise window of 2021–2023, the Maduro government shifted from mass street repression to **selective, targeted persecution of opposition organizers**, including through surveillance, arbitrary arrests, and criminalization — with over **15,800 politically motivated arrests** recorded since 2014. *See* Human Rights Watch, *World Report 2024: Venezuela* (Jan. 11, 2024), https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/venezuela. Mr. Méndez, as a documented organizer of opposition rallies in Caracas during this same period, is a textbook member of the class of persons that the Venezuelan state has systematically targeted. His two detentions and home raid fall squarely within the documented pattern HRW describes. Under *Elias-Zacarias* and its progeny, where an applicant's profile matches a documented pattern of politically motivated persecution, nexus is established. *See* 502 U.S. at 482–83.

#### 3. The GNB's Threats and Statements Evidence the Persecutor's Motive

Courts have long held that a persecutor's own statements and conduct are among the most probative evidence of motive. *See Navas v. INS*, 217 F.3d 646, 658 (9th Cir. 2000) ("[T]he persecutor's motive is a factual question."). Here, during both the May 2022 and November 2022 detentions, GNB agents did not merely detain Mr. Méndez — they **threatened to kill him**. Death threats issued by state security agents in the context of an individual's arrest for opposition organizing are direct evidence that the persecutor views the victim's political activity as the reason for targeting him. The threats were not demands for money, not criminal accusations, and not mistaken-identity incidents; they were politically inflected warnings issued to an individual the GNB knew to be an opposition organizer. This satisfies the *one central reason* standard under § 1158(b)(1)(B)(i) with no ambiguity.

### C. The January 2023 Home Raid as an Act of Political Persecution

The raid on Mr. Méndez's home in January 2023 further cements the nexus. The UN Fact-Finding Mission's December 2025 report specifically documents the GNB's practice of **home raids targeting opposition figures** as one of the systematic modalities of political persecution it attributes to that institution. *See* OHCHR (Dec. 11, 2025), *supra*. Additionally, HRW's April 2025 report, *Punished for Seeking Change*, documents the Venezuelan state's systematic use of **home raids** against opposition leaders and party members as instruments of intimidation and political control. *See* Human Rights Watch, *Punished for Seeking Change: Killings, Enforced Disappearances, and Arbitrary Detention* (Apr. 30, 2025), https://www.hrw.org/report/2025/04/30/punished-seeking-change/killings-enforced-disappearances-and-arbitrary-detention. The January 2023 raid on Mr. Méndez's residence — occurring within weeks of his continued organizing activity and immediately preceding his forced departure — is not an isolated incident. It is an instance of a documented, state-directed practice aimed specifically at opposition organizers. The protected ground is the cause; the raid is the effect.

### D. Imputed Political Opinion as an Independent and Reinforcing Nexus Basis

Even setting aside Mr. Méndez's *actual* political opinion — which is manifest — the nexus requirement is independently satisfied by imputation. The BIA has held that persecution motivated by the persecutor's belief that the victim holds a particular political opinion constitutes persecution "on account of" political opinion, even where the victim's opinion is attributed rather than expressed. *See Matter of S-P-*, 21 I&N Dec. 486, 489–90 (BIA 1996). The GNB's conduct toward Mr. Méndez — sequential detention, physical abuse, death threats, and a home raid — makes plain that agents of the Venezuelan state identified him as a political opponent and treated him accordingly. The U.S. Department of State's *2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela* confirms that Venezuelan security forces engage in precisely this pattern: systematically targeting and punishing individuals whom they perceive to hold anti-government political opinions, with a pervasive culture of **impunity** that permits such targeting to continue unchecked. *See* U.S. Dep't of State, *2023 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices: Venezuela* (2024), https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/venezuela/. Whether the GNB acted because Mr. Méndez actually organized opposition rallies or because it imputed that political role to him — or both — the nexus to his political opinion is established under either theory.
