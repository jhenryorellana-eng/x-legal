## I.5 Chronological Narrative of Persecution

The persecution of Ms. Karelis Andreína Pérez unfolded in a continuous, escalating pattern directed at her by agents of the Venezuelan state and their proxies, spanning from early 2024 through her forced departure from Venezuela in August 2025. Each incident is corroborated by contemporaneous documentary evidence contained in the record.

---

### A. The Triggering Conduct: Investigative Journalism on Public Corruption (2023–2024)

Between 2023 and 2024, Ms. Pérez, then employed as an investigative journalist at *El Nacional*—one of Venezuela's most prominent independent newspapers—published a series of investigative reports documenting the embezzlement of public funds by regional officials affiliated with the ruling party. *Sworn Declaration ¶ 2.* The reports specifically identified irregular government contracts and the systematic diversion of resources earmarked for public hospitals. *Id.* This work, conducted in her professional capacity and as a member of the Sindicato Nacional de Trabajadores de la Prensa (SNTP), directly implicated identifiable government actors and placed Ms. Pérez in the crosshairs of state security services. *Sworn Declaration ¶¶ 1–2.*

---

### B. First Act of Persecution: Anonymous Death Threats (February 18, 2024)

On **February 18, 2024**, Ms. Pérez began receiving anonymous death threats by telephone and text message demanding that she cease her publications. *Sworn Declaration ¶ 3.* The text message received that date—transcribed in full in **Exhibit A**—stated in explicit terms: *"Sabemos quien eres y donde vive tu familia. Deja de publicar mentiras contra la revolución o atente a las consecuencias. Es tu última advertencia."* (Translation: "We know who you are and where your family lives. Stop publishing lies against the revolution or face the consequences. This is your last warning.") *Evidencia A.* The threat was directed not only at Ms. Pérez personally but also at her family members, signaling the involvement of actors with surveillance capabilities and an intent to coerce, intimidate, and silence. *Id.* Ms. Pérez reported the threats to the SNTP on the same day they were received, thereby creating a contemporaneous institutional record. *Sworn Declaration ¶ 3; Evidencia A.*

---

### C. Second Act of Persecution: Armed Raid on Her Home by Presumed SEBIN Agents (September 12, 2024)

At approximately **5:30 a.m. on September 12, 2024**, multiple armed men dressed in civilian clothing and identifying themselves as state security officials—presumed agents of the Bolivarian Intelligence Service (SEBIN)—forcibly entered Ms. Pérez's private residence in Caracas. *Sworn Declaration ¶ 4.* The agents searched her home office, seized her personal computer and work documents, and threatened to "disappear" her if she continued her journalistic activities. *Id.* The raid bore the hallmarks of a targeted, state-directed suppression operation: it occurred at dawn, was carried out by armed individuals invoking state authority, and was specifically directed at the tools and materials of Ms. Pérez's journalistic work.

The physical consequences of the raid were documented the following day. On **September 14, 2024**, Ms. Pérez sought medical attention and received an evaluation recording contusions on her right arm consistent with forcible restraint (described in the medical record as hematomas compatible with "sujeción forzada del antebrazo") and acute anxiety symptoms consistent with post-traumatic stress disorder. *Evidencia C.* A colleague and fellow SNTP member who personally witnessed Ms. Pérez in the immediate aftermath of the raid attested that she bore visible injuries and was in evident fear for her life. *Evidencia E.*

On **September 13, 2024**, Ms. Pérez filed a formal criminal complaint (denuncia) with the Cuerpo de Investigaciones Científicas, Penales y Criminalisticas (CICPC), Sub-delegación Caracas, recorded under Expediente No. K-24-0154-00987. *Evidencia B.* The complaint documented the armed intrusion, the theft of professional equipment and documents, and the death threats made against her and her family. *Id.* The filing of this complaint with the very agencies of the Venezuelan state underscores both the gravity of the attack and the futility of seeking domestic protection, as Ms. Pérez received no protective response from Venezuelan authorities following her report. *Sworn Declaration ¶ 7.*

---

### D. Third Act of Persecution: Warning of an Imminent Arrest Warrant (June 2025)

In **June 2025**, a confidential contact inside Venezuelan security services warned Ms. Pérez that an arrest warrant had been issued against her, charging her with the crimes of *instigación al odio* (incitement to hatred) and *traición a la patria* (treason against the homeland). *Sworn Declaration ¶ 5.* Ms. Pérez's declaration notes, and country condition evidence corroborates (see *Evidencia D*), that these are charges habitually deployed by the Venezuelan government to criminalize the work of journalists and political dissidents. *Id.* The issuance of such a warrant—however pretextual in its legal basis—represented a concrete and imminent threat of arbitrary detention, creating an objectively reasonable and well-founded fear that Ms. Pérez's liberty and physical safety were in immediate jeopardy.

---

### E. Forced Departure from Venezuela (August 2025)

Confronted with an outstanding arrest warrant, a history of violent state intrusion into her home, documented physical harm, and credible threats of forced disappearance, Ms. Pérez fled Venezuela in **August 2025**. *Sworn Declaration ¶ 6.* She entered the United States through Miami International Airport on humanitarian parole and immediately expressed to authorities her fear of persecution upon return to Venezuela. *Id.* She has resided at 8425 NW 53rd Street, Apt. 12, Doral, Florida 33166 since her arrival. *Sworn Declaration, Introductory Paragraph.*

## I.11 Nexus and Application of the Protected Grounds

### A. Legal Standard

To establish eligibility for asylum, an applicant must demonstrate that the persecution she suffered or fears is "on account of" at least one of the five enumerated grounds set forth in the Immigration and Nationality Act § 101(a)(42)(A): race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion. 8 U.S.C. § 1101(a)(42)(A). The nexus requirement demands proof that the protected ground was "at least one central reason" for the persecution. *Barajas-Romero v. Lynch*, 846 F.3d 351, 360 (9th Cir. 2017); *Matter of C-T-L-*, 25 I&N Dec. 341, 348 (BIA 2010). The persecutor's motive need not be singular; it is sufficient that a protected ground be a central motivating factor. Where the evidence of record demonstrates an unmistakable pattern of targeting directly linked to the applicant's journalistic output and the political opinions expressed therein, the nexus element is satisfied as a matter of law.

The record before the Court establishes, with compelling specificity, that the Venezuelan state's campaign against Ms. Pérez is inextricably linked to two independent, overlapping protected grounds: (1) her imputed and expressed **political opinion**, and (2) her **membership in the particular social group** of investigative journalists in Venezuela. Either ground, standing alone, is sufficient to sustain her claim; together, they present a nexus of exceptional clarity.

---

### B. Ground One: Political Opinion (Imputed and Expressed)

#### 1. The Record Directly Establishes the Persecutors' Political Motive

The nexus to political opinion is established not by inference alone but by the persecutors' own words and actions, preserved in the evidentiary record. Critically, the anonymous death-threat message received by Ms. Pérez on February 18, 2024—transcribed verbatim in Exhibit A—explicitly frames her journalistic work in ideological terms: *"Deja de publicar mentiras contra la revolución o atente a las consecuencias."* The reference to "la revolución" is a deliberate invocation of the political framework of the governing Bolivarian movement. The persecutors did not characterize her reporting as defamatory, commercially injurious, or personally offensive; they characterized it as an act of political subversion against a self-defined revolutionary order. This is dispositive. The message constitutes direct, contemporaneous evidence that the threatened harm was motivated by the political message the persecutors attributed to her work. *See Elias-Zacarias v. INS*, 502 U.S. 478, 483 (1992) (the record must contain evidence from which the factfinder could reasonably conclude persecution was on account of a protected ground).

#### 2. Her Investigative Reporting Constitutes the Expression and Imputation of Political Opinion

Ms. Pérez's sworn declaration establishes that between 2023 and 2024 she published a series of investigative reports documenting the embezzlement of public funds by regional officials affiliated with the governing party, specifically identifying irregular contracts and the diversion of resources from public hospitals. *Decl. ¶ 2.* This is not generic commercial reporting. Exposing corruption within a ruling party, in a state where that party controls all branches of government, is itself an act of political expression—a public repudiation of the regime's claims to legitimacy and revolutionary rectitude. Courts have consistently recognized that journalism critically examining government conduct constitutes the expression of a political opinion. *See Canas-Segovia v. INS*, 970 F.2d 599, 601 (9th Cir. 1992); *Sangha v. INS*, 103 F.3d 1482, 1487 (9th Cir. 1997) ("political opinion" encompasses any opinion on matters in which the machinery of state is engaged).

Moreover, even where an applicant's subjective intent is neutral or professional rather than partisan, persecution by the state based on an opinion it attributes to her constitutes persecution on account of *imputed* political opinion—a ground fully cognizable under the INA. *INS v. Elias-Zacarias*, 502 U.S. at 481 n.1; *Grava v. INS*, 205 F.3d 1177, 1181 (9th Cir. 2000). Here, the persecutors themselves supplied the imputation. The arrest warrant—of which Ms. Pérez received warning in June 2025 through a contact within the security apparatus—charged her with *instigación al odio* (incitement to hatred) and *traición a la patria* (treason against the homeland). *Decl. ¶ 5.* These are paradigmatic political charges deployed against journalists, human rights defenders, and dissidents in Venezuela. The choice of these charges, rather than any private-law equivalent, confirms that the Venezuelan state frames her conduct not as lawbreaking but as political betrayal. The charges themselves are the government's formal articulation of the political opinion it imputes to her.

#### 3. The Armed Raid Confirms Politically Motivated State Action

On September 12, 2024, armed agents who identified themselves as state security officers—the declaration specifies a presumed SEBIN affiliation—conducted a warrantless predawn raid on Ms. Pérez's home. *Decl. ¶ 4.* The CICPC complaint (Exhibit B) corroborates that the raid occurred, that the agents seized her computer and work documents, and that they threatened to "disappear" her if she continued her work. *Exh. B, Expediente No. K-24-0154-00987.* The specific targeting of journalistic work product—her computer and professional documents—and the specific conditional threat tied to continued reporting eliminate any hypothesis of random criminality or personal animus. The raid was a targeted operation conducted by political police against a journalist on account of her reporting. The SEBIN is Venezuela's intelligence and political police agency, constitutionally tasked with counterintelligence and the suppression of threats to "national security"—a category the state has systematically expanded to encompass critical journalism and political dissent. The institutional identity of the perpetrators is itself probative of political motive.

#### 4. The Centrality Requirement Is Met

The record does not present a mixed-motive ambiguity. There is no competing hypothesis—no personal dispute, no private commercial conflict, no familial animus—that could displace the political motive documented in the record. The causal chain is linear and direct: Ms. Pérez published politically charged anticorruption reporting → she received threats explicitly invoking her opposition to "the revolution" → state security agents raided her home and seized her reporting materials → she was warned of arrest on facially political charges. Each link in that chain is supported by a discrete item of evidence. This is precisely the factual pattern that satisfies the "at least one central reason" standard as applied by the Board and the federal courts.
